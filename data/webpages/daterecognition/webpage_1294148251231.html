Random thoughts about the future of technology

Posted by MikeKavis
November 17, 2010

It is late in the year I and I am already seeing predictions for 2011. In addition, many people are making predictions about how cloud computing and social software will change the business and IT world going forward. My friends at Zapthink published an interesting article about how they see enterprise IT changing in the future – read ZapThink on Enterprise IT Trends in The Next Decade.

Well tonight it is my turn to share my thoughts.Â  By the way, I was fairly accurate with my predictions back in 2008 if you are interested.Â  Here is where I see technology going over the next 5 years.

1. Decline of devices

This one is misleading.Â  What I mean is a decline in the number of devices we are willing to buy.Â  Most people will settle on one or two devices: a smart phone and/or a portable Internet device (like today’s iPad).Â  I see devices like the Kindle, mp3 players, big laptops, portable game players, and the like all becoming obsolete as consumers expect every application to be delivered over the Internet as a service without the need to purchase any kind of device to run it. The days of single purpose devices are coming to an end.Â  If it won’t run on the phone or on a lightweight iPad-like device, it will not be widely accepted.

2. Super Smart Phones will replace your wallet

Say good bye to loyalty cards, credit cards, club cards, and cards in general.Â  In fact, say good bye to paper money.Â  In fact, in countries like Japan, this is already reality.Â  Improvements in mobile reception, WiFi everywhere, and acceptance in mobile payments will make all of this possible.Â  There are a lot of companies working on these solutions.Â  The best solutions will be gobbled up by the big carriers and be delivered as apps on new phones.Â  It’s already happening folks.

3. Point of Sale systems will move to the Cloud

If you have never worked in the grocery industry, you probably have no idea how proprietary and old these POS systems are.Â  In addition, they are extremely expensive to upgrade and maintain and usually require a third party to manage it for the retailer.Â  The POS vendors have a lot of clout and there are very few of them.Â  They are very limited in modern functionality but they work like a champ.Â  Microsoft is one vendor that is already working on POS in the cloud.Â  Cloud solutions for POS technology will be a no brainer for mom and pop businesses who don’t want to manage and maintain hardware.Â  The same goes for businesses like restaurants, movie theaters, doctor’s offices and the like.Â  This movement will force the big POS vendors to start moving to SaaS solutions as well.Â  Once that happens, the grocery industry will move to the cloud with their favorite vendor, but it will be the last industry to do so.Â  It will take the independent grocers to start the movement.Â  Once the big retailers see the independents cutting their POS costs substantially and reflecting those costs in the price of groceries, the big guys will have no choice but to join the party.

4. Many IT professionals will leave the IT workforce forever

As more companies shift to the cloud, roles and skillsets will change.Â  Many people will not adjust and will no longer be marketable in IT.Â  For example, as database and system administration tasks become more automated, those admins who don’t adapt will have zero value.Â  I have already heard the grumblings from some of these admins as my team tells them how quickly we can have a new server up (5 minutes) and how easy it is for us to manage virtual machines through simple web base interfaces.Â  Instead of grumbling, these guys should embrace these changes and become cloud administration and security gurus.Â  Unfortunately, not everyone is up to the task.Â  But it is not just the admins.Â  Architects, developers, and testers should beware as well.Â  Architects and developers will need to build systems that can easily “plug-in” to other enterprises (can you say SOA?).Â  It will all be about integration.Â Â  Testers not only have to keep up with the learning curve but they are in danger of being replaced in large numbers by crowd sourcing testing companies like UTest.Â  You just can’t compete against the price point of some of these testing companies.Â  Companies like SOASTA will gobble up much of the performance testing work.Â  It just makes so much financial sense to use these services.

I am not saying that there will be a huge reduction in IT jobs, although there will be some reduction.Â  What I am saying is that many enterprises will outsource much of their IT services to service providers.Â  Those service providers will have the lion share of jobs and a large pool to choose from.Â  You better be prepared!

5. Cloud Computing will move past hype to reality

Five years from now the term cloud computing will be as commonly accepted as the term client server.Â Â  Almost all modern software will be delivered as a service in a browser and accessible by your mobile phone.Â  Improvements in security services, adoption by major companies and governments, and the maturity of the cloud vendors’ services will make cloud computing the norm.Â  Companies not in the cloud will die off (with few exceptions).Â  They simply won’t be able to compete at a price point and keep up with the rate of change of their competitors.

6. Architects will still be arguing about the value and definition of enterprise architecture

There will still be a large body of self proclaimed architects who continue to argue about heavy frameworks and methodologies and semantics while the rest of us have learned to deploy changes weekly to keep up with consumer’s expectations.Â  People who adapt light EA principles will survive, the heavy guys will perish.Â  With everything being web enabled, cloud enabled, and consumer enabled, one of the the biggest differentiators will be speed to market.Â  Those battles are won on the battle field not in the ivory towers.

Summary

The next 5 years will bring change at a rate we have never seen before.Â  I hope in 2015 I get a chance to reflect on this post and score myself.Â  So these are my predictions.Â  Am I crazy?Â  Am I smoking something?Â  Am I on to something?Â  I would love to hear your thoughts.

Cloud Computing Case Study: M-Dot’s high speed transaction network

To some pundits cloud computing is just the next buzz word, the next marketing hype, the next fad, and nothing new.Â  For every pundit, there is an innovator out there taking advantage of on demand resources at a price point that can’t be matched, with fewer resources and quicker to market.Â  In this post I will show how my company used cloud computing to build an impressive high speed transaction network on a bootstrapped budget and went from unknowns to leaders in the market in just 2 years.

The Problem:
Marketers and technologists were building and deploying innovative solutions to get mobile and web content in the hands of consumers, but there was no standard, secure way to execute at the point-of-sale (POS). POS integrated solutions were expensive, invasive, and often required new hardware or required users to have smart phones thus alienating a majority of consumers.

The following presentations explain the problem M-Dot Network set out to solve two years ago to the day.

The solution:

Build a standard and secure high speed network with seamless integration at the POS with no additional hardware.Â  Consumers select digital incentives (coupons, web and mobile ads, digital gift cards, etc.) on the web or their mobile device and simply identify themselves at the POS via their loyalty card or mobile phone and automatically receive discounts without ever printing out anything.

The requirements:

Much like an ATM machine, the system must be highly reliable, scalable, and secure.Â  In addition, the implementation of this high speed network should have a zero footprint (no hardware, no additional communication lines, etc) at the retail stores and headquarters and must respond in subseconds.Â  The system must also be able to achieve high scale and handle large spikes in traffic to handle heavy shopping patterns such as special events, holidays, peak hours/days, new retailers joining the network, etc.Â  The network must be up 24x7x365 and needs to have a robust business continuity and disaster recovery plan.Â  Money is only made when transactions are occurring.Â  Therefore, outage = lost revenue and angry consumers and retailers.

The constraints:

All of this must be done on a bootstrap budget with limited capital and a very small IT team.

The solution:

Build a high speed cloud based network connected to a digital incentive Platform as a Service (PaaS).Â  On this platform, channel partners can build marketing and analytical solutions to execute these campaigns in real time across a large network of POS systems for immediate redemption.Â  M-Dot supplies the “rails” so marketers can focus on the consumers and not integration.

The approach:

Step 1: Build a business architecture

In this post a year ago I talked about how we leveraged the E2AF framework to first define what our business architecture was before we chose what our technology was (Real time transactions coming soon to a cloud near you).Â  We used the E2AF Cheatsheet that I created to answer the important who, what, when, how, where, and with what questions.

Step 2: Gather additional requirements

After processing all of the information in the E2AF cheat sheet, it was clear to us that we needed to build a highly scalable transaction network that required the security and compliance of an ATM machine.Â  So we hired an expert in that field (ZSquad) to define all of the security and regulatory requirements that we would need to adhere to when processing digital incentives and consumer data.Â  The E2AF cheat sheet information also helped us decide that the elasticity, low CAPEX, speed to market, and computing capacity of cloud computing was a no brainer for being our choice of our target technology platform.Â  Since we had high speed performance requirements, we chose IaaS (infrastructure as a service) over PaaS (platform as a service) because we wanted total control over the operating system, application server, database, etc.

Step 3 – Evaluate IaaS vendors

Once we made that decision to use an IaaS provider,Â  we hired another industry expert (David Linthicum) to help us with the IaaS vendor selection process and to review our proposed architecture to point out any risks or issues that we may have missed.Â  Based on these factors: global presence, technical maturity, financial stability/longevity, thought leadership, available tools/services…our choice of Amazon as our IaaS provider was a no brainer.

Step 4 – Prototype

One of the best things about the cloud is that you can fire up instances in five minutes and try things out.Â  My architect, Greg Rapp, who is an expert at high speed transaction processing, was able to try many permutations of virtual machines with different mixes of CPU and memory.Â  He was also able to test different database storage engines like MyIsam and Innodb.Â  He could do all this without the need of buying any hardware, requesting change requests to system administrators or DBAs.Â  It was simply Greg and a self service platform, experimenting, testing, researching over and over until he was able to achieve subsecond response time.Â  I summed this up in SeptemberÂ  2009 in an article called using cloud computing to build better software.

Step 5 – Test scale

Once we built it, I hit the road with my CEO to sell the platform to grocery retailers.Â  Many retailers doubted our ability to take each transaction out of their building, go to the cloud to process all of the business rules, and return to the store in subseconds across their hundreds of stores.Â  No matter how much I talked about our backgrounds where we managed a network of 30K+ stores to deliver paper coupons on printers at the POS, they were just not convinced.Â  I knew we needed to do a major performance test to prove our worth.Â  In the technical assessment that Dave Lintihicum did for us, he recommended SOASTA as a testing as a service vendor.Â  I called up the SOASTA guys and paid for a 10K concurrent user test.Â  We were able to prove that our transaction network could handle 10K concurrent transactions with an average response time of 98ms sustained over 9 hours.Â  At the same time, SOASTA was trying to find a customer that could handle a one million concurrent user test, something that had never been done before.Â  SOASTA graciously offered to fund the effort for us because they believed our architecture could achieve this milestone.Â  Sure enough, our transaction network passed with flying colors as we successfully delivered a million concurrent transactions with no errors over 1 hour sustained with an average time of 108ms (see press release).Â  After that test, we were never doubted again by any retailer.

Step 6: Build the platform

Now that we had built our high speed transaction network and filed our patents, it was time to build our PaaS on top of Amazon.Â  It is important to note that we have never spent a dime on actual hardware/servers or on software.Â  Our entire development environment is open source (LAMP stack).Â  All of our 3rd party tools are SaaS (Basecamp, Unfuddle, Sugar CRM, Google Apps, etc.).Â  For more on the tools go here.Â  We were able to get to this point with a total IT infrastructure spend of about $1K per month with just two developers, no system administrators, no DBAs, no nothing!Â  We brought on a great offshore team to build our B2B and B2C applications on our platform.Â  The key to this effort was our approach to dataservices.Â  Architect Jack Hickman implemented our DJango framework and built a suite of RESTful web services so that our offshore team (and channel partners in the future) can simply connect to our platform without any knowledge of the underlying database structures and physical location of the database.Â  I wrote an article called reaping the benefits of data services that goes into detail on our approach.

Step 7: Planning for production

The next step was to prepare both the real time transaction network and the PaaS for production.Â  This is where the cloud really shines.Â  We architected a fully redundant, self healing, auto-scaling environment thatÂ  replicates all of our data and virtual servers across multiple zones.Â  In essence, we have real time disaster recovery with all of our assets being used all the time.Â  The following article (Taking the disaster out of disaster recovery) does a deeper dive into how the cloud makes business continuity and disaster recovery both feasible and reliable.

Step 8 – Launch

We just officially launched our first chain yesterday (press release).Â Â  I am on cloud 9 right now – pun intended!

Things to think about

This has been a journey, almost 2 years in the making.Â  I think it is important that I point out some things so that my readers can really understand the value of cloud computing.Â  Here are some facts about our solution and path to implementation.

  • We do not own a single server
  • We do not have a datacenter
  • We do not have a system administrator (although we will hire one in the future)
  • We do not have a DBA (although we will hire one in the future)
  • We have a fully redundant production environment across multiple zones
  • Our solution is faster than our competitors who have dedicated servers in the stores

  • Our production environment autoscales to meet the demands of fluctuating traffic
  • We have not spent a penny on software licenses
  • We have built a robust enterprise solution on a bootstrap budget

And most importantly, we are now the market leader in our industry with more retailer contracts than our competition and we did not exist 2 years ago.Â  Although a lot of this can be attributed to the heroic efforts of our people and our leadership, none of this would be possible without the cloud.Â  We would not even be in business if we had to raise the money to do this on premise.Â  How many companies do you know that have built a high speed transaction network capable of delivering 1M concurrent transactions per minute in 1/10 second on $2k/month worth of infrastructure?

Taking the “Disaster” out of Disaster Recovery

Posted by MikeKavis
October 28, 2010

In this post I will show how cloud computing can make modern disaster recovery planning affordable and actually capable of handling a major crisis. But first, let’s go back and look at a typical DR strategy of a non-multi billion dollar company who cannot afford the capital expense of fully redundant data centers.

Pre Cloud DR Scenario

Company XYZ has two data centers and has hired a company to pickup tape backups each day from both locations and store safely in an off-site location a few states away.Â  Company XYZ does not have the capital to have an exact replica of their main data center so they had to carefully pick and choose a few core systems to try to replicate as close as possible.Â  The end result is they have some systems that are fully redundant, but most systems are only replicated in an active-passive mode on lesser hardware.Â  Company XYZ has determined that the less critical systems will have to run in a degraded state because the cost of a fully redundant active-active disaster recovery plan is just too expensive to justify. Company XYZ has a full time resource dedicated to business continuity and disaster recovery.Â  This person has assembled a swat team consisting of both business and IT domain owners and set off to create a large document that defines the company’s strategy in the event of a disaster.Â  They have documented a ton of processes that have to be implemented in the event of a disaster in order to have a prayer of getting the business up and running.Â  There are huge logistical hurdles such as ….

  • How do they get their people to the backup site in the event of a disaster?
  • How fast can they get these passive systems up and active?
  • How do they keep the documentation and the plan up to date as the systems change over time?
  • How can they schedule and practice outages?
  • How do they keep all the active and passive systems current on patches, firmware, parts, etc.

The list goes on.Â  For most companies that don’t have 9 or 10 digit IT budgets, disaster recovery planning is an exercise of mitigating the risks in the event of a disaster as opposed to ensuring that the business can run uninterrupted.Â  After all, the feasibility of having fully redundant and active data centers compared to the odds of a disaster make it hard to justify, at least that is what we thought before 911 and Katrina!Â  Now we know better ;-} To sum it up, many DR plans are constrained by budget and simply try to mitigate risks based on the amount of capital available to them (or the amount that IT was able to beg for from the CFO).Â  Most DR plans are a disaster waiting to happen!

Cloud DR Scenario

Company ABC is a 2 year old company who built their solution from scratch in the cloud. Â  They have multiple virtual data centers that are running fully redundant active-active data centers.Â  Company ABC has deployed redundant servers managed by redundant load balancers across multiple virtual data centers which are physically located in different data centers.Â  Company ABC has an active-active DR strategy where all zones run identical hardware and software all the time.Â  In fact, company ABC leverages all of their assets all the time, not just when an outage occurs.

From Cloud Computing

The image above shows how each layer of the architecture is fully redundant across multiple zones.Â  If one zone failed, the other zones would pick up the diverted traffic and automatically scale up more nodes if needed to accommodate the spike in traffic.Â  So let’s see how this approach addresses the issues listed above:

  • How do they get their people to the backup site in the event of a disaster?Â  They don’t need to.Â  These are virtual data centers with virtual machines.Â  Everything can be managed from a browser.Â  The admins only need to get to a place that has Internet access.Â  They don’t need to go to a physical data center.
  • How fast can they get these passive systems up and active?Â  They are already active and running 24×7.
  • How do they keep the documentation and the plan up to date as the systems change over time?Â  They still go through the same process of collecting application and business specific information.Â  The difference is the actual plan and the execution of the DR strategy is much simpler and requires way less logistics.
  • How can they schedule and practice outages?Â  Simply shut down a zone and watch the other zones scale up.
  • How do they keep all the active and passive systems current on patches, firmware, parts, etc.Â  Apply patches to the standard image(s) and snapshot them.Â  Take old image offline, create a new patched image from the standard image and deploy code on the newly patched image.Â  Test and then put new image into the rotation behind the load balancers.

Summary

With the right architecture, your cloud based DR plan can actually can actually provide fully redundant virtual data centers that are active, not passive, and available 24×7 for a fraction of the costs.Â  The need for purchasing or leasing multiple data centers and hiring the staff associated with the management and operations of the data centers are greatly reduced.Â  In other words, DR can be cost effective and feasible in the cloud.Â  At the end of the day, the money you spend can actually prevent a disaster instead of causing one.

While many companies still debate whether it makes sense for them to look to the cloud for their application and data center needs, there should be no debate that the cloud is the place to go for testing. In this post I discuss three examples of how my company (M-Dot Network) has leveraged the cloud for testing in ways that are not even feasible in the on-premise world.

Prototyping/Discovery

Pre Cloud – In most of the previous projects I worked on in other companies, we typically had to purchase our hardware early in the life-cycle due to long procurement cycles and setup time.Â  Usually we had to order this way before we started prototyping which means we had to take our best guess.Â  The consequences would normally be that we would have to tailor our solution around the hardware that we purchased or existing hardware that we were forced to use.Â  The end result was usually a less than optimal performing solution due to hardware constraints.Â  Instant legacy, how nice!

Cloud - The procurement and setup issues go away because resources can be provisioned in minutes in the cloud.Â  This allowed us to get deep into the prototyping phase and then test our design against numerous combinations of CPU, memory, and database subsystems.Â  The result was the complete opposite of the old days.Â  Instead of rigging our design to meet the constraints of hardware, we were able to provision the virtual servers that met our performance requirements based on actual test results.Â Â  That is nirvana!

Performance Testing

Problem:Â  It is very time consuming and expensive to create performance tests that can simulate millions of transactions from multiple touchpoints.

As I have mentioned in numerous posts over the last several months, I am finding that things like process, governance, architecture, SOA, cloud computing, and others are much easier in my new startup world than in my old corporate world that I battled in since the 80′s. Even though I never intend to return to the corporate world I feel obligated to share with my colleagues in the corporate world because I know how hard it can be innovate and promote change in established cultures. In part one on process, I recommended creating a startup atmosphere by building a small team free from the constraints of the corporate setting.

In this post I will focus on roadmaps.Â  Whether you are building a roadmap for you overall architecture, for a portfolio of projects within a given domain of your architecture, or for reengineering business processes, roadmapping can be a challenge because of large amounts of legacy systems and ingrained behavior.Â  Many roadmapping exercises start with a long process of capturing the current state.Â  Often this leads to analysis paralysis and lots of time and money is spent while nobody is building the future state.

WWSD?

What would a startup do?Â  Well, a startup’s current state is that they have a blank sheet of paper and an opportunity to build the best possible solution with no legacy constraints.Â  Hmm, doesn’t that sound attractive?Â  I wrote a post back in 2007 called Getting to Future State where I recommended designing the future state first and then capture the current state later.Â  The reason is simple, if you start with the current state you immediately constrain the innovation process for future state.Â  Why not start with the perfect world and work back instead of starting with an imperfect world and adding to it?

Starting with current state can create undesirable results


Starting with future state can increase your chances for a desirable outcome

WWSD?Â  A good startup would map out what it wants to be when it grows up first and then work towards that goal while carefully managing its precious resources and capital.Â  A startup will also deliver early and often because it has to generate revenue, customer interest, and investor enthusiasm before it goes broke.Â  That is exactly what a corporation should do!Â  Deliver early and often making incremental improvements and proving its value to the executives (corporate equivalent of investors).

I have seen and been involved in too many promising projects where the next new technology, process, or organizational change was going to solve all of the world’s problems.Â  Each time these initiatives fell short of expectations and each time these new solutions were just another layer on top of the last solution.Â  Each layer added a new layer of complexity and legacy on top of the previous layer.Â  The reason for this is these teams started with what existed and figured out how to “wire in” the next technology, instead of figuring out how to move off or abstract parts of the legacy systems in order to take advantage of the newer technologies.

So the next time you have a roadmapping exercise, don’t start by analyzing what exists.Â  Start with a blank sheet of paper and ask “If we were a startup and were starting business today, what would the future state look like?”Â  Once you define what the perfect world looks like, then figure out how to get there.Â  You will likely find that you will have to make some sacrifices here and there but at least your innovative thinking was unconstrained when you envisioned the future state!

What corporate IT should learn from Startups: Part 1 – Process

Posted by MikeKavis
May 1, 2010

I have spent well over 20 years in corporate IT environments, most of it working for IT shops in the 100-300 person range. In every company that I worked for, IT was seen as a bottleneck and IT struggled to satisfy the needs of the business. In many companies, this is the standard. The reasons for these struggles can be boiled down into the following categories:

1) Process – Too much, too little, or the wrong process for the organization
2) Architecture – No focus on EA (“Wild West”), vendor driven, or Ivory Tower Syndrome
3) Culture – Silos, change resistant, IT thought as a cost center, wrong or unmotivated people
4) Priorities – No portfolio type thinking, decisions made at the wrong level, lack of accountability/justification

In the last 18 months, I have worked in a startup with aÂ  team of 10+ employees advisors, partners, and consultants. Many of the above issues are not a problem in our environment for the following reasons:

1) Process – Teams are small, easy to communicate, everybody depends on everyone else
2) Architecture – We see architecture as both a necessity and a competitive advantage
3) Culture – Survival depends on alignment, agility, and change
4) Priorities – Every penny spent better contribute in some way towards a penny earned

Established organizations can usually survive and sometimes thrive even despite their deficiencies in IT because their core business is bringing in sufficient revenue. For startups, I have seen some great business models fail because of the company’s inability to execute. Startups, especially those in early stages, rarely succeed if they are inefficient. So how can larger, established companies create that entrepreneurial environment that brings the same motivation, accountability, and alignment that comes naturally in startups?

What does success look like?

Often, companies like to create a profile of their most successful employees so they can use this profile as a hiring tool for identifying talent that matches the best people in the company. I recommend that any company who recognizes that it needs to make changes to improve delivery and business/IT alignment create a profile for what a successful startup looks like. Notice I did not say what a successful well established company looks like. The reason is simple. Startups typically deliver frequently, with little capital, with just enough features, and with more modern solutions. Isn’t that what established companies really want at the end of the day?

So why do startups tend to move faster and innovate more?

1) Smaller teams
2) Better communication/alignment
3) Smaller budgets = less features and shorter deadlines
4) Employee incentives (survival) are directly tied to results
5) Everybody matters, everybody contributes
6) Not married to legacy systems, processes, cultures
7) Everybody sees the big picture

So how can leaders in IT create this kind of environment that fits the profile of so many successful startups? Transforming an entire organization can be an ominous task. We have seen many companies fail implementing new technologies because of the inability to change the culture. It takes buy in at the highest levels and great transformational leadership to change a company’s culture and motivation. Maybe the solution is simpler.Â  Create a “startup” within your organization and ask yourself, “What would a startup do?”

WWSD?
A good startup would build a business plan that shows investors what it will build, how it will generate revenue, how it will keep its burn rate to a minimum, how it will deliver quickly, and why it is better than the competition. Then the startup would build a small team with its limited funds and create extremely aggressive goals and targets that are back loaded with incentives for employees pending on the outcome of their delivery and future funding. Then the team would be empowered to do whatever it takes to meet those deliverables within the limits of the existing funds and resources.Â  In a startup, it takes a certain mentality for an IT person to survive and thrive in this type of environment.Â  Make sure that only those types of people are included in this internal startup.Â  It only takes one corporate attitude to destroy a startup.

To meet the commitments, team members will have to heavily rely on one another. They will also need to innovate to figure out how to balance features, functionality, and quality. Decisions will need to be made quickly and documentation will have to be just enough. In my startup we call this JEJIT - Just Enough, Just In Time. Our requirements and design documents are more visual than textual. You won’t see any 150 page requirements documents because nobody has the time to create it. Instead you will see an iterative process where we get just enough requirements to throw a prototype together. Then we show the prototype to the product owner and iterate through changes until the requirements are good enough for a demo.Â  The first slide of the following presentation shows how we iterate through each phase.

What I just described is just an example of the process my company put in place.Â  It is agile in nature, but by no means is what purists would call Agile Development.Â  I do not need to enforce any new “by the book” methodology.Â  I did not need to send my team to training to learn how to be scrum masters.Â  I just surrounded myself with a few talented people with the right incentives and we all agreed that this is “how we roll”.Â  If the startup team helps define the process that they will use, they will not resist it.Â  They will own it and be proud of it.
Other options
Creating an internal startup is one option.Â  Another is acquiring, partnering, or investing in a startup that can deliver a specific product or service that the company needs.Â  This is a quicker method but could create all kinds of unhappiness internally when employees see this as a threat to their jobs.Â  Outsourcing is another option which comes with the same type of baggage.Â  I recommend trying the internal startup method.Â  Create a team that is isolated from all other projects, free to create new processes, free to use the right technologies for the job instead of what exists, and let them run with it.Â  If it works, repeat it with another group.Â  Continue doing so until you have created an entrepreneurial environment.
