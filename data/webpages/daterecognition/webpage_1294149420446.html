Grandparent Scam

12/10/10

The Yancey County Sheriff�s Department wants the community to be aware of a scam called the �Grandparent Scam�. Chief Deputy Thomas L. Farmer stated the scam involves an elderly victim being contacted by someone who identifies themselves as one of their grandchildren. The caller is very convincing, often knowing information about the victim�s family, including relative�s names, birthdays and other personal information. The caller often claims to be in Europe or Canada. They explain they are in jail and need money fast to be released or post a bond. They instruct the victim to wire money to them. The victim, thinking they are helping a relative in distress, then wires money to where the caller instructed which is often alleged to be a county jail or detention facility. The result is the loss of thousands of dollars from the victim. The perpetrator may call more than once and may instruct the victim not to tell other relatives about the transaction. Chief Deputy Farmer stated that a recent incident did occur in Yancey County and an elderly grandmother wired money to an address believing that she was sending the money to post bond for her grandson when in fact he was not in jail but rather in class at college as he was supposed to be. Farmer stated that the money was received at a location overseas and was a fraudulent scam. To avoid being a victim, if you get a call, don�t send money without getting in touch with other family members to confirm the whereabouts of the grandchild or other family member. Farmer stated that law enforcement officers are not certain how scammers are getting the phone numbers of grandparents or family information. Local law enforcement agencies have few resources to deal with international crimes therefore warning residents as often as possible is the best way to combat scams.

Increased Deer on Highways

10/08/2010

N.C. Department of Transportation reminds motorists to be aware of the increased presence of deer on state roads during fall months. More than 19 thousand animal-related crashes were reported each of the last three years, and 90 percent of those involved deer. Since 2007, the incidents have resulted in 17 fatalities, over 33-hundred injuries to people and nearly $127 million in property damage. While a crash involving a deer can happen at any time, the majority of deer-vehicle collisions occur between the months of October and December, when deer activity increases due to mating and hunting seasons. Crashes are most common during the hours of 5 p.m. to 7 a.m., when deer movement increases and limited lighting makes it more difficult for motorists to see them on or near roadways. DOT Officials say people need to understand that often a worse crash occurs when a driver swerves to avoid the deer in the roadway. This reaction can cause the driver to hit another car head-on or run off the road. It is better to hit the deer than to lose control of your vehicle and hit a tree or someone else head on.

NC Driver Licenses to Undergo Change

07/02/2010

North Carolina driver licenses will undergo changes by 2012 which will allow the N.C. Division of Motor Vehicles to produce more secure and tamper-resistant licenses and identification cards. The licenses and ID cards will carry a black-and-white photograph taken by a camera with multiple lenses. The resulting photo will be laser engraved into the card, providing a clearly identifiable image. The cards also will be made of a more durable polycarbonate material than is currently used. According to the U.S. Consumer Product and Safety Commission, there are nearly 9,000 emergency room-treated injuries associated with fireworks a year. You can enjoy a safe Fourth of July by following these safety tips:

Never give fireworks to small children, and always follow the instructions on the packaging.

Keep a supply of water close-by as a precaution.

Make sure the person lighting fireworks always wears eye protection.

Light only one firework at a time and never attempt to relight "a dud."

Store fireworks in a cool, dry place away from children and pets.

Never throw or point a firework toward people, animals, vehicles, structures or flammable materials.
