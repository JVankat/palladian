Casting a Vote Against Unnecessary Absentees

In the big scheme of things, it really doesn’t matter how many votes Mathilda Karel Spak received on Oct. 7. As a write-in candidate without name recognition, campaign funding or a support staff, she never posed much of a threat to the top three candidates, who received a combined 7.6 million votes.

Still, it would be interesting to know just how many voters threw their support to the 101-year-old candidate for governor.

Unfortunately, the information is a bit hard to come by. Los Angeles County election officials said Thursday — nine days after the election — that they still are counting the absentee, provisional and write-in ballots that might include some votes for Spak. It takes awhile, a spokeswoman said, because the county has almost 4 million registered voters, and there were a lot of absentee and provisional ballots cast in the recall election.

As of Thursday morning, the county still had 94,000 ballots to process, Secretary of State Kevin Shelley reported. Sacramento County wasn’t doing much better — it had 52,324 ballots left to count even though a mere 615,187 registered voters live here and only 344,992 of those actually voted.

Since there were but four questions on the ballot and all were decided by significant margins, the slowness of the vote-tallying isn’t having any effect on the way California is governed. But in a tight race, such as last year’s race for state controller, the drawn-out process wastes valuable time that a candidate otherwise could use to prepare for taking office.

A major reason for the delay in vote counting is that more and more Californians are casting absentee ballots. The absentee ballot, created to safeguard the voting rights of the disabled, military personnel and others who have legitimate reasons for not making it to the polls, is now being used by just about everybody.

Gone, apparently, are the times when Election Day was a bit of a ceremonial event, when a trip to the polling booth was a special occasion that gave cause for civic pride. Now, voting on Election Day is seen by many as an inconvenience that should be avoided at all costs.

Even Shelley, the state’s top voting official, advocates the use of absentee ballots for simple convenience. In a press release last month, the Democratic officeholder boasted that he had voted with an absentee ballot, and he urged others to do the same.

“I encourage Californians to follow my lead in voting by mail in order to avoid long lines at polling places, and be able to take the time in the comfort of their own homes to read and understand this unique ballot,” Shelley said.

The problem is that opening all those envelopes and verifying the accuracy of absentee ballots takes a lot of extra time. By state law, an absentee ballot cannot be counted until the signature of the voter is verified to ensure that the person is eligible to vote.

For those who vote the old-fashioned way, by showing up at their neighborhood polling places with sample ballots in hand and smiles on their faces, eligibility information is verified in person, usually by a friendly retiree who is volunteering as a poll worker.

It’s probably too much to ask for a return to the more personal way of doing things in this age of telecommuting, voice-mailing, e-mailing and text messaging. It would require a little more individual effort and a lot less sitting on the couch.

So we may be stuck with a vote-counting process that goes slower than necessary. It certainly isn’t the most pressing issue facing California, but it would be nice if Mathilda Spak didn’t have to wait until her 102nd birthday to find out how many of her friends and neighbors gave her the nod on Election Day.
