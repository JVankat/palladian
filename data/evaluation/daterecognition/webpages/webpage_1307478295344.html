July 05, 2007

Gold Cup Numbers Chart Soccer's Creeping Growth

By Tony Edwards

SAN FRANCISCO, CA (July 5, 2007) USSoccerPlayers -- Looking for a growth property in television sports?  Basketball?  Hockey?  How about the Gold Cup?

When the NBA season ended, Commissioner David Stern was asked repeatedly about the Finals’ lowest television ratings for some years.  In the NHL, the Stanley Cup Final audience consisted mostly of Canadian viewers and the Anaheim Ducks’ season-ticket holders.  Baseball’s World Series was the same, with ratings generally decreasing.

But if you’re looking for a competition where ratings are on the rise, how about CONCACAF international soccer and the tournament that the New York Times called the ‘anti World Cup’?

The Gold Cup final between the US and Mexico on Sunday June 24 drew more than 5 million viewers on Univision, and the semi-final on Telefutura the previous Thursday evening between Mexico and Guadeloupe outdrew every other show on television between 10 and 11pm.  Overall on that Thursday night, TeleFutura outdrew NBC, CBS, ABC, the CW, and Telemundo, among men aged 18-34.  In English and Spanish.

Breaking the semi-final down by major markets, Mexico-Guadeloupe was the highest rated show for men 18-34 and 18-49 in Chicago, Dallas, Los Angeles, and San Francisco (which includes Oakland and San Jose).  The broadcast was the top rated show for adults 18-49 in Los Angeles and Chicago.

Admittedly, those aren’t National Football League numbers, but those are almost NBA numbers.  According to Nielsen Media Research, cited by an Associated Press article on June 15, the NBA finals finished with a record-low 6.2 television rating on ABC. The NBA finals averaged 9.3 million viewers this year, down 27% from last year.

So, a heavily promoted network broadcast of a series featuring perhaps the most highly marketed young player in the NBA, LeBron James, in real terms barely outdrew the Gold Cup.  In Spanish.

Before getting too giddy over the numbers, remember that it’s summer television and these games weren’t going up against new episodes of The Office or Scrubs.  Nevertheless, compared with the last Gold Cup tourney in 2005, numbers on TeleFutura were up between 25% and 225% for the entire tournament in Los Angeles, Houston, Chicago, Dallas, and the San Francisco Bay Area. 

What does all this mean?

Well, CONCACAF likes to say that the Gold Cup pays for everything else in the confederation, like women’s world cup qualifiers, youth development programs, and U-20 and U-17 qualifiers and tournaments.  One can imagine Jack Warner and Chuck Blazer making the most of these numbers come TV contract time.  The downside, I guess, is that it gives Jack Warner another lifeline.

Does this mean soccer is more popular than, say, hockey?  On television, with Mexico playing, yes.  Hockey has always been a local sport.  The NHL’s success, in terms of season-ticket sales, in places like San Jose, Anaheim, and some of its more southern locations, while not bolstering its TV ratings, shows that with the proper marketing to the right crowd, teams can consistently do well in the arena.  However, that hasn’t translated into selling more Sidney Crosby jerseys.

It is no shock to say that the most popular soccer team in the US might be the Mexican National Team.  One can only imagine how many tickets could be sold for a game between Manchester United and the Mexican National Team in Los Angeles or Chicago.  Or even between United and Chivas Guadalajara.

A Chivas stripped of its stars recently drew 12,000 people on a Saturday night to Spartan Stadium in San Jose to play a Korean club team.  Twelve thousand people who could have been doing something else, knowing it wasn’t the Chivas first team, paid good money to sit in a 70-year old stadium on bleachers to watch them.

It would be easy to take the Gold Cup ratings and run with them to a place that posits soccer as the sport of the next decade.  We’ve seen that happen too often since 1966, when World Cup numbers were higher than expected and lead to the creation of not one, but two leagues.  However, the bottom line remains that not enough people want to come out, or turn on their TVs, on a Thursday night and watch FC Dallas play Columbus.

On the other hand, the Gold Cup ratings stress again what Soccer United Marketing executives have always known. The soccer audience is out there in the United States, and it’s definitely not a shrinking market.

June 27, 2007

Management Issues

By Tony Edwards

SAN JOSE, CA (June 26, 2007) USSoccerPlayers -- The announcement of a revived San Jose Earthquakes franchise, owned and operated by Oakland Athletics’ general partner Lewis Wolff, is apparently imminent. Any such move will immediately open up the question of not only who would coach the team, but how the soccer part of the organization would be run.

When Wolff took ownership of the A’s from Bay Area developers Ken Hoffman and Steve Schott in April 2005, his first move was to give general manager Billy Beane and president Michael Crowley contract extensions and partial ownership shares in the team. Wolff was not only rewarding them for their success on and off the field, but making it clear they were the people to move the A’s forward.

Beane and Crowley are still with the American League team, and recently had their contracts extended through 2014. Both have been praised as forward-thinking, yet budget-aware leaders fronting an organization that has overachieved on the field.

You could argue, perhaps, that the A’s organization would be the most consistently successful one to move to Major League Soccer from another sport, as judged by field results. The Athletics have had progressive on-field leadership from the likes of former general manager Sandy Alderson, former field manager Tony LaRussa, and now Beane and his group of talented scouts and assistants. Of course, there is a written rule that you can’t mention Beane or the A’s without Michael Lewis’ book Moneyball, which detailed Beane’s organizational philosophy of building a team around undervalued players and types of players.

One aspect of Beane’s philosophy has been to consider the field manager, effectively a middle manager, and ask the question, ‘What multimillion dollar business lets a middle manager run the show?’

Hence, Beane has instituted a top-down philosophy, where he essentially determines where the hidden value, the undervalued players are. When Moneyball was written, the undervalued players were power hitters who could work the pitch count and take a walk along with high school pitchers. Now, the thinking is more of good fielding players and college pitchers, indicating moneyball is an evolving philosophy.

This isn’t the traditional sports management structure, and Beane has taken a lot of heat for it. He inherited Art Howe as field manager, and despite the A’s improving significantly during Howe’s time in the dugout, Beane essentially handed him to the New York Mets. Similarly, Beane fired Howe’s successor, Ken Macha, after he took the A’s to the American League Championship Series, ostensibly because of Macha’s communication, both with the players and with Beane. Then, after a long search, he hired A’s bench coach Bob Geren, who was the best man at Beane’s wedding.

Now, any manager is hired to be fired, so at some point Beane and his best man are doing to have to handle that tension, but for a young team with a ton of injuries, Geren seems to be doing a good job handling the pressure both from the games and from his involved boss.

What could this mean for the Earthquakes organization? We know Wolff is an involved owner who gives his people the resources and space to do their work. A budget is involved.  But this is MLS, where every team has about the same amount to spend on players, as opposed to competing with the money of the New York Yankees and Los Angeles Angels.

Even if the same statistical analysis that gave rise to moneyball didn’t apply to soccer, what about the same organizational philosophy? Would the Quakes have a director of soccer who essentially set the standards for every team that wears pacific blue, from the academy to the MLS franchise to the WMLS franchise they are said to be pursuing? Who chooses which players to draft and trade for? Who essentially sets the tactics and formation?

At that point, the head coach becomes the person who manages the first team as determined by upper management and the game as it happens.  The level of control is drastically different from what’s expected of coaches, much less the established manager model from Britain and, in some instances, the National Football League where the first team coach is also the general manager.

Though we’ve already seen multiple MLS teams tinker with the concept of a technical director with varying degrees of influence, it’s the coach that remains the soccer-specific person.  Discounting those desperate for jobs, what coach with professional experience would fit in to such a system?  Would it be expecting too much in a league like MLS?

June 04, 2007

No Fun For Fans In San Jose

By Ian Plenderleith

It seems that since the Quakes left Spartan Stadium, attending a soccer game at SJSU is no longer much fun, judging by the experiences of former USSoccerPlayers correspondent Tony Edwards, who attended Saturday night’s game there between the US and China, and had a number of observations to offer us by e-mail:

"First, and not SJSU's fault, the two exits on the freeway that you can access Spartan from were both clogged, and there was no sign of traffic cops anywhere to help improve the flow of traffic.

"2. Certain parking lots were open, but certain ones closer to the stadium were not. Of course, saying "parking lot" in this case means, tennis courts, plural intended.

"3. The security guards at the entrances decided that the best way to enforce the "no horns/no drums/no soccer balls" rule at the Stadium was to take the word ‘enforce’ literally. I saw people having their horns and, umm, balls ripped out of their hands. Now, I don't like those horns at all, but that doesn't mean you rip them out of people’s hands.

"4. I knew there were going to be 20,000 people there, but somehow that message never really made it to the guards at the gate, who had 8 small gates open for about 800-1000 people 15 minutes before kickoff. Added to this the fine public relations practiced by the rent-a-guards. We left our line, which wasn't moving, and walked to the other side of the stadium to get in.

"5. There was, I'm not kidding, sand in the rows where you put your feet. Sand. As far as I know, it’s not like there was motocross or something there earlier that day.

"6. The usual Spartan complaints. No video screen, no seats (only bleachers), lousy PA system, etc."

So the USSF managed to attract 20,000 people for a National Team game, then organized the evening so badly that many of them might wonder whether they’d bother coming back. At least if the Quakes return to MLS next year, the last place it looks like they’ll be playing will be at Spartan Stadium.

The determination of the aggrieved fan is not to be underestimated. I never returned to Tottenham Hotspur’s White Hart Lane ground after a senseless run-in with an obstinate steward. Tottenham have survived without my patronage, but US soccer can’t afford to lose a single fan because of heavy-handed security, or because it failed to take a simple measure like opening up an existing parking lot to ease access to the ground.

As for sand under the bleachers, you can only assume that was a nice touch for any kids who might have got bored by the game, though any kid bringing a bucket and spade probably had it ripped out of his hand at the gate.

April 17, 2007

Victory Filling NoCal Soccer Gap

By Tony Edwards

USSoccerPlayers (April 17, 2007) -- It's been the vogue for Major League Soccer clubs to form partnerships with big name sides from abroad in recent years, with teams such as Real Salt Lake and the Colorado Rapids hooking up with Real Madrid and Arsenal, respectively. But a newer trend has seen alliances further down the US soccer family.

In the United Soccer Leagues, a team based in Annapolis, Md., will start out this season bearing the name of English second-tier side Crystal Palace (USA) in Division Two. And one division above them, the newborn, San Francisco-based California Victory will be trying to fill the gap left by the San Jose Earthquakes with the help of a partnership in Spain's La Liga.

A few weeks ago, the Victory took the field for their first-ever game, a scrimmage against the University of California-Berkeley. The team entered wearing the blue and white striped jerseys of its parent club, Spain's Deportivo Alaves.

In a line-up dotted with young Mexican and European players as well as a few former MLS veterans, the San Francisco-based team often looked like an outfit playing its first ever game. Cal caused problems for the professionals, especially down either wing, as the Victory's fullbacks struggled to keep pace.

That this game ended tied at one, and much of the Victory's better play since then, was largely attributable to their number 10, Berkeley product Mike Munoz, who is back in the Bay Area after two years in Los Angeles with Chivas USA. Throughout the game against Cal, Munoz delivered intelligent, space-creating passes.

Playing as an attacking midfielder in coach Glenn Van Straatum's 4-5-1 formation, Munoz was the fulcrum of most of the Victory's attack. He seems to understand when to calm the game down and when to look for the attacking opportunity. Why would a player of such evident skill, even in an exhibition game, choose to play USL1 as opposed to sticking in MLS?

Munoz said it was a combination of conditions that brought him back to the Bay Area.

"When I heard the Bay Area was going to have a professional soccer team, I immediately knew I wanted to further my career [here]," Munoz said in an email interview. "Going to Berkeley for four years and then being in L.A. with Chivas USA for two years made me miss the Bay. I wanted to come back."

Van Straatum pointed to another advantage the Victory has over MLS teams when it comes to attracting players.

"First of all, opportunity," the veteran coach said. "We have an alliance with a European club. Not only will some players from Alaves join us in time for the season, but our players have an opportunity to play in Spain. I tell the players, if you're good, you have a chance to go over there and play"

Munoz echoed his coach when asked about the differences between Chivas USA and the Victory.

"Honestly, there isn't much difference between the two clubs," he maintained. "Chivas USA is also backed by a major international club, and I was able to have a great two years there. However, the Victory is also backed by a big international club, and [the club] is very serious in its goals and its professionalism."

Van Straatum continued that part of the appeal is not only being in San Francisco, but part of a new project.

"It's an intriguing opportunity for all of us," he said. "We want to build partnerships in the community, we want the younger fans to have local heroes, so kids can look at the players and think, I could play for them someday."

With players such as Munoz, and a commitment to reaching out to the community (this game was a benefit for two East Bay youth soccer clubs, a canny move for a young organization), it may be that the Victory can overcome the considerable obstacles any minor league team faces in a large metropolitan area, especially in the Bay Area.

The Bay Area sports landscape includes six major league teams in four sports, professional lacrosse, minor league basketball, and at least two other USL clubs (the Seals and the Frogs are both at the PDL level). When you add in college sports (Cal, Stanford, Santa Clara, St. Mary's, etc) and thriving youth sports in soccer, baseball, and basketball, there is a lot of competition.

Munoz, however, is optimistic.

"There has been a big void since the Earthquakes left," he went on, "and now the Victory can bring back an entertaining, hard-working professional side that the fans can be proud of. As professionals we have a job to win games first and foremost. However, this team has the potential to be more than that in a soccer-hungry Bay Area."

Review: The Italian Job

<p>USNSTPA - Article</p>

The Italian Job
Gianluca Vialli and Gabriele Marcotti
Bantam Press, London, 2006
Available online

By Tony Edwards

USSoccerPlayers (February 26, 2007) -- Gianluca Vialli's career as player and manager in Italy and England embraced two countries with starkly contrasting soccer styles. He was so struck by the distinctions that he has written a readable, thought-provoking, and well-researched book on the soccer culture in both countries.

"The Italian Job" covers everything from fan culture and his life as a player, to his fascinating perspective on the press and the business side of the game. However, it is the book's examination of the two countries' tactical philosophies that ought to be most beneficial to those readers with an interest in the future development of US soccer.

Vialli won four cups as a player and then as player-manager at Chelsea, and before that was a creative and respected striker for Sampdoria, Juventus and the Italian National Team. To write this book, he enlisted the help of Gabrielle Marcotti, the London-based columnist probably best known to US readers for his intelligent commentaries on SI.com.

Instead of a typical "I did this and I did that" autobiography, the authors actually commissioned research and conducted interviews with leading coaches in the game, such as Arsene Wenger, Alex Ferguson, Marcello Lippi and Jose Mourinho. A number of other lesser-known soccer figures share the credit for the book's thoughtful and intelligent tone.

What's the relevance for US Soccer and MLS? Besides a mention of Landon Donovan, Vialli and Marcotti's analysis can be applicable to our youth, professional and National Team development.

In English soccer, 4-4-2 is the standard formation. As Vialli notes, even when Sven-Goran Eriksson wanted to play a diamond midfield, his captain David Beckham told him the players weren't comfortable with that. Eriksson readily agreed, and went back to a formation the players preferred.

Vialli contrasts this with Italian players being able to play well in different formations and with different tactics. The book offers many reasons for this, with Italy's youth training and coaching culture being two of the principal factors. Vialli cites how EPL managers are rarely fired, and hence the formation doesn't often change, while in Serie A and B management isn't afraid to sack a coach. If your old coach played a 4-4-2 last week, and the new guy plays 3-5-2 this, you'd better be able to adapt.

While it might not be best for the US to completely adopt the Italian style of youth and professional training (which is often likened to a job, compared with the joy in their profession expressed by English players), it could be useful to take on Italy's best aspects, which include developing players of high technical skill and tactical flexibility.

It can start with the National Team and MLS, but tactical flexibility needs to begin at a much lower level. Asking a 22-year-old to adapt quickly to an entirely different tactical system is difficult, at best, and as any coach needs results to keep his or her job, it's understandable why many aren't willing to risk innovation.

However, coaches have to be willing to change the culture and prevent US soccer from becoming too much like England's in the long term. With MLS instituting youth development systems (and a new women's professional league should do the same thing), a coach with long term security and resources -- Bruce Arena at New York or Frank Yallop at Los Angeles -- should institute a "no 4-4-2" policy in their youth development systems.

The coaches hired for the youth and reserve teams could deliberately emphasize technical ability and tactical flexibility -- that is, Galaxy reserves could play 4-3-3 one week, then 3-4-3 the next. Soccer would be less about imposing the coach's will on the game, and more about building towards success on a higher level.

In the short term, the results might not be good. The youth coaches hired would have to be given contractual security and high enough pay to make it clear that results are not as important as developing players. For players, an education and a life beyond soccer should not be left out. In the longer term, the benefits of smarter, more technical players and coaches seems evident.

In the unique, top-down world of American soccer, it would be nice if such an initiative could be spearheaded by the US National Team coaches and the US Soccer Federation President. As a spur to a discussion of the issue, at least, they could do worse than read Vialli and Marcotti's fine book.

February 05, 2007

A Bay Area Return?

By Tony Edwards

USSoccerPlayers (February 5, 2007) -- Since the San Jose Earthquakes upped and moved to Houston in late 2005, Bay Area Major League Soccer fans have had little to sustain them besides memories, old action clips on YouTube, and watching former Quakes play for the US National Team.

Thinking about the team's entirely preventable move to Texas, and the twice-christened Dynamo's subsequent MLS Cup success, has served to sharpened the pain when, back in Northern California, various supposed champions of Bay Area MLS soccer emerged to raise hopes of a re-birth, only then to disappear again. Some proved to be lacking the financial resources. Some have had time to make a deal, but lacked the desire.

Now, however, Oakland Athletics owner and real estate magnate Lewis Wolff has stepped forward as the Quakes' likeliest redeemer. His organization, Earthquakes Soccer LLC, purchased an exclusive window to bring MLS soccer back to the Bay Area, and opened an office in Wolff's San Jose Fairmount Hotel. Then, last week, news surfaced that Wolff and San Jose State University are close to a deal that would see him pay for a new stadium on the campus of SJSU, to house both Quakes Mark 2 and the college's gridiron team.

Another significant player in the region has been Soccer Silicon Valley, a grouping of fans, players, business leaders and political leaders long involved in the campaign to keep MLS in the Bay Area. It has been a model organization in helping to facilitate both public and behind-the-scenes activities and negotiations.

SSV's Vice President, Jay Hipps, told USSoccerPlayers that his body has no comment on negotiations between Earthquakes Soccer and any other possible partners, but did say that he feels Wolff is different from previous "champions" who have stepped up and then quickly back down.

"As for what's different, the list is long," Hipps said in an email interview. "Lew Wolff has negotiated an option for a new team with MLS, hired a very capable and professional staff, opened new offices for the venture, and is sponsoring (along with SUM and MLS) a friendly between Mexico and Ecuador at the McAfee Coliseum in Oakland at the end of March. It's an exponentially greater level of commitment than we've seen from anyone else."

Such commitment has been absent from past potential investors, and even from those who operated the Quakes. As well intentioned as people like former Clash/Quakes GMs Peter Bridgwater, Lynne Meterperel and John Moore were, they lacked the organizational support and commitment to accomplish their goal: a vibrant, successful, profitable franchise.

Hipps said that having someone as successful and experienced as Wolff step up gives some degree of confirmation to everything SSV, and Bay Area Quake fans, have been saying all along about soccer's huge potential in the region.

"The fact that Lew Wolff sees the same potential in this market that we see, that he believes the way we believe," said Hipps, "really validates everything that SSV has done over that time, from meeting with area politicians to seeking out local investors." Hipps points to the appointment of David Alioto as the top man with the new Earthquakes organization: "Alioto left a great job with the Oakland A's to do this. Why? Because he enjoys soccer."

It should also be noted that Alioto's position with the A's has now been filled, so there is no easy way back for him.

For all the current optimism, there will be no quick fix. Even if a franchise is awarded and starts playing in 2008, fans in one of the largest media markets in the country will have to make do with indoor and semi-pro soccer in 2007. If MLS returns, it may prove difficult to lure back old fans to an expansion team not coached by Dominic Kinnear, and without Brian Ching leading the front line.

Yet, as Hipps concluded, this gap in the area's pro soccer calendar may prove to be a worthwhile sacrifice.

"If the price we have to pay to get them involved as owners, with a new stadium, is going for a couple years without the Earthquakes," he said, "I think in the long run we will have achieved something beyond our best hopes."

January 10, 2007

Q and A with San Francisco Seals owner Tom Simpson

USSoccerPlayers.com (Jan. 10, 2006) -- We all know the old saw about how to make a small fortune in American soccer ... start with a large one. And we know about the difficulties of making it in the United Soccer Leagues, financial and otherwise.

Yet, Tom Simpson is bringing the San Francisco Seals back. After a multiple-year interruption, the franchise returns to San Francisco and the East Bay as a PDL participant, armed with a unique plan for stability.

After seeing how the Earthquakes experiment concluded, it's fair to ask why anyone would be interested in getting into the market. Simpson was kind enough to sit for a long e-mail interview that stretched over a couple of weeks that included why the Seals are coming back and how a PDL team can keep its financial head in the game.

Tony Edwards: Tom, as you are listed as the General Manager and Head Coach, let's start with the obvious question first: What motivated you to bring the Seals back?

Tom Simpson: There were a number of developments that led to the rebirth of Seals soccer in the San Francisco Bay Area. Prior to those developments, I would say that it wasn't an idea I would have entertained.

It started about a year ago when I was asked by some youth groups to help them establish a Y League franchise. They had tried on their own but because of some management issues it looked like the whole thing was going to fall apart. I called the USL on part of the group and renegotiated some conditions for them. One condition required by the USL was that I manage the Y League franchise. I agreed to that condition because I believe the time is right to bring back high functioning youth soccer to San Francisco. The Y League franchise is called San Francisco United Soccer Club.

The USL put another condition on our entrance in the Y League: they wanted me to look into bringing a PDL franchise into the league to compliment the youth program. I didn't immediately agree to this.

Edwards: Why not?

Simpson: I told the USL that I would investigate that possibility. I spend a few months talking to key people at local universities. I was pleasantly surprised to find that the colleges, in general, were more accommodating than in the past. The main reason for this is that the PDL is now a favored summer time training opportunity for their college players.

Edwards: Who else did you speak with?

Simpson: I also talked to a number of youth groups in the area, who pledged support. With that I told the USL that the situation seemed favorable for the consideration of placing a franchise here. If they were to reactivate the Seals franchise I would make a go at it. However, the USL wasn't willing to reactivate without a franchise fee and I told them the deal was off and left the idea.

Within a month of my decision to not go forward, the owner of the Nevada Wonders (Randy Roser) contacted me. He expressed an interest in moving his PDL franchise to San Francisco and didn't want anything other than to retain some ownership. We negotiated a relationship that involved a 60 percent ownership by Randy Roser and a 40 percent ownership by the San Francisco Soccer Development Corporation, the corporate owner of the San Francisco Bay Seals. Randy Roser gave 100 percent management of the franchise to us and we changed the name to the San Francisco Seals.

Edwards: Quite a road just to get this far. But once you started investigating the possibility, what was your motivation?

Simpson: The real motive is this: we are interested in creating a business model for a soccer franchise in the United States that works. The franchise must be self-sustaining and have the capability of growing with 1) the community's willingness to support the game and 2) the ever-increasing numbers of high functioning soccer players.

It is our observation that the entertainment value of soccer in the United States is insufficient to support a franchise at any level, with very few exceptions that have unique features that apply only to the exception. Franchises should stay within the limits of their support, yet owners have consistently violated this principle for two reasons: 1) competitive ambition and 2) unrealistic expectations on part of the community.

We believe the successful model will require a strong youth base (i.e. SFUSC) and will develop alternative sources of revenue that do not rely on the fan base and sponsorship for revenue. American soccer players have grown in stature and numbers in the international market place and European clubs are showing a growing interest in American talent. We see the transfer market as the emerging opportunity for revenue generation. Randy Roser, while the franchise was in Nevada, had some success in the international transfer market. We're going to try to expand what he started. While we are working to grow the pipelines for transfer and discovery fees, we will collect traditional revenues from ticket sales, merchandizing, sponsorships and camps. We are currently seeking investors who believe enough in soccer to try our model.

Edwards: Fascinating answer. Let me follow up. A good 10-plus years ago in his wonderful book, The Grass of Another Country, writer Christopher Merrill interviewed the then-owner of the New Mexico Chiles in May of their first season. The gist of this owner's quote was "I see things in decades and I plan to be around with the Chiles for at least a decade."

They were, of course, out of business by Christmas of that same year, victim to many of the enthusiasms/problems you list below. So this is clearly not a new problem. When is it realistic to think that the revenue model you are promoting might begin to see some positive feedback (not profitability initially)?

Simpson: Here's my best guess (based on our past experience): By our final year (1999), the Seals generated revenues of $300,000 (about 1/3 of the average expenses of an A-League for that year). We spent $360,000 that year.

When we sold the franchise for $100,000 we were able to collect enough to pay off our debts and break even for the year. However, [the buyers] never finished paying us and went bankrupt: hence, I've reclaimed the name.

The PDL expenses are going to be at least $50,000 per year. I'm pretty careful about expenses so we're limiting our budget to $50,000 for this year with a maximum increase of 10 percent per year for the next three years. Other PDL teams are rumored to be running operations for $100K to $250K, but I haven't verified those figures. We will do everything we can to keep our expenses below $100K by the time we reach maturity, which I expect to require up to five years. If we can generate even half of the revenues we generated in our last year of operations in 1999, we will be ahead of the game. My business plan is to be revenue neutral within three years and reach revenues of at least $150,000 within 5 years.

The Seals may not be the one who pulls this off [proves this plan is workable], but someone will. It's inevitable.

Edwards: And how does the Earthquakes situation affect yours [asked before AEG pulled them out of San Jose]?

Simpson: People can argue all they want about why soccer franchises fail. The bottom line is this: it's not worth watching! How do I know? The fans tell us at the turn-style.

Edwards: Can you expand on that?

Simpson: I don't want to come off as blaming the fans or the players in any way, they aren't the problem. But the problem is that it's heartbreaking to hear the truth about what's happening in soccer in the United States. When I say "soccer is not worth watching" I mean it very literally: people will not pay enough to support the game at the level the owners, players and fans would like to see it played i.e. great players, great venues, big time TV, high profile games, etc ...

The bottom line is this: Americans will not pay to watch MLS to the level that MLS can pay their bills. It's a shame, but the fans don't own this shame. It's the shame of the business people who create plans that don't work. It's not the fan's fault that American soccer falls so flat on its face, so often, so convincingly, and so disappointingly, the fault lies with those who have miscalculated what the American fan is willing to support. When I say that "soccer is not worth watching" I'm putting the brutal truth out there with the fewest words needed. If it's too brutal and my words are misinterpreted to mean that I don't believe in the fan who does support soccer, then I have made a major faux paux.

Edwards: How are the Seals going to be different?

Simpson: The Seals are a totally different business model than the Earthquakes. Ours is bottom up, theirs is top down. We are determined to not spend above our means. We will not spend above what the community will support. If we get 250 fans per game and bring in $10,000 for the season, then that income defines our budget. If we can add to that through merchandizing, camps, and through the capturing of player transfer fees and discovery fees, then that income permits an increase to our budget. Our players come from our youth programs, local men's teams, and the local colleges. The Earthquakes players come from wherever they can get them.

In spite of this, I continue to hope that the top-down Earthquake style model does work because what is good for the Earthquakes, in the end, is good for soccer. It's just that, for now, I don't believe in the model, and I worry that one more failure in the model makes it that much tougher for us who are slugging it out at the grass roots level.

Review: Forza Italia

Forza Italia
Paddy Agnew
Ebury Press, London, 2006
Available online

By Tony Edwards

USSoccerPlayers (January 1, 2007) -- Is it a coincidence that many of the best writers on soccer are newspaper columnists? Simon Kuper, Sid Lowe, and Paddy Agnew all fit the bill. Along with Simon Kuper's fine books, Paddy Agnew joins the short list with his work, Forza Italia.

Nothing like beginning a review with a cliché, but Paddy Agnew really is some one you wouldn't mind sitting next to on the train or on the plane.

Want to talk about soccer? He's been covering the game for years. Politics? He's lived in and written about Italy and Italian politics for years. Religion? He's been the Irish Times' Vatican correspondent.

It's also a cliché to say that as a reviewer, particularly in a World Cup year, I've been sent a lot of 'history of the World Cup' books. I'm your man if you have questions about who scored in the first World Cup or the name of the manager who led Italy to success in 1934 and 1938. But, when a book as well reviewed and interesting as Agnew's comes along, it's a pleasure.

Forza Italia is an eminently readable, yet appropriately serious, overview of life, soccer, and politics in Italy. Appropriately serious because Agnew isn't afraid to detail, for instance, the Juventus drug scandal (which is still shocking in the disregard for the players' long-term health, the sheer amount of drugs present in the Juve training center, and the plausible deniability enacted by the club's then-leadership), or to examine Silvio Berlusconi's rise to power on the back of his AC Milan's success. Even these matters, though, have Agnew's deft touch.

Agnew demonstrates the newspaper reporter/columnist's ability to present the information and allow the facts to speak for themselves, without overtly injecting himself into those stories. Another way of saying that might be that Agnew treats his readers like adults.

What about the eminently readable part? As every review of this book has noted, it's not all soccer. Some of the book is dedicated to Agnew's story of his life in Italy, with all the joys and all the difficulties. This is no Peter Mayle making Provence seem like the most perfect place on Earth.

Agnew's approach places soccer in its place in the framework of life in Italy. Agnew weaves his book with that knowledge, not divorcing sport from business from religion from life.

As Forza Italia is an overview, there are aspects that are going to disappoint. Would more on, say, Inter and a little less on Napoli and Maradona have hurt? Maybe, but I still found Agnew's experiences with the Maradona phenomenon and disgrace fascinating. The blind eye everyone turned to Maradona's 'lifestyle and friends' brings no credit to anyone, in the end.

If you're looking for exhaustive review of Serie A, go someplace else. If you want to have a better idea of what soccer and life in Italy are like, start here.

Review: Anatomy Of A Winner

<p>USNSTPA - Article</p>

By Tony Edwards

USSoccerPlayers (Jan. 12, 2006) -- In Patrick Barclay's engaging and well-written study, he mentions how, during the 2004-05 season, a British newspaper ran a "fantasy stock market" game where participants could "purchase" soccer personalities at values determined by the number of column inches devoted to them.

So, who won? David Beckham, right? Becks and Posh, Real Madrid constantly in crisis, no question, right?

Nope.

Frank Lampard then. Great midfielder for Chelsea and England. Dynamic and never injured. Scores goals, creates goals, plays defense.

No.

Thierry Henry? The best player on a team coming off an unbeaten season?

No.

Obviously, the book would be about them if one of those gentlemen had been the winner.

Chelsea manager Jose Mourinho molded Claudio Rainieri's players into a team that literally swept all in its path during the EPL season. And Chelsea shows no sign of let up, with a healthy 13-point lead over Manchester United through 21 matches.

Mourinho's path to Chelsea is quite well known. He was raised in the game by his father, a goalkeeper and a coach, is fluent in many languages, experience as a school teacher, translator and assistant to Sir Bobby Robson and Louis van Gaal; and conqueror of all at FC Porto.

It would be a distinguished resume for anyone, much less someone not yet 45 years old.

And yet, why the fascination? There is no question Mourinho is incredibly intelligent in all dimensions and applications of the word; he's a skilled team builder and tactician; and he's quick with a phrase.

Yet, one could probably easily apply all those descriptions to Arsene Wenger, Guud Hiddinck, Bruce Arena, and Fabio Capello. Yet, it's Mourinho who wins the Coach of the Year awards and it's his tactics -- and grey cashmere coat -- that are copied and commented on.

It's part of the larger cult of the coach, for lack of a better term. And it's not limited to Europe. How many books have Pat Riley and Phil Jackson written? How much does an NFL head coach get for making off-season speeches to business executives? Why is Bill Parcells so fawned over? Is there any who isn't sick of Larry Brown? Not his coaching, but the endless stories that accompany him.

There are many contributing factors in the growth of this cult. Increased media coverage; sport becoming bigger business; sportswriters perhaps finding it easier to identify with a coach as opposed to with players; the general aging of the media, if I'm a 45-year-old sports columnist for a major metropolitan newspaper, do you really connect with some 20-year-old who didn't go to college or with a coach around your age who can speak intelligently about a subject you are probably passionate about also?

Most of us learned at a very early age that we weren't going to have our name chanted by 20,000 people at Spartan Stadium after scoring a game-winning goal. But, we all can play Worldwide Soccer Manager or Hattrick. So how difficult can coaching be, right? You took Luton Town to the Champions League in WWSM, so obviously, you could do it. Except we can't. And of those who can, even fewer of them have consistent, sustained success.

As Barclay's study concludes, Mourinho is much more than the sum of his intelligence, experience, charm, petulant spats, and tactics. Barclay quickly recognizes that you can't pin down Mourinho as this or as that. Rather than trying to do so, Barclay builds his profile with compelling interviews with Mourinho's peers in coaching and his mentors.

It's this combination of interviews, analysis, and research that combines into more than the sum of its parts and makes this such a compelling, readable book.

May 12, 2005

433

SAN JOSE, CA (May 12, 2005) USSoccerPlayers - Long the trademark of the Dutch National Team, the 4-3-3 formation seems to be coming back into vogue as a reaction against the established 4-4-2. During one of the recent Champions League semi-finals, both PSV Eindhoven and AC Milan lined up in this formation. When Chelsea had Arjen Robben and Damien Duff healthy, Jose Mourinho would often use this tactic, with a central striker. Chivas USA is struggling with it, but as success breeds imitation, more teams may well be adapting tactics or personnel to play this formation.

To get a better sense of the advantages, and disadvantages of the 4-3-3, I solicited the opinions of Lawrence Fine, editor of the wonderfully useful youth coaching newsletter Fine Soccer (http://www.finesoccer.com). We corresponded in an email interview about the advantages of the formation and of the evolution of the game's tactics.

There is a general disclaimer that should be applied to any discussion about formations, in that no one should get too focused on it. As Fine said, "Many coaches understand that formations are somewhat overrated as opposed to the importance of maintaining balance and good spacing at all times."

Tony Edwards: What do you make of the recent higher profile of this formation? Is it an innovation or a reaction against the 4-4-2?

Lawrence Fine: Soccer is a constantly evolving sport but sometimes it evolves back to where it once was. Gradually we see coaches going to more players upfront (just like there used to be) with the understanding of the importance of quick transition, field position, and numbers up situations.

TE: What are the advantages versus a 4-4-2 or a 4-5-1?

LF: One of the main advantages of the 4-3-3 is the ability to get consistent width in the attacking part of the field early on. With the 4-4-2, much of the width is provided by the outside midfielders and while there are many advantages to this, it somewhat limits the availability of the width early on.

The 4-3-3 frequently opens up the availability for attacking runs from the outside backs since in many cases, the three midfielders play in somewhat of a triangle more as three center midfielders than one outside midfielder and two outside midfielder. It also allows for an easy transition to a 4-5-1 from a defensive standpoint when necessary.

While the 4-4-2 allows for a lot of creative runs upfront, it's a bit slower for quick transition since you don't have the numbers upfront right away.

TE: Right. And as compared to the 4-5-1?

LF: There are a lot of similarities between the 4-3-3 and the 4-5-1. The 4-3-3 being a bit more offensive minded and easier for the quick transition.

The other advantage of the 4-3-3 is the ability to organize defensively upfront and force the play to one side as opposed to the other formations which are much more difficult to do this because of the lack of numbers upfront. There is no doubt that it's a formation geared toward transition but not just from the back forward but also defending upfront as well.

TE: Does using this line-up necessarily show a commitment to attacking soccer?

LF: I'm not sure it's just a commitment to attacking soccer but I think it's somewhat of a commitment to total soccer

TE: What's necessary to make the 433 work? I'm not talking about for professional scale necessarily, but for a coach of a youth club team. What kind of players are you looking for? What should they focus on in practice?

LF: You need outside backs with the willingness and ability to go forward. Also it's important to have forwards with the willingness and ability to defend. This formation is one that strongly encourages ¿total soccer."

The other thing that is vital is to have midfielders who truly understand balance and shape both offensively and defensively. When playing with three center midfielders they must make sure they balance out at all times. This requires excellent vision and communication.

What MLS can learn from FC Barcelona

SAN JOSE, CA (February 10, 2005) USSoccerPlayers - Well, it's the day after the first game of the Hex, so of course I'm writing about something unrelated to World Cup qualifying.

As my esteemed colleague Kevin McGeehan pointed out, there are many things MLS can learn from clubs such as Real Madrid. But, there is even more to learn from Madrid's eternal rival, FC Barcelona. There are probably more, but here are five key points MLS should consider'

Regionalism works. One of the first items that comes to mind is about Barca IS Catalonia. The fact that the entire team isn't entirely composed of Catalans is secondary to the fact that the club represents Catalonia.

It takes time to build up, but why can't the Revolution represent New England in more than just name? Why can't the Quakes represent Northern California? In real terms, this means MLS should negotiate with the MLS Players' Union to encourage teams to choose local players for the new reserve teams. They should legally not only start youth teams, but also somehow maintain the rights to the players up to a certain age. In a league as young as MLS, local identification needs to be a key marketing point, especially for the youth soccer crowd. Also, we've seen that the NBA strategy of marketing stars only works as long as there are, oddly, marketable stars. Short of David Beckham playing for the Galaxy, MLS can't market stars, they need to market teams and experience.

Brazil and Holland. Having said that about regionalism, it's also okay to look elsewhere for inspiration, specifically to Brazil and Holland. When you think of Barca's most successful players and coaches, there is a long list from those two countries (Cruyff, Neeskens, Koeman, Romario, Ronaldo, Rivaldo, Ronaldinho, Deco, Michaels, Cruyff, van Gaal, Rijkaard, etc). MLS should be a league for American players, but for attacking, creative, technical soccer players, it would be nice to see some more Brazilians and some Dutch players.

Attacking soccer sells. How many times have you heard that Barca 'ground out a 1-0 victory.' Right, never. Attacking soccer is part of the mission statement, if you will, of FC Barcelona.

Sure, MLS fans will say, 'I'd rather win 1-0 than lose 4-3.' Fair enough, but in a regular season that means as little in a league that needs to draw fans. Excitement sells. Look, I admire Jose Mourinho's philosophy as much as anyone, but Chelsea dominates games playing his way. I want to see MLS teams playing thoughtful, attacking, possession soccer. No defensive 4-5-1s with ten men behind the ball, no subbing out the goalscorers right after they score to bring on another defender. One of the genius moves Frank Yallop regularly made was that when the Quakes were ahead, he never brought on a defender to replace an offensive player. He always brought on a midfielder or a forward. Why? Because pressure wins games. So much for playing style, what about image?

Clean shirts. Perhaps the most notable image of Barca is their shirt. What is on their shirt? A badge and a manufacturer logo. Does it say the team name? Is it a billboard for some mobile phone company? No, its clean, and clean shirts look better.

A rivalry helps. Finally and possibly most importantly, you can't talk about Barca without mentioning Real Madrid. Yes, a rivalry helps. Play up Quakes-Galaxy matches more. Make the Brimstone Cup a bigger deal. And rather than giving those fans who are trying to create a Rapids-Real Salt Lake rivalry grief, give them credit for doing something to stir up some passion during a mostly meaningless regular season.

January 06, 2005

Enter The Dragons

SAN JOSE, CA (January 6, 2005) USSoccerPlayers - On a sunny Northern California Saturday, twelve 4, 5, and 6-year-olds gathered around their coach before the start of their last game of the season.

“Who are we?” I shouted.

“The Dragons!” they replied, mostly in unison.

I stole that pregame shout from Jose Mourinho, who asks his players that very question before every match. And while his players shout Chelsea, let’s see Mr. Mourinho deal with runny noses, untied shoes, and players asking if it was time for the goody bags yet. Yes, it can only mean I’ve finished my first season as coach of a U-6 team.

Going into my rookie year as coach, I thought I did a lot of preparation for our practices and for the season. I read books, ran a mini-academy, and talked with other coaches and my assistant. And there is no substitute for any of those things. Yet, with kids this age, I quickly discovered I needed to have some flexibility in my practice plan. Even after matching up players of similar skills (and fortunately my best players were sharers, not ball hogs), it boiled down to, ‘he pushed me’ or ‘coach, my shoe is untied.’ I found out that the main purpose is about helping the kids find the joy in the game.

I was determined to keep it as simple for the kids as possible. Which way are we going? What do you do when the ball goes out of bounds? They began to learn to dibble, to kick with the side of the foot, and to try not to bunch up too much. But mostly, they just wanted to have fun.

As we play 3v3 in this league and don’t keep score, it’s easy to measure the players’ enjoyment and progress. Some of the players are so full of enthusiasm that when they are playing, they can’t help but forget what they’ve learned at practice. Some of the players need gentle reminding from their parents not to pick the flowers. And some of the players really got it all together, from a combination of ability, practice, and their own determination to get better.

At press conferences and in news releases, you often hear Major League Soccer Commissioner Don Garber touting soccer, and MLS in particular, as the Game for the New America. Besides a dislike for unnecessary capital letters in press releases, if you looked at my team, full of Asian Americans, African-Americans, Hispanics, and Caucasians, a glib, feel-good, PR statement does take on some ring of truth. These kids aren’t just here because their parents want them to be here or for the halftime and post game snacks. They’re here to play. If we could have expanded our four 8-minute quarter games to an hour, they would still be asking for more.

Our final game of the season was a wonderful example of the commitment these kids and their parents showed all season. There was a terrible traffic backup on the main freeway leading to the field. Yet, 15 minutes before game time, right on schedule, the entire team was there, shirts tucked in, and doing our warm-up stretches.

It certainly goes beyond cliché to write that I learned more from these kids and their parents than they got from me. The parents didn’t have to work as hard as they did to get their kids to practices and to the games. They didn’t have to organize the party and the snack rotation. But they did it anyway and they were all supportive.

Kids at this age need some space to play soccer and have fun without some adult putting pressure on them. It’s my feeling that it’s easy to overload beginners. I’m not qualified to teach the finer points of any system, but I hope I helped them have fun and want to come back for the spring season. I know I can’t wait for it.

A deep appreciation and critical eye to all aspects of the game. I was walking over to the field to join my team a couple of weeks ago and stopped to watch the two teams that were in the middle of their game. Just the movements of the players and the ball, the gestures, the support between teammates, captured for me what it is I love about the game. Having said that, the business and media aspects of the game fascinate me and I hope to spend some time on those topics. And for someone with no graphic design skill, I'm interested in the visuals of the game. Uniforms, badges, fans, etc.

What's the best game you've ever seen?

Last November's Quakes-Galaxy first round game at Spartan Stadium in the playoffs where the Quakes came back from a 4-0 deficit. So much about that game remains fresh even now. I was privileged to be in the crowd that evening.

What's your back story?

It started with the Cosmos. I remember watching them on cable and becoming totally obsessed with the game. I had a Beckenbauer poster on my wall.

Like Tobias, my high school, in gridiron-mad northeastern Pennsylvania, also didn't have a soccer team while I was there. A bunch of us would get together on the weekends to sneak onto the only local field that was available. I also played on a men's league team, although ‘played’ somehow does not manage to convey how baffling it all was to me. This teenaged American who had only started playing a couple of years ago and had no coaching or skill, to be out on the field, or even on the bench, with this crew of men from Europe, Central America, and South America.

Somehow, my Dad got us tickets for the Cosmos-Aztecs semi-final in 1980, I think it was, at Giants Stadium. I don't remember anything about the two hour drive down or back, but after we parked the car, I was overwhelmed by the smells, languages, colors, and passion, and this was in the parking lot! I couldn't believe this many other people felt the same way I did. The game was a blur, all I remember is the Cosmos won.

After high school, I went off to Villanova (yay Jim Curtin) where to say I played for the JV team my freshman year would be a great overstatement. I was underskilled, undertrained, and desperately out of sorts compared with my teammates, who could do amazing things with the ball. I did have one shot on goal late in a game after coming on as a substitute, of which I remain inordinately proud.

The Atoms were gone, the Fury was gone, the Fever were gone, so I didn't follow the game much after college. I went to journalism school at Temple University in Philadelphia, then got a job. I had the great fortune to meet the woman who has become my wife and we moved out to California a couple of years later. I didn't really start passionately following the game again until the WC qualifiers in 1996/97. And then WC '98 led me right into MLS and the Clash, which meant I spent a lot of time wondering why John Doyle always had to try to cover up for Mauricio Wright. It's an understatement to say that since 2001, the experience has been much more pleasurable, both club and country.

I've also started playing again, with indoors and moving back outdoors in a wonderful, friendly league. Good supportive teams, a great environment, and varied skill levels; everyone contributes and everyone is a part of the team.

Being a good parent, I've taken steps to infect my oldest son (he's 5 and just started playing at the local Y), who now constantly asks to play soccer with me in the backyard and who sits with me and watches games. He already knows the difference between the Earthquakes and the National Team, and he gives me 'tips' before I go out to play on the weekends.
