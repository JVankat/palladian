There is also a note at the end from John Donvan about Ali Kadan Rabaia, a producer for ABC News who died of a heart attack.

Subject  NIGHTLINE; Supply and Demand 

TONIGHT'S SUBJECT: Scarce resources and high demand are always a recipe
for tension, sometimes war. Diamonds, for instance, have caused nothing
but misery in some of the African nations where they're mined. But when
the resource is a human heart and the demand comes from a cross-section of
Americans, including prisoners, you begin to appreciate the ethical
minefield. This is truly life and death decision-making.

----

There are some 4,000 Americans on a national list for a heart transplant. 
About 700 die each year waiting for one. When a California prison inmate
recently received a heart, you can only imagine the torrent of outrage on
radio call-in shows. But the Supreme Court has made it abundantly clear
that the law is with the prisoner. Authorities are mandated to take care
of their inmates, whatever medical care is necessary. And the decision as
to who gets a transplanted heart is a function of medical need, not social
worth.

What's at the center of the outrage is not so much that a prisoner is
getting a heart and an upstanding citizen with no criminal record is not. 
It's simply that prisoners are guaranteed health care in this country, but
there are no universal health services for those who don't live behind
bars.

ABC Correspondent Judy Muller is our guide tonight through the complex
ethical dilemma of heart transplants. Chris Bury will discuss this
emotional issue with professionals who must think about the consequences
of transplant policies or who, indeed, must make the life and death
decisions.

A footnote from last night's broadcast on journalists under fire in war
zones. Shortly after the program aired, we learned again what a dangerous
business it is to be a war correspondent. An Italian photographer was
killed by machine gun fire from an Israeli tank in Ramallah early this
morning. Also in the West Bank, a French photographer was injured by
shrapnel and an Egyptian journalist's car came under fire. Fortunately, he
was wearing a flak jacket and escaped serious injury.

And a final, sad note from NIGHTLINE Correspondent John Donvan about a
friend and colleague, whom we lost last night at age 44. Ali Kadan Rabaia,
a Palestinian-Israeli, who worked as a producer for ABC News for 19 years,
died of a heart attack. He was the first Palestinian to join an
all-Israeli local staff for ABC.
---- 

One of the parts of this job that rarely shows through in what gets on the
air is how much we reporters, particularly we American reporters overseas,
rely on other people to help us understand the world. Ali was one of those
people for me. I will never forget the day we met, 19 years ago. He worked
in a place called the Palestine Press Service, which has been set up to
try to put out a Palestinian account of news at a time when - as hard as
it is to fathom now - their side of the story rarely got told.  I was
younger, much greener, and, when I walked into the PPS office to begin my
reporting career in the Middle East, not taken very seriously by the men
there. I was an American, and they thought we American reporters were
hopelessly pro-Israel, and they more or less sent the message that I
should take a hike. At that point a skinny, curly-haired guy, easily the
youngest person in the building, walked into the room and saw what was
happening. It was Ali. He turned to the others and said, �Hey, this guy
wants to hear us out. Let�s show him some courtesy. Let�s take that
seriously.� And so Ali and I talked. And talked. Over days and weeks. On
long car rides to Gaza. Over many cups of coffee. He made his side of the
story real to me, and human, in all its complexities. That was a gift to
me I can never lose. Thank you, Ali.

John Donvan
Nightline Correspondent
----

Too often the people who make things happen in the field for a news
organization and put themselves in harm's way don't get the credit they
deserve.  It's always unfortunate that it takes a death to trigger the
kind of tributes people should hear when they are alive.  But that is the
way we are.

Wednesday, March 13, 2002

Richard Harris 
Senior Producer
NIGHTLINE Offices
Washington, D.C.

Just 125 miles to your east, there was the case of Columbia, 
Missouri, where there was effectively no over-the-air PBS service 
until earlier this year. PBS affiliate KMOS-TV/6 claimed coverage of 
Columbia. However, there was a little problem: interference in the 
city itself from KCOU/88.1 that made KMOS unwatchable. Even though 
KMOS came first, it was operated until about 1978 as a low-power 
repeater of CBS affiliate KRCG-TV/13 in Jefferson City. I was once 
told by a longtime broadcast engineer that this was intended to keep 
a third commercial VHF out of the Columbia market. It succeeded in a 
way: Columbia didn't get an ABC affiliate until KCBJ-TV/17 came on in 
1971. KRCG eventually wanted to increase power but had to dispose of 
KMOS first and thus donated it to Central Missouri State University. 
KMOS could rarely be seen outside Pettis County up until that time.

CMSU put KMOS on a relatively short stick north of Sedalia, bought a 
used transmitter from an Indianapolis TV station, and fired up the 
thing at full power for a station on channel 6 in 1980. But in the 
meantime, KCOU had come on the air in 1972 or 1973 (I forget which) - 
and effectively "got there first" as far as coverage of Columbia was 
concerned.

So, for those people who think that technical matters don't 
matter...well, they do, dang it, and this case proves it. KCOU's 88.1 
frequency is immediately next to channel 6 in the ol' electromagnetic 
spectrum. It effectively blocked KMOS for a radius of 10 miles or so, 
depending upon propagation conditions. No use complaining...KCOU was 
there first and KMOS hadn't exercised its full coverage rights soon 
enough.

Columbia's cable company couldn't even get a clear signal off the 
air. Twelve years later, it finally solved that problem by yanking 
KCPT from its Kansas City cable relay and substituing KMOS.

Spurred by the mandate to convert to DTV, KMOS built a new, much 
taller tower east of Sedalia, near Syracuse, and it went into 
operation a couple of months ago. I haven't heard how well it's doing 
in Columbia, but between that and a recent technical tweak at KCOU, 
PBS finally should be receivable over the air in a city once promoted 
by its chamber of commerce as "College Town U.S.A."

Before 1980...no PBS at all, anywhere nearby. I remember when 
Columbia got cable service in 1977. I was a journalism and political 
science student at the University of Missouri. The profs were so 
excited that they *finally* could see "Washington Week in Review" 
every week!

               As Letterman Trails Leno, Will More Promos Help?

   By BRIAN LOWRY
   TIMES STAFF WRITER
   March 15 2002

   David Letterman's new contract with CBS requires the network to throw
   extra promotional weight behind his late-night program, bowing to
   Letterman's hope that will help narrow the gap between his show and
   ratings leader "The Tonight Show With Jay Leno."
   Yet after a tumultuous 10 days in which Letterman's possible defection
   to ABC commanded national headlines, late-night ratings this week have
   been virtually unchanged, with Leno beating "Late Show With David
   Letterman" all three nights for which ratings are available--including
   Monday, when Letterman returned from a weeklong vacation and announced
   on the air his intent to stay at CBS.

   Analyzing data from 50 major cities monitored by ratings service
   Nielsen Media Research that account for well over half of U.S.
   households (national estimates for late night won't be available until
   next week), Leno has averaged a 4.6 rating and 12 share this week,
   meaning 4.6% of all homes and 12% of TV sets in use during that hour
   were tuned to "The Tonight Show." Letterman enjoyed a minor spike
   Monday, with a 3.9 rating and 10 share, but dropped the next two
   nights and averaged a 3.6 rating Monday through Wednesday, mirroring
   his average this season.

   Meanwhile, "Nightline" averaged a 4 rating in its half-hour and
   "Politically Incorrect With Bill Maher" averaged a 2.7 rating, both
   deviating only slightly from their season-long averages.
   Those results reinforce the sense that shifts in late-night viewing
   habits tend to be glacial, raising questions about how much impact
   additional promotion is likely to have--especially as long as NBC
   continues to beat CBS from 10:30 to 11 p.m., leading into late local
   newscasts. On Wednesday, for example, a rerun of NBC's "Law & Order"
   more than doubled viewing of "60 Minutes II" in that half-hour.
   The value of promotion may also be mitigated by how long the
   late-night players have been in place, with many people having made
   their viewing choices.

   For the season, Leno is averaging 6 million viewers per night
   nationally, compared with 4.3 million watching Letterman.
   Tune-in for both shows drops after midnight, and each leads ABC's
   combination of "Nightline" and "Politically Incorrect With Bill
   Maher," which would have been displaced had ABC landed Letterman.
   Those shows average 4.6 million and 2.6 million viewers, respectively.
   Letterman's contract calls for CBS to leverage assets from parent
   company Viacom to promote the show, including MTV, VH1 and more than
   180 radio stations across the United States. Viewers can also expect
   to see extensive "Late Show" promotion during CBS' coverage of the
   NCAA basketball tournament.
