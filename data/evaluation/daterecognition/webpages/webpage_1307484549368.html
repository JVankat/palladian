First, grill robo-salmon's producers -- and the FDA

Published: Wednesday, July 07, 2010, 6:23 PM
By The Oregonian Editorial Board The Oregonian
Follow
Tweet
Share close
Google Buzz Digg Stumble Upon Fark
Share Email Print
Engineers in a lab say they've figured out a way to make a salmon grow twice as fast, delivering it to supermarkets in half the time it normally takes and using less resources doing it. Their Massachusetts employer, AquaBounty, has applied to the U.S. Food and Drug Administration to create and sell genetically altered eggs to create such fish.

On first blush, this looks promising.

Instead of taking three years to mature, this Atlantic salmon would be full-size and ready for the grill in roughly 18 months. It is said to be nutritionally identical to nonengineered Atlantic salmon, almost all of it farmed in ocean pens. People around the world presumably would have wider access to salmon, valued for its nutrient profile.

But the proposal is an ethical game-changer. If it goes forward, this fish would become the first genetically modified animal approved for human consumption.

What-ifs run everywhere.

For starters, the FDA would be opening the gate to fundamental alteration of other animals for human consumption -- maybe a good thing, maybe bad. But it goes beyond husbandry or naturally occurring gene mutation.

Pigs are undergoing genetic experimentation to produce lower-phosphorous excrement, a plus for the environment. But can -- or should -- we produce pigs whose bacon is more healthful? How about cholesterol-free shrimp? Or cattle whose DNA is tweaked to repel the brain-wrecking prions of mad cow disease?

At what point would we stop tinkering with the DNA of food animals -- or would we need to stop at all?

The decline of species worldwide looms. A genetically modified Atlantic salmon could in time become a common but corrupt descendant of the once abundant wild Atlantic salmon, today struggling at less than 1 percent of its historic levels and barred from commercial harvest.

Yet lab-adjusted food animals may be the future. And if approving a fast-track salmon isn't a walk onto a slippery slope, it would be a walk onto a slope requiring careful footing.

That's where "changes creep up on us incrementally," says Gregory Kaebnick, a Hastings Center scholar who cautiously speculates that a DNA-modified fish might work if kept in a controlled environment away from other fish.

Truth in advertising is essential. Would genetically modified salmon carry a disclosure label so people could choose -- in some cases demanding the real (farmed) deal instead of a gene-tampered proxy? Now, genetically altered corn and its many corollary products are sold label-free.

The FDA isn't saying. That's because the agency considers an application for a genetically modified food fish just as it would a proposal for a new veterinary drug -- in secret, with public participation limited by the release of the agency's preliminary findings late in the deliberations.

This needs changing. Too much is at stake when altering the food chain. Reviews on AquaBounty's proposal and subsequent pitches like it should be opened up earlier for public and peer consideration. Careful steps on the slope are taken this way, with protocols hard-earned.

Any animals modified for human consumption, meanwhile, must be labeled as such.

An encouraging claim from AquaBounty is that its salmon would be raised well away from the ocean, in tanks or farms inland, and all engineered eggs would create sterile females, making breeding or interbreeding of any kind impossible.

Only the FDA and an informed public, however, can decide whether those assurances, and others, are enough to support serving up a fast-track salmon -- on the market and on the grill.
