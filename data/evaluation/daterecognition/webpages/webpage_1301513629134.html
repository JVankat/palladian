Quirk: Brain Science

Makes Sense of Your

Go & Do

Writer Hannah Holmes will be at RiverRun Bookstore, 20 Congress St., Portsmouth, at 7 p.m., Tuesday, March 8, to talk about her new book: "Quirk: Brain Science Makes Sense of Your Peculiar Personality." Call 431-2100 for details.

Peculiar Personality

Hannah Holmes

Random House

Holmes' style may be relaxed, humorous, and full of charming personal anecdotes, but her substance is cutting-edge science. Author of "The Well-Dressed Ape," this time out Holmes, who lives in Portland, Maine, tackles personality research, specifically the genetic and chemical studies being done with mice, rats and voles.

She flies all over the world meeting the scientists and their subjects and boils it all down to very organized, very entertaining evolutionary hypotheses of why people are the way they are, from Nervous Nellies to skydivers, drug addicts to compulsive organizers, doormats to fighters, and everything in between.

Holmes organizes her book around five personality factors: Neuroticism, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness and Openness. Each section, Neuroticism, for example, opens with a page listing the personality facets being studied (anxiety and depression), the chemical neurotransmitter (Serotonin), the brain regions involved (amygdala, prefrontal cortex) and the "mascots," — the study animals.

She, as it happens, fits into the neurotic personality type, and after a particularly bad bout of sleepless anxiety, she was prescribed a serotonin drug. "The drug that the shrink gave me demonstrated just how biological my personality was. Over a couple of weeks I became a different person. You'd still have recognized me. I just wasn't impatient, brooding, frustrated, worried, and heaving great sighs. I was at ease."

Of course, it's not always that simple. Holmes was less anxious but still had her phobias. And there are lots of anxiety disorders. Brain chemistry is complicated and too much serotonin can be as disruptive as too little. Also, serotonin drugs help only about half the people with any given anxiety disorder. Hence, the mice.

Mice can be depressive too, though people might call it timid in a mouse. Holmes discusses a range of natural personality types and shows the evolutionary advantages of each. In a rich environment, the anxious mouse stays close to home and thrives as does her brood, while the adventurous mouse gets eaten by a fox and her pups starve. The next year, when drought makes food scarce and the timid mouse is too scared to venture out of her comfort zone, she and her pups starve while the bold mouse ventures far enough to feed her family.

"Each lifestyle works adequately. Neither works flawlessly. But because a diversity of personality styles gives the entire species a better chance of surviving nature's challenges, that diversity persists."

Scientists breed mice to accentuate the traits they want to study, like depression, ADHD, aggression and addiction. Lab mice are also used to study other personality traits, like empathy. One experiment found that more sociable animals — those who spent more time greeting and sniffing one another — could learn from others' experiences.

A sociable and an unsociable mouse were put into blind galleries adjacent to a shock chamber. A warning tone sounded and a stranger mouse squeaked as it received a shock. When it was the subjects' turn to enter the shock chamber the sociable mouse froze in fear at the sound of the tone; the nonsocial did not.

It seems harsh, I know, and so do the scientists who conduct these myriad experiments. Many show more empathy than you'd think they could afford for their test subjects and of course, they are all terrified of PETA. One scientist was reluctant to let Holmes into his lab even after she'd brought him the lobsters he asked for.

"I have delivered unto this man ten living animals that I understood he would plunge, still alive, into boiling water. Even confirmed carnivores consider this savage. And still he suspects me. I wonder if there might be a pigeon handy whose neck I could ring."

Holmes' agreeable, empathetic, well-organized personality is perfect for this subject. She is dogged in her pursuit of reluctant scientists, engaging in her portrayals of them and their subjects, and masterful at explaining biological science in logical and lively prose. She also includes an extensive bibliography and uses some of her research to bolster findings or raise questions. Locusts, for instance, are solitary until population density sparks serotonin production, making them terrifyingly social.

Illuminating and provocative, Quirk will make you look at your friends and family in a more understanding light. Their unreasonable fears, thrill-seeking behaviors, excessive neatness — whatever tics you find annoying — are part of their chemical make-up. They can't help it. Of course, neither can you.

This is a book that will appeal to anyone interested in the workings of the brain, except maybe talk therapists, whose work looks increasingly uphill. Not everyone will agree with Holmes' or the scientists' theories (they don't always agree with each other), but most will find her enthusiasm, clarity and wit well worth the journey.

Those interested in the Five Factor personality model can go to Holmes' Web site, hannahholmes.net, and take a fairly lengthy standardized 300-question personality test, which will compare them with others who have taken the test. This was fun, but no surprises. The final report suggests that questions about accuracy are best resolved by showing the report to those who know you best. Now, that could be interesting.

Lynn Harnett, of Kittery, Maine, writes book reviews for Seacoast Sunday. She can be reached at lynnharnett@gmail.com.
