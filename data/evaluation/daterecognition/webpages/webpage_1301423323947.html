Margaret Thatcher's former offices are taken over by Eurocrats

The Westminster building where Margaret Thatcher celebrated with the Conservatives is reopening as a European Commission propaganda headquarters.

Margaret Thatcher waving to well wishers  
By Richard Eden 6:20AM BST 03 Oct 2010

Best known as the building where Margaret Thatcher celebrated her election victories, 32 Smith Square will reopen this week as Europe House.

It will be a propaganda headquarters for the European Commission, which spent £20 million of taxpayers' money on buying it last year and £5 million on making it fit for Brussels bureaucrats.

Flagpoles have been erected so that the European Union's stars can shine over the front of the building, which was gutted and refitted with luxurious office and recreational facilities.

Critics claim that the move amounts to a ratcheting up of EU propaganda. Stephen Booth, of Open Europe, said: "The problem with the representations of the Commission and the European Parliament is that they tend to act more like lobbying groups defending the EU's every policy, rather than as information points or, heaven forbid, actually listening to the legitimate concerns of the majority of British citizens, who feel EU integration has gone too far.

"Taxpayers have every right to ask why they are funding an expensive new headquarters for such an outfit. The Commission and Parliament claim that buying these new premises will save money in the long term, but, looking at the plans, few expenses have been spared."

The funding for Europe House is being split between the institutions, with 60 per cent paid for by the Commission, and 40 per cent by the Parliament.

EU officials settled on 32 Smith Square after they had to abandon buying another building in nearby Tothill Street because the owner stipulated in the purchase deal that the European flag should not be flown there.

The European Parliament and Commission said they needed to move out of the four-storey London offices they rented nearby for £165,000 a year.

A spokesman for the EU Commission said: "The building is going to be a venue for debate about EU issues in London and we hope that people out there who are interested in these issues will work with us."
