Ex-gang members learn to break 'cycle of dysfunction'

October 24, 2010 1:38 PM
By MICHAEL BARAJAS/Valley Morning Star

Luis Vidal recalled sitting in a Brownville jail cell about 2 1/2 years ago, afraid that the night’s events might have landed him in prison for the rest of his life.
Vidal had just been involved in a fierce shootout, something commonplace in the life of drugs, violence and gangs to which he had grown accustomed. The evening also left one of Vidal’s rivals fighting for his life in a hospital with gunshot wounds, he said.
“I realized that when I was locked up in jail and there was a man dying of two gunshots, I knew that if this man died that I would never see the free world again, that I would never see my kids, my family again,” Vidal said, remembering that night.
Vidal explained his progression from that incident to his life now — a former longtime gang member who, since finding religion in a jail cell, strives to reach out to young gang members to pull them away from the culture of drugs and violence that plague some Rio Grande Valley communities.
Vidal, now 27 years old and on probation, speaks with fervor of what pulled him toward gangs at a young age, what got him out of it and what he’s doing now to reach young people entrenched in that dangerous lifestyle.
From the Southmost area, Vidal said he “grew up in an environment of violence and gangs.” Vidal guessed that by the age of 13 he was already dealing and using a number of drugs, running with local gangs committing petty and sometimes serious crimes.
“I would say I was a full-blown addict by the time I was 15. The violence I had in my house, it was a hard-core place where I lived, and that pushed me out to the streets,” he said. “I didn’t want to be home.”
Tommy Ramirez, executive officer of the Juvenile Justice System in Cameron County, said that in combating gangs, the county must fix what he called “the cycle of dysfunction from one generation to the next.” He said the county is now doing that by targeting outreach and services not just to juveniles, but to the entire family, hoping that dealing with the problems of parents will trickle down and benefit the children of troubled families.
“If we can reach out to whole families and start redirecting them, then hopefully we can break that cycle of dysfunction,” Ramirez said.
Vidal, like many other ex-gang members, admitted that tough surroundings and a troubled home life propelled him toward a life in gangs.“Everything these kids see going on in the neighborhood — in the home, in the schools, on the streets — that’s what they’re going to absorb and eventually become,” Vidal said.
Vidal recalled sleepless nights as a child with violence surrounding his home, saying he would frequently wake up to screaming and police sirens down the block.
“I started shooting up heroin through my veins, I started doing crack because I had so much pain inside my heart,” he said.
Then, as he grew older and became more entangled in a local gang, Vidal said he became increasingly violent.
“I wanted to hurt people because I was in so much pain,” he said bluntly.
“Eventually, I became a monster.”
Vidal, though he’s no longer in a gang, still remains closely tied to gang members in his community, focusing much of his energy trying to get them to change, too. He also works as a peer counselor with the Palmer Drug Abuse Program in Brownsville.
An aspiring rapper under the name “X-Triste,” Vidal also goes into local youth centers, high schools and even detention centers telling his story, performing and trying to pull young local teenagers away from gangs.
———
Jose T. Meza, 25, insisted he used to pull in about $2,000 per day as a teenager pushing drugs like marijuana and cocaine with a local street gang in the Alamo area. In the eight years he was involved with gangs, Meza said he grew from a young teenager dealing drugs into a hardened criminal, involved in violent gang-related beatings and even shootings.
“I felt like I went from a little kid to a professional,” he said.
Meza credits a jail-cell Christian conversion as the catalyst that broke him out of a life of gang-related violence and crime.
He also blames people he grew up with for dragging him into that lifestyle in the first place.
“… (They) started teaching me how to make fast money,” he said. “I was used to it; I had been around it my whole life.”
He said some of his childhood influences had been involved with a violent prison and street gang known for widespread narcotics smuggling and for its ties to Mexican drug cartels. Meza and his friends would often work closely with the gang pushing drugs through the upper Valley, he said.
Meza recalled a violent incident in 2006 that eventually made him turn his life around. Meza and some others were guarding a stash house when a rival gang sent them a stark message.
“(The rival gang) sent us a message that we were gonna get robbed, that they were gonna come and shoot us up and steal our drugs, our money and kill our families,” Meza remembered. About 2 a.m., Meza said, three men drove up and began firing at the house. Meza and several others inside, armed to the teeth, began firing back.
After the exchange of gunfire, in which at least one man was injured, Meza and the others went back to his parents’ home, hiding out and watching for the police.
“By 9 a.m. the next morning I was lying down in the middle of the street, hands cuffed behind my back,” Meza said.
Meza, who eventually escaped without injury or criminal charges, said that while sitting in a jail cell that night, he found God and decided to change. He has since built a makeshift church in a trailer behind his parents’ home, that same home where he hid from the police after the shootout four years ago.
The initial decision to leave gangs, Meza said, was difficult because he was still surrounded by people entrenched in that lifestyle. “I remember, when I told one … that I was done with this stuff, he took out a big sack of (cocaine) and a gun, and said to me, ‘Come back,’ ” Meza said. “I’m surrounded by it.”
Meza, also an aspiring rapper under the name “Pistoleros de Christo,” now speaks and performs at high schools, detention centers and state penitentiaries, he said, adding that most recently he was a guest speaker at the Border Prevention Gang Violence Task Force forum in McAllen.

———
Gilbert Torres, 33, sat inside his office at the Darrell B. Hester Juvenile Justice Center in San Benito, tracing the events that turned him from a local gang member into an asset for the Cameron County Juvenile Justice System.
Torres, who spent some 16 years in a San Benito gang, now works as a gang resource coordinator for the county’s juvenile justice system. He goes into various neighborhoods to meet local gang members and tries to direct them toward education, job training, counseling and faith-based outreach programs.
Torres, from San Benito’s La Palma neighborhood, said youthful influences drove him into gangs.
After seeing people around him begin to run with local gangs, Torres said, “I really wanted to be a part of that … and it became almost like a second family, almost like a neighborhood thing.”
Torres said that for roughly six years he was a drug addict and alcoholic involved in burglaries, thefts, robberies and countless fights.
The turning point in Torres’ life, he said, was when a friend, another ex-gang member, invited him to a church service in San Benito. “I remember that the only reason I went was just to make fun of him,” Torres laughed.
Torres said that while leaving a gang was a relief, it was a difficult path, given his years of drug abuse, crime and violence. “It’s hard to admit to gang members that you’re coming out — it gives you a new enemy,” he said. “I lost a lot of friends. It was hard.”
Shortly after leaving his gang, Torres joined Valley International Christian Center in San Benito and started a ministry he called “Firme Street Ministry,” meant to help young teenagers involved in gangs.
Ramirez, chief of the Cameron County Juvenile Justice System, noticed this year the effect and reach Torres was having with those in gang-plagued neighborhoods around the Valley. Ramirez quickly hired him as a gang resource coordinator for the department.
“We’ve tried in the past and we’ve just had a really difficult time connecting to that population,” Ramirez said. “(Torres) is our connection.”
Torres and other ex-gang members play a vital role in helping root out spots of gang activity that have grown in several Valley neighborhoods, Ramirez said. “The whole idea is that we have to find some way to connect to or reach these young gang members, and generally, they’re not exactly receiving us here (in Cameron County Juvenile Justice) with open arms.”
“Basically, these ex-members are the people who have been there, done that, found a way out and are now normal, productive citizens in our community,” Ramirez remarked.
“We’re hoping to use them to help reach the rest of that community.”
