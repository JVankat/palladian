Dingoes threaten pastoralists

Source: Stateline South Australia
Published: Friday, October 23, 2009 7:30 AEDT
Expires: Thursday, January 21, 2010 7:30 AEDT

Pastoralists are warning that their industry faces collapse because of dingoes.

IAN HENSCHKE, PRESENTER: Pastoralists are warning that their industry faces collapse because of dingos. At Mulga View Station in the Northern Flinders, the owners are selling all their sheep saying they can't continue to bear the financial and emotional pain of huge stock losses. Pastoralists have told Stateline their claims about dingos weren't believed at first, but now authorities are acting. Next month, they'll begin a concerted baiting program. Simon Royal reports on whether that might be too little too late.


SIMON ROYAL, REPORTER: It's morning at Mulga View, a 403 square kilometre sheep station in the Flinders Ranges about seven hours drive north of Adelaide.

Bill and Julie Reschke are making an early start, checking on their sheep. The property is still in the clutches of a 10 year-long drought. The Reschkes have had just 25 millimetres of rain, or an inch in the old language, so far this year. Even so they're pretty happy with how this lot are going.

But in recent times, they faced another unwelcome challenge besides drought. Since August last year the Reschkes have lost 600 sheep to dingos.

JULIE RESCHKE, MULGA VIEW STATION: They're cruel.

BILL RESCHKE, MULGA VIEW STATION: Two or three get together and they just sorta chase 'em round and they tear the side out of their mouth or throat and sort of up under the front shoulder and the back legs, or tear the hamstrings out. And the sheep dies anyway because he's been poisoned, so, you know, it's just bloody no good at all.

JULIE RESCHKE: Yeah, it's really heartbreaking watching it, like, seeing sheep like that.

BILL RESCHKE: And lambs, they love just lambs. They go through and kill them as quick as they're sort of being born.

SIMON ROYAL: So Bill Reschke now spends days carefully setting and checking traps. It's how he caught five of the 10 dogs he believes were on his property. He tried baits but found the dogs seemingly treated them and him with contempt.

BILL RESCHKE: Well they'd dig it and they'd either urinate on it or they'd do a shit on it, and then just walk off to the next one, and ... I don't know why, whether they'd smell something in 'em or something. But they just weren't working. We didn't think they were working anyway. So, this is a better way. Just a lot more time in this, that's all, you know.

SIMON ROYAL: And the Reschkes are not alone. Geoff and Dianne Mengerson are from Depot Springs Station. Allan Ireland comes from Narinna Station. They've all driven two hours over rough dirt roads to tell Stateline of their dingo woes

ALLAN IRELAND, NARINNA STATION: We had the one or two and you could get rid of them and then you'd get a couple of months off or something before they came again. This time we had about six most of the time. And if you caught three in three weeks, well, then, within three weeks you got another three come back, so you still had six again.

SIMON ROYAL: Since October last year Allan Ireland has shot 14 dingos. In the same period Geoff Mengersen trapped and shot 20 dogs. It barely made a dent. 2,800 ewes were mated on Depot Springs last year; just 70 lambs survived. Geoff Mengersen says the drought killed some, but dogs got the majority.

GEOFF MENGERSEN, DEPOT SPRINGS STATION: Very frustratin', eh? You get rid of one and two more take his place. It was unreal. Every time I'd do the water run, you'd just go to do a job and you'd run into dog tracks. So then you'd spend the rest of the day bloody looking around for a dog. Well 20 weeks early this year I did nothing but chase dogs and run waters. Didn't do any fencing or road maintenance or anything, just chased dogs.

JULIE RESCHKE: A lot of people didn't believe us. They were, like, going, "No way, it couldn't have that much - it couldn't be that bad." You know, like, "There couldn't be that many." Yeah, we've taken photos of the dingos and what they've done just to prove our point. Just to show people what's happening because, yeah, just to tell people, they just wouldn't believe us.


SIMON ROYAL: And part of that perhaps stems from the fact that Mulga View is 50 kilometres inside the dingo fence, the 5,500 kilometre long structure that protects valuable pastoral country from the dogs. A few animals have always managed to get through from time to time, but the pastoralists say their current troubles go back 10 years ago when the fence was down for three months after being washed away in floods. The dingos that got in then have bred up, because the old doggers, men who had the skills to trap and catch the animals, have either retired or died.

BILL RESCHKE: There's not a lot of people on the stations now. They just haven't got time and they just sorta breeding up and just movin' south. And I just don't know where it'll end.

SIMON ROYAL: Geoff Mengersen was so worried about the lack of action, he wrote to Environment Minister Jay Weatherill, telling him unless something was done, the sheep industry in this part of the state would collapse. Next month, the local Natural Resources Management Board is holding 'Operation Biteback', which will get local landholders to coordinate a day of baiting. And despite their reservations about baits, the pastoralists are backing 'Biteback'.

ALLAN IRELAND: It's good to have everyone involved. They're trying to get every property to come and things and hopefully that'll help.

GEOFF MENGERSEN: This baiting now, like, we got a baiting in the next couple of weeks, and it's very important because you got all these young dogs now and they're a little bit silly and they'll pick up a bait more than an older dog will. And, yeah, so hopefully - and everyone's gotta to do it. It's gotta be a full blanket of everybody.

SIMON ROYAL: But irrespective of whether 'Biteback' works or not, it will come too late for Mulga View.

Despite the affects of the drought, the Reschkes say this is still really good sheep country, but the affects of the dingos have been so severe and so prolonged that the dogs have now done what the drought couldn't: these sheep are being sold. The Reschkes are getting out of the sheep business

It will be the first time in 100 years that the property hasn't had sheep on it. Bill Reschke concedes this isn't the best country for cattle, but at least they're better able to fend off dingos.

JULIE RESCHKE: It will be very sad. It will be.

BILL RESCHKE: In a way it will be, but you don't wanna see 'em dead either, so ...

JULIE RESCHKE: No.You've just gotta be practical and you've gotta be positive and just do what's right for them and for us, I guess. You can't just leave them out there to be killed. It's not fair.

GEOFF MENGERSEN: We're a pretty proud bunch of people, you know. We don't like to walk away from it. We like to bloody hang on until the end. And, yeah, it's in our blood, init? You sorta my wife's family's been here for - like, Deppo for 60 years, and you sorta built the place up and you don't wanna get driven out by dogs.

SIMON ROYAL: And if that happens the pastoralists argue the dingos will move on to landowners who thought themselves immune, safe on the right side of the fence.

BILL RESCHKE: Dogs are goin' south now. There's plenty south, but they're just not as thick as up here.

JULIE RESCHKE: As far south as Orroroo, that I know of. Like, it's just going to be a big problem for everybody soon.

SIMON ROYAL: In July, Stateline reported that farmers almost 800 kilometres south of Mulga View had already got a small taste of dealing with dingoes. Punthari is 80 kilometres east of Adelaide, heading towards the Murray.

ROGER FIEBIG, FARMER: I thought everybody was havin' a lend of me. There wouldn't be a dingo down here. W wouldn't believe it, until we saw it. We're that far away from the dog fence, it's - yes, why would a dingo be this far, but, yeah. And it was just unbelievable to see that it was a dingo here.

SIMON ROYAL: Eventually, Anthony Horstmann managed to shoot the dog, and this week the results from DNA tests conducted on the animals ear came back.

KYM HAEBICH, MURRAY-DARLING NRM BOARD: The results of the DNA testing was predominantly of dingo content. Just at this stage, the dingo or dog was destroyed and there've been no further attacks in the area since this time.

SIMON ROYAL: And maybe that is how things will turn out. The message, though, from the far north is: "Don't count on it".

GEOFF MENGERSEN: You can't just keep spending money chasin' dogs and meanwhile everything else is falling down around you. I think it's gonna happen to a lot of us - you know, it'll get you down.

IAN HENSCHKE: Simon Royal reporting there.
