Damages denied for dad who witnessed son's death

  • e-mail
  • print
By Steven Walters

July 10, 2009

Proof and Hearsay Blog

Madison - Ending a case that had an eight-year journey through all levels of Wisconsin's court system, the state Supreme Court ruled Friday that a father who witnessed the birth and death of one of his twin sons cannot recover damages for emotional trauma.

State law "does not permit bystander claims for negligent infliction of emotional distress arising from medical malpractice of health care providers and their employees," Justice Patience Roggensack said, writing for the five-justice majority. Two justices dissented.

The ruling threw out a $200,000 damage award to Gregory Phelps of Pewaukee, who ran to get help after his wife, Marlene, reported that she felt the baby was in the process of being born while she was at St. Joseph's Hospital in Milwaukee on Nov. 24, 1998.

The baby, Adam, arrived minutes later but soon was pronounced dead. Court papers say the baby was pronounced dead as a result of asphyxia.

A second boy, Kyle, was delivered shortly thereafter and was healthy.

According to court records, Adam's death was caused by the misdiagnosis of a first-year resident physician, Matthew Lindemann, who was working as a contract employee of St. Joseph's.

The Phelps family sued St. Joseph's in Milwaukee County Circuit Court, where a judge first awarded the family $990,000. It included $500,000 to the parents for the wrongful death of the baby, $200,000 to each parent for emotional distress and $45,000 to each of the couple's other two children.

Several times, the Supreme Court and Court of Appeals returned the case to Milwaukee County to resolve other issues. At one point, the $200,000 award to Gregory Phelps was thrown out, and that was the issue decided Friday.

The money awarded Marlene Phelps and the couple's children stood.

In the dissenting opinion, Justice Ann Walsh Bradley said the father should be entitled to damages.

"Phelps witnessed the spontaneous delivery of Adam. He witnessed the injuries and death of his son," Walsh Bradley said.

The case played a role in the April campaign that led to the re-election of Chief Justice Shirley Abrahamson.

Abrahamson's opponent, Jefferson County Circuit Judge Randy Koschnick, said Abrahamson had taken campaign contributions from Phelps' family lawyers.
