Growing deer population brings an abundance of issues for something that won't go away

Comments 0

July 13, 2009 2:19 PM
Glenn Ayers

It’s hard for any writer, particularly one who must represent various viewpoints, to seem consistent in his content. So it’s with this outdoor writer and his positions on the white-tail deer.

Through the years, I’ve written many outdoor column ledes about the overpopulation of deer, here and elsewhere. Two years ago, I wrote a front-page story on the subject, and last year, an editorial. All of these dealt with the danger of the burgeoning herds to ordinary citizens through personal injury and property damage. 
    

On the other hand, there are my continued warnings about the terrible prospect of Chronic Wasting Disease that to a rational, non-political neophyte might seem the perfect solution. Furthermore, I often rail against poaching, support trophy buck production schemes and warn citizens not to harm or disturb baby fawns. 
    

This journalistic waffling came to mind recently in two situations. First was out at Buttermilk Creek outfitters where I ran into Chris Escherich. I had met him the year before at Lake Mackintosh, where he had run down a wildlife officer to obtain a special permit to destroy the deer who were destroying his tobacco crop. Asked how it turned out, Escherich told me they had effected the transmigration of 39 white-tails. 
    

So, that helped his crop last year, but is there anyone out there who will testify that the problem is solved for this Year? Even for Escherich? 
    

The second incident is my own. I’ve mentioned before how much of planting on my wildlife habitat goes for naught, especially sunflowers that are the best cover and food plant for so many critters and birds. During hunting season we harvested 35 to 40, but this spring it seems two have appeared for every one killed. 
    

Small wonder. This spring, between cutting hay and grain silage, we slaughtered eight fawns, yep — 8. Moreover we spotted two escaping. And all this was done on a plot between 65 and 70 acres. 
    

"So we beat on, boats against the current …" Might as well be protecting cockroaches. 
    

VIRTUAL ARCHERYTOURNAMENT:

The National Archery in the Schools Program in North Carolina recently held a virtual tournament with five teams participating in separate locations across the state. NASP is offered through physical education classes for grades 4-12 in public schools. Students aim at bulls-eye targets in a gym, with special backdrops and safety restraints, in Olympics-style archery.

Adaptable equipment allows boys and girls of varying physiques to compete. Hoggard High School in Hanover County was the overall winner, with Dillard Middle School in Caswell County placing second. NASP teams in the tournament were required to have 16 to 24 students, at least five of whom were the opposite gender.

Each team competed in its own gym, with a wildlife enforcement officer and / or hunter education specialist present to record and verify scores. Connected by the Internet, scores were registered with the Hunter Education Program of the North Carolina Wildlife Resources Commission, headquartered in Raleigh. Trophies and medals were presented to first- and secondplace teams and all competitors received a T-shirt. It was the second such competition of NASP in the state. To learn about the National Archery in the Schools Program, call (919) 707-0031. 
    

AUTOMATIC CHEF GRILL:

Jnb Enterprises LLC announced the first battery-operated rotisserie grill for outdoor cooking, the "Automatic Chef." This portable, adjustable campfire grill features a battery-powered rotisserie mechanism to evenly cook food over an open flame. 
    

When grilling food over an open fire, the heat source isn’t as even and controlled as an indoor grilling appliance. With the Automatic Chef’s batteryoperated rotisserie mechanism, the food is slowly rotated to ensure an evenly cooked meal. Outdoor enthusiasts can cook a home-style meal without constantly tending the food, which leaves more time to enjoy the great outdoors. 
    

The Automatic Chef is ideal for a wide range of people, from permanent campsite dwellers and weekend warrior campers to cabin owners and hunters. Even homeowners who like to spend the summer months outdoors around a fire pit can create a great meal with this product. 
    

It takes less than five minutes to set up the Automatic Chef in the desired location. The base’s anchoring pin is easily inserted into the ground near the fire. To adjust the height, pull up or down on the handle located at the opposite end of the height adjustment cable, which enables the grill to be accurately positioned over the fire. 
    

To access the food after it has been thoroughly cooked, the Automatic Chef can be swiveled to the left or right for safe and easy access. Convenient accessory hooks keep the utensils near the fire making them accessible. The Automatic Chef is available for $149.99. It comes with the grill and the convenient hard plastic carry case that can be easily stored and eliminates the mess associated with outdoor grills. For information or to purchase the Automatic Chef, visit www.automatic-chef.com
