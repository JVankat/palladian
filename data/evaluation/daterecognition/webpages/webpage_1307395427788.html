Serious Horseplay: A Timely Decision?
Written by Mark Thompson   

Judges have urged cutters to keep herd settling within workable time frames before, but there has never been a set rule, and cattle changes sometimes take 40 to 45 minutes or longer during the National Cutting Horse Association’s high-profile limited-age events.

That could change as early as November at cutting’s most prestigious and highest-paying show, the 2008 NCHA Futurity. A proposal by the Judges Rules Committee, following a discussion at June’s annual NCHA Convention in Grapevine, Texas, calls for establishing a 25-minute maximum at the Futurity for calling judges back to the stand.

Members of the Judges Rules Committee estimate that after they receive a call that a herd is ready, it typically takes five to seven minutes for them to take their seats in a judges’ stand. That means, if the NCHA’s Executive Committee accepts the proposed new rule at its late August or early September meeting, herd settlers would have 30 to 32 minutes to complete their task. The proposed rule would require herd settlers to promptly exit the arena once judges are seated – marking the first time a strict limit has ever been enforced.

After the Judges Rules Committee voted unanimously to recommend a time limit at the NCHA Convention in June, the Limited Age Committee and the Professional Trainers Committee both voted to research the issue further during the NCHA Summer Spectacular limited-age cutting event. It started July 14 and concludes Aug. 3 in Fort Worth. Some trainers have voiced strong objections to the NCHA enacting a time limit.

Dennie Dunn, an amateur and non-pro weekend and aged-event cutting horse rider since 1976, a longtime cutting event judge, and the Judges Rules Committee chairman from June 2006 through June 2008, said the committee could revise the maximum limit it recommends. At the same time, Dunn, a winner of $114,329 as a cutting rider from 1976-2007, said the committee does believe the line should be drawn somewhere.

“I think that somewhere in the neighborhood of 25, 30, 32, 35 [minutes], to me, as a showman, that’s plenty of time in the herd,” Dunn said. A time limit would also make life a little easier, and perhaps a little more productive, for cutting event judges, he said.

“If you do 15 bunches a day and you take 40, 45 minutes [to settle each herd], that’s like having 14 halftimes,” Dunn said. Judges are forced to congregate in cramped quarters during cattle changes, and it’s difficult to remain sharp during a long day with many long breaks, he added.

Dunn understands why some horse trainers are worried about a settling time limit, as they want to protect their horse owners’ and their horses’ best interests, while competing for shots at advancing toward big-money payouts. In his opinion, though, a time limit could be established that could work well for the cutters, the horses and the judges.

“We don’t need to rush into it, but certainly, we need to make some kind of an attempt to find a time frame that works for everybody,” Dunn said.

Ronnie Rice, Cedartown, Ga., a two-time National Cutting Horse Association Futurity Champion and a career winner of more than $4 million in his first 31 years as a cutting horse rider, is regarded as one of cutting’s top herd settlers. Many times, Rice will finish settling a herd within 20 to 30 minutes, but he said every herd is different, and he’d hate being tied to a specific time limit.

“I’d hate to be hired to do a job and then they’d run me out of there in 30 minutes, me knowing that I hadn’t got the cattle settled,” Rice said. “Most of the time, you would [get them settled in 30 to 32 minutes], but some sets of cattle, you’re not going to get it done. That bunch, no one advances, just because we didn’t take another 10 to 15 minutes. Sometimes, it will take longer.”

Long cattle-settling sessions extend days for trainers and non-pro and amateur riders, too, but Rice said speeding up the process would hurt a lot more than it would help.

“If you don’t get them settled in those 25 minutes, those first four or five horses, I don’t feel like they’d get a shot,” Rice said. “I have settled cattle in less than 20 minutes before. But with the cattle we have, a lot of cattle we have, you just can’t get it done.”

Matt Gaines, chairman of the Limited Age Event Rules Committee at the June convention, said time spent settling cattle is a legitimate concern, and sometimes, too much time is taken on a herd. But Gaines also said he does not favor a specific time limit.

“If we are taking 40 or 45 minutes each set, then we might need to do something,” Gaines said, but then added he wouldn’t be surprised if the average per-set settling time during the NCHA Summer Spectacular turns out to be more in the 30 to 35-minute range.

Recent modifications to the back fence at Will Rogers Coliseum, which allow cattle in the arena to more readily sense cattle behind them, have helped quite a bit, he said. Some sets still take longer to settle, and he hopes that isn’t prohibited, Gaines said.  He also doesn’t think any maximum limit would work every time.
While there’s a perception more time is taken settling Open herds at limited-age events, Gaines said, “I don’t agree with that.” As much, and sometimes more time, is actually taken settling rough Non-Pro or Amateur herds, the $5 million winner as a cutter said. The difficulty in settling a herd has nothing to do with the division, Gaines said.

Dunn and other Judges Rules Committee members are monitoring time taken during cattle changes at the NCHA Summer Spectacular, too. The committee’s final version of its proposed time limit later this summer could hinge on what it observes at the show.

“We are not going to fly in the face of facts we are trying to establish,” Dunn said. “We picked that [the proposed 25-minute limit] as a point of reference and as a place to start the discussion.”
