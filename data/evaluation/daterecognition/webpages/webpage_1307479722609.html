    Officials accused of looting DPS of $3M

    As district struggled, they partied, indictment says

    BY JENNIFER DIXON AND CHASTITY PRATT DAWSEY

    FREE PRESS STAFF WRITERS
    • Filed Under
    • Local News
    • City of Detroit

    What the indictment alleges

    Among the accusations:

    Stephen Hill, ex-DPS executive risk management chief:

    • Set up a scheme with Detroit businesswoman Sherry Washington's company to create a wellness coaching program for school employees. Associates for Learning received $3.32 million in inflated wire transfers.
    • Received $158,100 in kickbacks from Washington and three partners in her company.
    • Had a $40,000 party in his honor paid for with district funds.

    Christina Polk-Osumah, ex-risk finance and technology manager, and Hill's assistant:

    • Improperly wired funds to school vendors.
    • Used $17,650 for birthday and retirement parties for her mother.
    • Obtained $6,254 worth of jewelry from a company owner who did business with DPS.

    Chastity Pratt Dawsey

    Related Links
  • U.S. Attorney's press release on DPS indictments
  • Federal indictment against 2 DPS officials, 2 vendors

    • By any measure, 2005 was a difficult year for Detroit Public Schools. It had been taken over by the state. Students were fleeing. Thirty schools faced closure. There was a $200-million budget deficit.

    As the schools struggled, prosecutors say Stephen Hill, then DPS chief of risk management, threw himself a $40,000 going-away party. He then embarked on a scheme to send millions more to a firm that performed little work and paid Hill roughly $158,000 in kickbacks, prosecutors contend.

    Hill and his onetime assistant now face federal charges of looting more than $3 million from the cash-starved district, using wire transfers and a shadow computer system to benefit themselves, friends and family.

    The grand jury indictment, unsealed Wednesday, also names Detroit businesswoman Sherry Washington and her sister, Gwendolyn Washington, a doctor. It was their company that received $3.32 million for a wellness program that DPS says did little work.

    The indictment accuses Hill's assistant, Christina Polk-Osumah, of extorting jewelry worth $6,254 from one vendor and using more than $17,650 in school funds to celebrate her mother's birthday and retirement from DPS.

    The government said that after spending $40,000 on Hill's farewell party, Polk-Osumah falsely labeled the expense as "equipment."

    None of the defendants offered comment Wednesday.

    Ex-DPS official had own rules, indictment says

    In summer 2005, the director of risk management at Detroit Public Schools encouraged prominent Detroit businesswoman Sherry Washington to propose a wellness program as an outside contractor, even though the school district already had a similar initiative.

    Stephen Hill then advised Washington and her company, Associates for Learning, to bill the district for $1 million and approved payments of $2 million more for allegedly inflated invoices. Washington, her sister, Dr. Gwendolyn Washington, and two partners split the money four ways and rewarded Hill with a combined $158,100 in kickbacks, according to a federal grand jury indictment unsealed Wednesday.

    The eight-count indictment claims Hill ran the DPS risk department as his own private fiefdom. He handpicked the companies that contracted with his office, based on his own network of friends and professional contacts. He bypassed the school district's checks-and-balances system for paying vendors and diverted millions without bids or written contracts.

    • Related Free Press report: DPS says scam cost $57M

    • Related Free Press report: Hill loses job offer in Cook County, Ill.

    • Related Free Press report: Lavish spending by Associates for Learning

    The indictment accuses the Washingtons and Hill and his assistant at DPS, Christina Polk-Osumah, of crimes involving money laundering, extortion and bribery in 2005 and 2006.

    Hill and Polk-Osumah appeared in federal court Wednesday with chains on their legs and told Magistrate Judge Virginia Morgan they could not afford lawyers. Both were released on $10,000 bond.

    Polk-Osumah's court-appointed lawyer, Patrick Cleary, declined to comment on the charges directly.

    Hill, 58, is to return to court Friday with a court-appointed attorney to complete his arraignment. He told the magistrate that he thought his former attorney would make an appearance Wednesday, but then acknowledged he didn't have the money to pay him because he is unemployed and has no pension or investment income.

    As he stood in court, Hill read the charges against him for the first time.

    The charges are the latest blow to a city already buffeted by public corruption in its schools and at City Hall.

    Prosecutors and the Internal Revenue Service noted Wednesday, without elaborating, that the investigation into Hill's former risk management office is not over.

    U.S. Attorney Barbara McQuade said, "Stealing funds from a public school in these times is particularly egregious, as it threatens to rob our children of their futures."

    The allegations were previously described in a Free Press report in March, which detailed evidence in a civil lawsuit the district had filed against Hill, Polk-Osumah and several vendors.

    The Wayne County Circuit Court suit accuses Hill and Polk-Osumah of using secret offices and computer systems to divert more than $57 million in illicit wire transfers to a dozen vendors who did little or no work. The suit claims the vendors knew the payments were improper but still accepted them.

    The Free Press also revealed that Polk-Osumah gave Hill two cars worth nearly $90,000 at one point, gestures that were not specifically addressed in the indictment. The district claims companies connected to her relatives received some of the diverted school district money.

    Detroit school board members, who have been locked in a power struggle with emergency financial manager Robert Bobb, were quick to claim credit for the criminal charges.

    Board President Otis Mathis said the board "is very proud to have launched the investigation" that led to the indictment. "Criminal wrongdoing will not be tolerated."

    Earlier this month, the Free Press reported that Associates for Learning proposed a pilot wellness program to Hill in 2005, saying it would educate roughly 3,000 school employees on the benefits of a healthy lifestyle for $150,000.

    By 2006, payments to Associates for Learning had ballooned to $3.32 million, and fewer than 150 DPS employees took part in a health assessment survey. Software for the survey cost the district another $1.4 million.

    Bank records obtained by the district show the company made cash withdrawals at casinos and spent thousands on jewelry, pedicures, entertainment, restaurants, shopping sprees and a trip to the Cayman Islands.

    According to the indictment, Hill and Polk-Osumah steered more than $3 million in DPS funds to themselves, friends and family. As the newspaper previously reported, the pair allegedly spent $40,000 on Hill's retirement party from the district, a going-away bash for 200 guests atop a parking deck with Chilean sea bass, petit grilled tenderloin of beef, bananas foster, champagne, Jack Daniels and martinis.

    The indictment said that after Hill asked Sherry Washington to propose a wellness program, Associates for Learning submitted a proposal -- and an invoice for $75,000 in consulting fees -- one day in September 2005.

    The indictment said Hill later advised Washington to bill DPS roughly $1 million at the end of 2005. At the time, he wasn't even working for the district and was employed by an outside risk management and insurance company. The indictment said Polk-Osumah signed the department director's name on a document authorizing payment without his knowledge.

    After Associates for Learning received the money, each of the four partners collected $222,967 as their share. Washington then advised her partners to each pay 5% of their proceeds, about $12,000, to Hill for his role in facilitating the payment, the indictment said.

    In summer 2006, Hill authorized two additional payments for Associates for Learning of just more than $1 million each. The partners, at Washington's request, again gave a portion to Hill, the indictment said.

    At that time, Hill was working for a second large risk management company but was on loan to the district as an unpaid executive.

    The payments to Hill from Sherry Washington and her partners allegedly totaled $158,100. While the indictment names all four partners, only the Washingtons were charged.
