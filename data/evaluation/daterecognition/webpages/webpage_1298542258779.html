Pine Genomics

Carol Loopstra, Ph.D., Principal Investigator

The Pine Genomics Lab is interested in gene expression in forest tree species, especially Pinus taeda L. (loblolly pine). Loblolly pine is the most commercially important tree species in the southern U.S.  It has also become a model system for conifers.

We are interested in understanding which genes influence xylem development and how variation in gene expression may influence wood properties. We are also interested in stress responses, particularly drought. Loblolly pine is an interesting system to use to analyze natural variation in gene expression since most of the original genetic diversity still exists and can be traced back to the source location.

At the same time, there are tree breeding programs that can help with access to germplasm and large numbers of EST sequences are available.  We have recently completed the analysis of ~200 genes in over 400 trees from across the natural range from the Lost Pines of Texas to the Atlantic Coast using quantitative RT-PCR. We observed large amounts of variation between individuals, some of which is related to the region the parent tree originated in. Gene expression was used to cluster genes and to develop regulatory networks. We hope to expand the network analyses to include a much larger number of genes using massively parallel sequencing of cDNAs. SNP genotype information generated by colleagues at UC-Davis was used to identify positive associations between SNP genotype and gene expression. We have cloned the promoters of 15 transcription factors with probable roles in xylem development or stress responses and are currently working on SNP discovery and genotyping within promoters.
