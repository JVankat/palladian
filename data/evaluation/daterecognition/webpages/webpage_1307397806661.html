Hydro rates stir worry as winter approaches

 

The looming transformation of B.C. Hydro electricity rates into a carrot-and-stick billing system is making customers anxious about the winter ahead.

 
By Times Colonist (Victoria) September 4, 2008
 
 

The looming transformation of B.C. Hydro electricity rates into a carrot-and-stick billing system is making customers anxious about the winter ahead.

Hydro will charge a lower price for a basic amount of electricity, and a higher price for everything above the base amount. By April 2009, costs will be 38 per cent higher per kilowatt hour for electricity consumed beyond the base amount, on the premise that Hydro customers will look for ways to save money by using less power.

Documents show, on average, all Hydro customers in single family homes, duplexes and townhomes will be paying the higher rate at least part of the time -- adding up to about $109.50 a year for the owner of a single detached home.

Hydro says 70 per cent of customers across British Columbia will be better off than they would have been under the existing single-rate system which ends this month.

Hydro is offering a wide array of programs to help customers reduce consumption -- everything from free energy saving kits for low-income people, to half-price home energy audits for those with more substantial incomes.

But for the 30 per cent of British Columbians who will see their bills go up -- typically those using electricity for heat and hot water -- there are some anxious times ahead.

Gordon Robertson, who lives in a 140-unit apartment complex at False Creek, says he's worried that tenants "are going to be hammered, and hammered quite badly."

Apartment residents, at least according to Hydro's statistical analyses of electricity consumption patterns across B.C., don't consume enough power to be impacted by the higher rates. But Robertson said the units in his complex need huge amounts of electric heat to stay warm in the winter.

"The hydro bills here are horrendous, and I believe that is true of an awful lot of the construction at False Creek," Robertson said in an interview. "There are probably 4,000 dwelling units down here and most of them were built at the same time, from 1976 to 1980."

Robertson currently pays an average $120 a month for electricity. On an annual basis, his family uses more than double the amount of electricity available at the base price. He guesses that his bill could reach "$200 a month "within a year.

Robertson said many of the other tenants are elderly or disabled on fixed incomes-- and they, too, are facing sudden jumps in months ahead.

Even in West Vancouver, one of Canada's most affluent communities, the new system is causing concern.

West Vancouver resident Jay Lynne Fleming and her family live in a part of the community that is not served by Terasen Gas. Their 3,300 square foot, 28-year-old home has two propane-powered fireplaces, but their primary heating system is a pair of electrical heating units.

"Our Hydro bill can be between $800-$900 over the two month period on a regular basis," Fleming said. "Which means for sure, we are at that consumption level where we are going to get hit -- even at a reasonably good income level, it will be significant."

Hydro is confident that it has enough conservation programs and funding support in place to provide assistance to virtually anyone who is looking to cut electricity use.

The utility's call centre has "specialized, trained people" who can offer support and suggestions for cutting consumption, Hydro spokeswoman Susan Danard said.

The call centre number is 604-224-9376 in the Lower Mainland, and 1-800-224-9376 toll free.
