Guide to Congress

The following is a brief description of the two houses of Congress and what their functions are:

  • House of Representatives

    The lower body of the Congress. House members are elected to represent a geographic district. The US House (with 435 voting members and five nonvoting delegates) is much larger than the Senate (with 100 voting members). The number of representatives each state has is dependent upon the state's population. The House is seen as the more personal branch– US House Representatives have smaller constituencies and are the direct link to the Congress of the United States. The House Members consider bills based on how it will effect the constituents in their districts. To assure that member stay true to this, House Representatives have terms of two years. Essentially, they are always running for election.

    The House's most powerful function is presenting all spending bills or appropriations. The power of the purse starts in the house, but needs to be agreed upon in the Senate for these bills to pass.

  • Senate

    The upper body of the Congress. Each state has two US Senators elected at-large, to serve six-year terms, with one-third of the seats up for re-election every two years. Senators are less inclined to vote on short term passions of a smaller constituency in their state, due to the fact that they are more insulated and hold the seat for six years.

    The Senate's most powerful functions include, advise and consent to the President on Foreign Policy matters such as war and on political appointments such as judges.

Dividing the Congress into two chambers was thought out by the founders with the intent of having checks and balances on the system, requiring a positive vote of both bodies to approve legislation. By designing differences into both the House and the Senate, the founders assured that legislation would be carefully considered in both the short and long-term effects.

Key Differences Between the House and Senate

  House Senate
Powers granted by the constitution
  • Initiates all revenue bills (Appropriations)
  • Initiates (and passes or defeats) articles of impeachment
  • Gives "advice and consent" to treaties and to major presidential appointments
  • Tries impeached officials
Structure
  • 435 members with two-year terms
  • More hierarchically organized
  • Power distributed less evenly
  • Members are highly specialized
  • Emphasizes tax and revenue policy
  • More committees and subcommittees
  • 100 members with six-year terms
  • Less hierarchically organized (less centralized, less formal, weaker leadership)
  • Power distributed more evenly
  • Members are generalists
  • Emphasizes foreign policy
  • Fewer committees and subcommittees
Legislative/Committee Procedures
  • Bills introduced into "the hopper" and referred to committee by the Speaker
  • Speaker may create ad hoc committees
  • Committee action more influential than floor action for final decision
  • Scheduling generally controlled by majority party leadership and Rules Committee
  • Rigid floor debate rules favor majority (debate limits set by Rules Committee)
  • Bills introduced (may be introduced directly on the floor) and normally referred to committee by majority leader
  • No ad hoc committees may be created
  • Floor action as important as committee action for final decision
  • Scheduling generally mutually agreed by majority and minority leaders
  • Flexible floor debate rules protect minority

Source: Adapted from O'Connor, Karen, Larry J. Sabato, Stefan D. Haag, and Gary A. Keith. 2004. American Government: Continuity and Change. Pearson Education Inc. p. 226.; Edwards, David V. and Alessandra Lippucci. 1998. Practicing American Politics: An Introduction to Government. Worth Publishers. p. 417.
