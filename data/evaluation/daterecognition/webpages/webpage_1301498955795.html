BoC holds rate: What the economists say

  • Comments
  • Twitter
  • LinkedIn
  • Email

Reuters · Jan. 18, 2011 | Last Updated: Jan. 18, 2011 10:02 AM ET

The Bank of Canada held its key interest rate steady on Tuesday, as expected, while slightly increasing its growth forecasts for 2011 and 2012 to reflect the strength of the global economic recovery.

Here's what the economists say:

MARK CHANDLER, HEAD OF CANADIAN FIXED INCOME AND CURRENCY STRATEGY AT RBC CAPITAL MARKETS

"It’s a very, very cautious outlook. They cited a number of positive factors in the outlook, but if you look at the net revisions, three tenths this year and next to growth and the fact that they didn’t change the date at which the output gap is expected to be closed, it’s very cautious."

"I think that they have an open mind to change as the data comes in and their forecasts adjust, but at least now they don’t see any real benefit in laying cards on the table in terms of expected rate increases. They’re cautious but we still think there’s three months until the next forecast update and you’ll see a relatively sizable upward revision at that point."

DOUG PORTER, DEPUTY CHIEF ECONOMIST, BMO CAPITAL MARKETS:

"Overall it comes down on the mildly dovish side of what we might have been expecting. There’s nothing really shocking here but I think the overall tone is still quite cautious."

"They’re very mindful of the strength of the Canadian dollar. They certainly don’t want to send the dollar roaring higher."

"Their revision for growth this year is quite slight."

"They’ve laid out in pretty clear terms of where they see the weights are on growth."

DEREK HOLT, ECONOMIST AT SCOTIA CAPITAL

"This reaffirms our view that the bank is on a prolonged pause and reinforces our forecast that the next hike isn’t coming until October."

"I think (the tone) is consistent with the tone of their speeches and the last rate statement, but it’s a bit more dovish than some in the market might have expected."

"I would think it would push off some of the bets in the market that the Bank of Canada is going to hike sooner rather than later."

DAVID TULK, SENIOR MACRO STRATEGIST, TD SECURITIES

"It’s a little bit more optimistic in terms of the global economy. Nevertheless there are still sufficient headwinds that we don’t think will push the bank to begin hiking until July of this year."

"They did note the persistent strength in the Canadian dollar as well as perhaps a little bit more weakness coming from emerging markets as they respond to higher commodity prices."

MARKET REACTION

The Canadian dollar fell from a two-and-a-half year high against the U.S. dollar on Tuesday after the Bank of Canada kept interest rates steady at 1%.
