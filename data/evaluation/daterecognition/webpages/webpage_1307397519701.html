TIA Supports FCC as It Considers Changes to Hearing-Aid Compatibility Rules to Meet Requirements of New Law

Principles in FCC Policy Statement Can be Implemented Consistent With the Requirements of the 21st Century Communications and Video Accessibility Act

Washington, D.C. - The Telecommunications Industry Association (TIA) yesterday filed comments with the Federal Communications Commission addressing the implications of Section 102 of the 21st Century Communications and Video Accessibility Act of 2010 as the FCC considers amending its Rules Governing Hearing Aid-Compatible Handsets to apply to new technologies to be consistent with the Act.

"TIA supported the passage of the Accessibility Act, including the HAC provision, and commends the Commission for incorporating this important legislation into the early stages of this proceeding," said TIA Vice President for Government Affairs Danielle Coffey.

TIA supports the principles set forth in the FCC's Policy Statement, written to "ensure that all Americans, including Americans with hearing loss, will reap the full benefits of new technologies as they are introduced into the marketplace" and "maximize the number of accessible products for this population."

The principles address:

  • Early planning for hearing aid compatibility product design for developers of new technologies
  • Accounting for technological feasibility and marketability as the FCC promulgates rules pertaining to hearing aid compatibility, thereby maximizing conditions for innovation and investment.
  • Providing industry with the ability to harness innovation to promote inclusion by allowing the necessary flexibility for developing a range of solutions to meet consumers' needs while keeping up with the rapid pace of technological advancement.

TIA believes that the principles of the Policy Statement can be implemented in the amended Rules consistent with the Act. TIA also recommended that:

  • As the Commission expands the scope of its hearing aid compatibility rules to advanced communications technologies, as mandated by the Accessibility Act, it should be guided by the Policy Statement and consistent with Section 710 of the Act.
  • A limited form of the de minimis exemption should be retained for the discontinuance of legacy handsets irrespective of company size.
  • The commission should extend the "power down" option to all GSM 1900 MHz devices to promote technology and competitive neutrality.

TIA comments have been posted at TIA's FCC filings page at tiaonline.org.

About TIA
The Telecommunications Industry Association (TIA) represents the global information and communications technology (ICT) industry through standards development, advocacy, tradeshows, business opportunities, market intelligence and world-wide environmental regulatory analysis. Since 1924, TIA has been enhancing the business environment for broadband, mobile wireless, information technology, networks, cable, satellite and unified communications. Members' products and services empower communications in every industry and market, including healthcare, education, security, public safety, transportation, government, the military, the environment and entertainment.
