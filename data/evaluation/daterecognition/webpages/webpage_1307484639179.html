May 11, 2011

Maryville property sold at auction

GreenBank has taken back another property with ties to local developer Mike Ross.

At an auction conducted Tuesday by attorney Bradley Hodge, the bank submitted a credit bid of $1.5 million for approximately 40 acres of land on Sevierville Road, in Maryville.

According to a legal notice, Ross and Darrell Tipton defaulted on a $3 million loan that was executed in 2005, but was later amended to a $2.975 million note.

The property in question is located near the Asbury Place retirement community. Asbury had previously owned the acreage, but sold it to Tipton in 2005 for just over $1 million. A few weeks later, Tipton transferred the property to an LLC.

In an interview, Tipton said he was interested in developing the site and had spoken with Asbury's then-CEO, Bernie Bowman, about an arrangement in which residents of the new development could buy services from Asbury. Tipton said grading was delayed, fuel prices spiked and he ran into bad soil on the site, and by 2007 Asbury had changed its mind.

Bowman said he did have conversations with Tipton about working together, but that he eventually stepped aside as CEO and Asbury signed a management agreement with a company that wasn't interested in a partnership. Bowman said no legal agreement was ever drawn up to formalize the idea that he and Tipton discussed.

According to Bowman -- who is now Asbury's vice president of business development -- Ross was on the board of Asbury when it sold the property to Tipton, but he said the developer abstained from the vote and the discussion.

Tipton said Ross had told him about the opportunity to buy the property, and helped Tipton get the development loan from GreenBank. "They needed his strength to make the loan," Tipton said.

May 5, 2011

GreenBank parent agrees to a sale

Green Bankshares said Thursday that it has agreed to sell a 90 percent stake in the company to a North Carolina banking company.

In a news release, the parent company of GreenBank said the investment by Charlotte-based North American Financial Holdings, Inc., would be composed of approximately 120 million newly issued shares of Green Bankshares stock, priced at $1.81 a share. In addition, it said that on consummation of the investment, Green's current shareholders would receive a contingent value right entitling them to cash proceeds of up to $0.75 a share.

The deal is subject to shareholder approval, and is expected to be finalized in the third quarter.

Update:
According to the release, the agreement contemplates that GreenBank will be combined with NAFH National Bank. Green Bankshares chairman and CEO Steve Rownd is expected to become a member of NAFH's senior management, and its Tennessee market president.

"This partnership provides stability for GreenBank and its shareholders, enhanced ability to service our clients and great future opportunity for our employees," Rownd said in the release.

Upon closing of the deal, NAFH chairman and CEO Gene Taylor will become CEO of Green Bankshares.

Update #2:
Draft readers might recall this link from February, which highlighted a Charlotte Observer story about NAFH.

According to the Observer, the fledgling company is run by a trio of Bank of America alums and another partner, and raised $900 million during a road show in late 2009. As of the February story, the Observer said NAFH was a $5 billion bank with 84 branches.

It focuses on buying distressed banks, and Green Bankshares certainly fits that bill. In the second quarter of last year, the company set aside staffing for its special assets group and put additional resources into that area in a bid to accelerate its resolution of problem assets. The company's total nonperforming loans jumped from just under $75.6 million at the end of 2009 to $145.8 million at the end of 2010.

April 12, 2011

Bank takes back Rarity Mountain

GreenBank was the only bidder at today's foreclosure auction of the Rarity Mountain project, in Campbell County.

The Greeneville-based lender submitted bids totaling $7,933,762 in five separate auctions that took place at the Campbell County courthouse, in Jacksboro. Despite the lack of interest from other bidders, the auction process itself took more than two hours, as an attorney for the bank read each foreclosure notice in its entirety.

The sale brings closure to one of East Tennessee's most prominent real estate failures of recent years. Launched by Maryville developer Mike Ross, Rarity Mountain at one point was slated to include custom homes, a hotel, shopping and a golf course shaped by famed designer Pete Dye. Several years ago, the state built a $12.4 million interchange at the site, with most of that cash coming from the federal government and Rarity chipping in about $240,000 for engineering costs, but no homes were ever built and the golf course is unfinished.

Green Bankshares axes dozens of jobs

Troubled East Tennessee lender Green Bankshares has laid off dozens of employees across its footprint.

Outgoing CFO James Adams, who plans to retire in May, said Friday that about 70 jobs were eliminated, including loan officers and other employees. According to its most recent annual report, Green Bankshares employed 730 FTEs at year's end.

Earlier this month, the Draft reported that Ed Mayberry, the company's Sumner County regional executive, was no longer working for the company.

February 15, 2011

"Painful options" for GreenBank parent

The $53 million fourth-quarter loss racked up by Green Bankshares has sparked some ominous words from a pair of analysts.

Mark Muth, of Howe Barnes Hoefer & Arnett, last week downgraded the Greeneville-based lender to a "sell" rating, and said in a research note that "only painful options exist" for the company.

"Our thesis reflects our belief that (Green Bankshares) faces only two options at this point: a recapitalization or the distressed sale of the company," Muth wrote.

In a Feb. 3 research note Stifel Nicolaus analyst Carter Bundy wrote that he was keeping a "hold" rating on the stock despite the "bleak outlook" because the company "has a valuable deposit base."

Bundy wrote that the best course for Green Bankshares would be a sale to a larger institution. "The largest risk is that the company does not sell or is not able to (recapitalize) itself successfully and in a timely manner which would seemingly put the company's back closer and closer to the wall, eventually increasing the possibility of receivership," he wrote.

Green Bankshares is or has been a non-investment banking client for securities-related services of Howe Barnes within the past twelve months. Stifel Nicolaus expects to receive or intends to seek compensation for investment banking services from Green Bankshares in the next three months.

November 10, 2010

Green defers payments to government for bailout cash

From the AP:

Green Bankshares Inc. will not pay quarterly dividends it owes to the Treasury Department as part of its bailout deal, the company said Tuesday.

The Greeneville, Tenn.-based bank holding company told Treasury that it postponed the payments indefinitely. Green holds $72 million of government money.

The company also will defer interest payments on $88.6 million in trust-preferred securities held by investors. Trust-preferred securities are bond-like investments that count toward the capital cushion a bank must keep to protect against future losses.

The moves will protect about $5.1 million of capital per year for GreenBank, which is owned by Green Bankshares.

October 21, 2010

Analyst: Green "must raise additional capital immediately to survive"

Green Bankshares on Wednesday reported a third-quarter net loss available to common shareholders of $36.4 million, prompting a plunge in the company's share price and downgrades from analysts.

The company reported net loan charge-offs of $36.5 million, compared to $18.4 million in the third quarter of last year. As of Sep. 30, nonperforming assets accounted for 8.16 percent of total assets, compared to only 4.48 percent at the same time last year.

The company's stock had fallen by more than 40 percent in mid-day trading on Thursday, to $3.64 a share.

In a news release, the company said the results reflected higher costs related to loan charge-offs and losses incurred on real estate owned by the bank, due to completed sales or updated property appraisals.

The release also said that the company engaged a third-party loan reviewer, and that "this review contributed to the asset quality-impact reflected in our third quarter results."

"We continue to aggressively focus on dealing with our problem assets through our Special Assets Group and, depending upon economic conditions, envision the possibility of further credit challenges through the remainder of 2010," chairman and CEO Stephen Rownd said in a news release. "While credit quality remains a major obstacle, the core performance of our company is improving."

Analyst Kevin Reynolds, of Wunderlich Securities, downgraded the company's rating from Hold to Sell, and lowered the price target to $2 a share, from $7.50.

In a research note that painted the results as "horrible", Reynolds wrote that "we believe (Green Bankshares) must raise additional capital immediately to survive. Unfortunately, we have doubts as to whether the company can pull this off successfully."

A Green Bankshares executive took issue with Reynolds' analysis. Chief financial officer James Adams said the idea that the company must raise capital immediately in order to survive is "an overreaction."

"Clearly as we look at all the alternatives, raising capital is one of those alternatives," Adams said, adding that "our regulatory capital ratios are very, very strong."

November 7, 2008

GreenBank parent seeks blank-check shares

Green Bankshares may ask shareholders to authorize a class of one million blank-check preferred shares, a move that could pave the way to participate in the federal rescue package.

In a preliminary proxy statement, the company said the emergence of credit problems in the banking industry "suggests that the industry is entering a period where capital conservation and augmentation will be critically important."

The filing said the board has determined Green Bankshares "should have a full range of capital financing alternatives available in its Charter to, among other reasons, help support any future growth."

The board has also authorized management to explore participating in the Treasury's capital purchase program, according to the filing, which said that if the company did participate "the minimum amount of preferred stock issuable would be approximately $24.1 million and the maximum amount would be approximately $72.3 million."

James Adams, the company's CFO, said in an interview on Friday that the approval sought from shareholders is necessary for the bank to participate in the federal program and that the proxy statement was in for review at the SEC.

The filing also says that if approved, the proposal would "supplement and strengthen the Company's existing takeover defenses."
