Palin and the Federal Marriage Amendment: Dobson First

131 comments
By David Neiwert

Sarah Palin breaks with John McCain, telling CBN's David Brody that she would support a "Federal Marriage Amendment" effectively banning gay marriage:

I am, in my own, state, I have voted along with the vast majority of Alaskans who had the opportunity to vote to amend our Constitution defining marriage as between one man and one woman. I wish on a federal level that that's where we would go because I don't support gay marriage. I'm not going to be out there judging individuals, sitting in a seat of judgment telling what they can and can't do, should and should not do, but I certainly can express my own opinion here and take actions that I believe would be best for traditional marriage and that's casting my votes and speaking up for traditional marriage that, that instrument that it's the foundation of our society is that strong family and that's based on that traditional definition of marriage, so I do support that.

This is how the McCain campaign is using Palin to keep the religious right on board even as he stages a supposedly "moderate" agenda in pursuit of suburban votes. Palin's sending a signal to the Dobson faction that was responsible for her ascension that their agenda is in play.

On States' Rights
Karen — 10/20/08 3:33pm

ThePopulist (not verified):

What ever happened to states rights?

Oh, how I loathe that phrase. States do not have rights! People have rights. States have powers.

And whether we're talking about a united state or the United States, states only have the powers specifically delegated to them by the people.

gentlefury:

I heard someone on npr this morning mentioning there was a discrepency over something because the federal law conflicted with the state law, and the federal law was being followed.....this country is founded on a reverse pyramid of power where the federal gov is the least powerful.....federal law can be overturned by state, and state by county, and county by popular vote! Thats what this country is supposed to be OF THE PEOPLE!

Well, not quite. The philosophy is that all powers begin with the people, who, in order to secure their equal rights, may constitute governments, delegating power thereto.

Under our Constitutional system, the people ratified a federal Constitution, which is the "supreme law of the land." It delegates a finite list of powers to the federal government. As long as an act taken by the federal government is consistent with the powers delegated to it by the Constitution, such an act takes precedence over a conflicting state law.

However, the federal government is supposed to be limited to the very small list of powers specifically enumerated to it. Through ridiculous interpretations of those powers, the federal government now gets to do just about anything it wants. You know, like, outlaw marijuana by claiming that doing so is just regulating interstate commerce. Abject nonsense, but current Constitutional construction.

Any powers not specifically delegated to the federal government are, under the Tenth Amendment, "reserved to the states or to the people." Crazy "states' rights" conservatives think that means that the states have plenary, carte blanche powers to do whatever they want. Also not true. They can't violate alienate the rights we retain under the Ninth Amendment, and, as our founding document stipulates, are only rightfully delegated those powers designed to secure our equal rights.

But none of that matters today either. Through more inane Constitutional construction, the Ninth Amendment is meaningless, as is a lot of the Fourteenth, except for the Equal Protection and Due Process clauses, which, through jurisprudential acrobatics somehow protect certain rights of privacy like abortion and contraception. Oh, and the Declaration of Independence is but a mere historical document with no legal significance at all, despite the fact that it was an act of constitution that formed our nation, written, amended and passed by a unanimous Continental Congress.

The text of the Declaration and the Constitution are not that difficult to follow. Unless, of course, you want power over people to restrict their liberties.

Everyone is equally entitled to the pursuit of happiness. Wasn't that once self evident?

The Declaration of Independence was an act of constitution. By signing it, the members of the Continental Congress declared themselves "free and independent states." They also listed the philosophy on which their new government was to be based.

The Declaration was passed the same way any law is passed. Representatives wrote it, submitted it for debate, amended it, and ultimately signed it. By doing so, they knew that they were either setting themselves up for treason charges, or creating a new country. As we know, they did the latter.

How can it not be the law in the United States that we are all equally entitled to the pursuit of happiness?
