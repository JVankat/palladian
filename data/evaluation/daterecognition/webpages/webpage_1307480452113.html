Coal-ash ponds

By JOURNAL EDITORIAL STAFF
Published: October 15, 2009
» 3 Comments | Post a Comment

North Carolina utilities depend heavily on coal to produce a relatively inexpensive supply of electricity. This month, North Carolinians learned again, however, that the cost of coal-generated power involves more than appears on our monthly bills.

State data released by a Boone-based environmental group shows that contaminants from 13 utility-owned coal-ash ponds have been seeping into local groundwater. Coal ash contains arsenic, lead, mercury and selenium. All are heavy metals that the Environmental Protection Agency considers serious threats to human health.

Neither Duke Energy nor Progress Energy, the state's two largest utilities, disputed the report from Appalachian Voices, the environmental group.

Coal ash is the residue of coal generation. As the demand for electricity has grown, so has the amount of ash. Also, in one of those twists that proves the old saying that "nothing's ever simple," the nation's quickly growing ash pile is the result of efforts to improve air quality. As coal-powered plants have been equipped to better scrub air emissions, they've captured more ash. That ash has to go somewhere.

Some goes into construction, but a lot more goes into ash ponds. Less than a year ago, a 300-acre coal-ash pond in Tennessee burst and flooded a nearby community. That brought the issue of the ponds to national attention because there is little regulation of the ponds.

Those regulation-free days are about to end. The EPA has promised regulations by the end of the year and, most likely, those new restrictions will prove expensive.

EPA may require the disposal of dry ash in lined landfills. Today, disposal of that toxic ash is unregulated, whereas a family's far-less-dangerous garbage bag must go into an expensive landfill.

Lined landfills are considered the safest and most effective method for disposing of dry ash, but the most expensive. And because of that cost, the reaction a lined-landfill requirement would generate is predictable. Vested interests in the coal and utility sectors will protest that we cannot afford the change.

Of course we can afford the change. When lives and good health are at stake, we can afford the safest form of ash disposal. We'd just have to pay more for electricity and that might lead us to take other steps -- such as using less of it.

There's another economic consideration to be added to this equation. Alternative forms of energy production like wind and solar don't create these environmental and health problems.

Let's keep that in mind when the vested interests in coal power begin complaining about any new ash-pond regulations.
