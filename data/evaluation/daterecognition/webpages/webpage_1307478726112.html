Being Embraced by Your Spouse’s Family

Saturday, September 25th, 2010, by admin

I had to attend a funeral of one of my many sister-in-laws last week. It was a grueling week because she was the second sibling to die out of 13. My youngest child refused to attend, stating she hates funerals now, though she did attend the wake to see her cousin. My oldest son stayed in college and my middle son was a cross bearer. Fortunately as I walked into that hall where people were gathered, all of my husband’s brothers were lined up close to the door, and one of their wives took hold of my shoulder and told me to walk with them. That basically was a gesture, a good indication that I was to be included as part of their family still. We all sat up front together, and walked together to the cemetery following the hearse. Two of the brothers put their arm around me as we walked, with a sensitivity rarely shown. It was conjuring up memories for them also of burying their brother.

Losing a husband changes your place in the family. They wonder who you may marry, how you are caring for their nephews and nieces, and whether you even want to be included in their lives for regular events like Christmas, etc. The males may want to spend more time with your kids but they know they have a different rope to balance on now that you are single, so they may not feel they can spend as much time as they would with their brother’s children, in order to avoid any jealousy on the part of their spouses.

What I will say is this: being deserted by extended family after losing a spouse is devastating to you and your family. This is when you need them to gather round you, support you by taking the kids out and inviting you to those events that are hard to get through without two parents. Short of signing my children over to their care, I would have given a zillion bucks to have them spend more time with my children, taking them out hunting, fishing, camping, working on renovations at their home so they could learn to do some basic building or fixing, or watching the hockey game during playoffs.

One word of warning however to widows out there – be very careful that in your need to have positive mentors to fill the gap your husband left, you do not indiscriminately allow people you do not know well enough to spend time with your children. You are setting up a recipe for disaster in exposing your vulnerable children and yourself to God knows who. Find out everything you can about them, do a search on them, and if your gut tells you something is wrong with the person, listen to that vibe.
