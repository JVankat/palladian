Mercenaries 2: World in Flames Review (360)

When developer Pandemic's Mercenaries: Playground of Destruction was released near the end of the previous console cycle, many gamers missed it. An open-world war zone in which players are expected to capture or kill 52 members of a rebellious North Korean splinter faction, the game is most noteworthy for one simple fact: you can demolish any structure, vehicle, human or plant that you see. Were it not for that one fantastically well-implemented saving grace the original Mercenaries, with its somewhat aimless plot and buggy behavior, would have been relegated to gaming's Purgatory alongside the likes of Just Cause and Boiling Point: Road to Hell.

Enter Mercenaries 2: World in Flames for Xbox 360, PlayStation 3 and Windows PCs (a scaled-down PS2 version is also available). It's bigger, there are more buildings, vehicles, people and trees to destroy in the new Venezuelan setting and the story has been tightened up with a bit more focus. And the publisher is Electronic Arts instead of Lucasarts. The full-scale destruction is truly a joy to behold, moreso now than it was before thanks to the fancy new affordances of current generation hardware. Truly, Mercenaries 2 inches ever closer towards delivering the mother of open-world sandboxes. If only there weren't so many got-damn bugs.

Click through for the goods.

New Setting, Same Wanton Destruction in Mercenaries 2: World in Flames

Mercenaries 2 starts with players selecting one of three mercs to guide through the story, all of them returning for another tour after the previous game. Mattias Nilsson is the resident lunatic, an inked up Swede with a bad attitude and a penchant for rapid health recovery. Chris Jacobs is the obnoxious American, a former Delta Force soldier blessed with the ability to carry more ammunition than his fellow mercs. Jennifer Mui hails from Hong Kong, though her military experience comes from her time serving as a member of MI6. In addition to filling the game's "badass hottie" quotient, the athletic Jennifer can also run faster than her male counterparts.

The merc you choose to play as has no effect on the story. Each is seemingly tailored to different styles of play – and they are, to a certain extent – but in the end it all boils down to blowing crap up with the heaviest firepower available.

As for that aforementioned story, the game's tutorial mission ends with your merc of choice being double-crossed by the job-giver Ramon Solano, a soon-to-be Venezuelan dictator who will seize control of the country's oil reserves and generally behave like an ass in front of the global community. Before that can happen however, he's got to take care of your selected merc for knowing too much. Predictably, the assassination fails and your chosen gun-for-hire gets away with the parting gift of a bullet-ridden posterior.

Unlike the previous game, in which players needed to capture or kill lower-level bounties before taking on story missions for the higher-level ones, the unfolding of Mercenaries 2 is a bit more focused. Each faction has a set of missions available that further the story, with players traveling to event markers and watching cutscene introductions a la GTA. Then there are sideline tasks: outpost capturing (three per faction, unlocks more shop items and new helicopter transit points), HVTs (High Value Targets, or human bounties), specific structures targeted for destruction and a variety of more esoteric tasks, such as checkpoint races and timed challenges.

The overarching story is solid even if the voice-acting is generally laughable. Initially, players will only be able to pick up missions from two local, rival factions: United Petroleum, the Venezuelan arm of a major oil concern, and the People's Liberation Army of Venezuela, or PLAV, who oppose UP's presence in their country. It's not long either before a gang of organized, modern-day pirates enter the picture as well.

However, roughly halfway through the game a major political event triggers the arrival of two super-powers, the only returning factions from the previous game: China and the Allied Nations (or AN). And unlike Playground of Destruction, the two major nations are now at direct odds with one another, having finally reached the brink of open warfare. In the middle of all of this is your mercenary, who (**spoilers ahead**) has located Solano and will need nothing more or less than a nuclear bunker buster to exact proper revenge. (**end spoilers**)

Building Your Private Army in Mercenaries 2: World in Flames

Early on in the game, players are tasked with capturing a massive, ornate villa to serve as a home base for the duration of Venezuelan operations. Your two-person team will need to grow however, as airstrikes, vehicles and supplies don't drop themselves. A focus in the early missions then is on hiring first a helicopter pilot and later a mechanic and jet pilot to serve as allies in your own, personal PMC.

In the previous game, the only limits placed on the procurement of drops and airstrikes related to progress through the story, faction relationships and money-in-hand. The almighty dollar is still of primary concern, but players must also now be aware of their fuel stores (most drops consume some, though the maximum storage amount can be improved in faction shops) and their overall stockpile. Free fuel, along with airstrikes and cash caches, is scattered throughout the open-world; "appropriating" those resources is as simple as marking them and dropping a smoke grenade.

Instead of accessing shops from the game's menus, players must travel to different faction outposts and buy their drops there. Anything that's been purchased (or awarded as the result of a mission) is added to your personal stockpile. At any point, up to three drops/airstrikes can be mapped to a pause-free pop-up menu which is accessed by pressing up on the D-pad. It seems like subtle difference at first, but once factions start to turn on you for your actions – and they inevitably will – having a healthy stockpile of their best shop items saved up before the fan turns into a toilet becomes all the more important.

Open-World Warfare in Mercenaries 2: World in Flames

The basic controls and combat mechanics in Mercs 2 are same as it ever was for the most part. Helicopters are made easier to hijack roughly halfway through thanks to the addition of a grappling hook – only usable for heli-jacks, sadly – to your arsenal. Tanks and choppers also get a QTE minigame when they are jacked, but unchanging animations make this potentially exciting feature feel repetitive all-too-quickly. There are some new small arms to play with as well, but Mercs 2 weapons really boil down to two basic types: the ones that can't blow things up and the ones that can.

Unforunately, the sandbox in Mercs 2 is just as much a flawed mess as its predecessor was. Our review time – which amounted to one full playthrough plus half of another – was host to an unending parade of glitches. Helicopters rotating in place, allies and enemies alike walking into walls, helicopter transit requests ending with our merc being physically dropped from a decent height onto the landing platform (and taking damage as a result), friendly AI which wouldn't enter our vehicle when honked at, a horribly implemented GPS system, significant texture pop-in, floating items… seriously folks, this list goes on and on and on. And on.

The thing is, for all of the glitches, it rarely takes away from the main show: blowing crap up while going Rambo on the bad guys. It does occasionally get in the way, but never to the point that the game needs to be turned off. HVT missions can be frustratingly difficult to complete "properly" (i.e. by subduing and extracting the target) thanks to brainless enemies who will more often than not send a stray rocket into the guy you're trying to protect. That is if the HVT himself doesn't wander off a nearby cliff, a consequence which occurred more than twice during our play time.

The real downer is that there's no "Load Game" option once your in and playing. So if you're going for HVT extractions every time, be prepared to quit out to the main menu, reload, travel to the HVT and start the whole process over again multiple times. Not helping matters is the fact that there are so many damn load screens; hasn't modern open-world gaming progressed to the point that we can safely skip the 30-second-plus load screen every time we enter/exit a shop or call for transit to another map location?

It is somewhat amazing then that the Mercs 2's online component – drop-in/drop-out co-op play through entire game – functions at all. Not only does it work… it works well. Sure, you'll see the same level of glitchery online that you do off, but there are no additional twists like lag or dropped connections to worry about. The two online mercs can travel a good distance apart from one another before the game imposes a barrier (marked on the minimap as a red zone), so there's even space for some fairly complex coordinated assaults.

While it's true that the technical issues do bring the entire experience down, the core gameplay in Mercenaries 2: World in Flames is entertaining enough to keep players engaged for a full playthrough. Hell, some may even start over for a second go-‘round to see how the story unfolds if an alternate path is taken (there comes a point when one of the superpower factions needs to be chosen over the other). Mercenaries 2 is the very definition of sloppy game development, but it matters little in the end. If you're a fan of open gaming spaces, non-linear approaches to action and destruction on a mass scale, even the worst of Mercs 2's glitches won't get in the way of your good times.

Gameplay: B
Presentation: C-
Fun Factor: A
Value: B-
Overall: B
