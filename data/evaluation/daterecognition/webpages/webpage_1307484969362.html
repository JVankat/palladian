HAWIJAH FOURTEEN Iraqis were killed, half of them soldiers, in three car bomb attacks around the northern town of Hawijah on Tuesday, the latest blow to Iraq's fledgling security forces. The explosions occurred as the government hailed successes in a more than two-week-old sweep of the capital but warned against complacency saying the insur-gency's demise would be a "slow death". The three suicide bombers struck almost simultaneously, targeting army checkpoints on the northern, western and eastern entrances of the restive Sunni Arab town, which lies 210 kilometres north of Baghdad, police said. The latest casualty toll from the morning attacks stood at 14 killed and 20 wounded said Dr Jasim Hamad, director of Hawijah's general hospital. Among the dead were seven soldiers, three children and one woman, he said. US forces sealed the agricultural town. Streets were deserted as Apache attack helicopters patrolled overhead, an AFP correspondent reported. The deadliest attack was at a checkpoint on the north-western side of town on the way to Dibs in which nine people were killed. The bomber detonated his charge at the checkpoint as other cars queued to be searched, said witnesses and local police. Car parts and human remains littered the scene. A suicide bomber wearing an explosives belt struck outside an army recruitment centre in Hawijah on May 11, killing 32 people and wounding 28. North of Baghdad, four Iraqi soldiers were killed in an ambush and a roadside bombing, police and the army said, while two bullet-riddled bodies were found on the banks of a nearby river. Car bombings north of the capital last Thursday and Friday killed 25 people, the bloodiest attacks since the end of May, during which month around 700 Iraqis died in insurgent violence. A new car bombing in Baghdad on Tuesday wounded 28 people, shattering the relative calm of recent days as Iraqi forces backed by US troops pressed a sweep dubbed Operation Lightning. The blast hit a police patrol in the predominantly Shiite district of Shula in northern Baghdad, an interior ministry official and medics said. Government spokesman Leith Kubba said that the operation, now expanded south of the capital to the so-called triangle of death, had netted 887 detainees since May 22, revising down an earlier figure of more than 1,200. "Fighting these criminal networks ... and eradicating them will not happen with a knockout blow, but rather it will be a slow death and it will happen with continuous efforts to isolate them. "Despite a tangible improvement in the situation in Baghdad, we must not have a false sense of security. "All indications show that there is a retreat by these networks but they are still doing one or two operations to prove that they are alive." Militants released video footage of a Turkish businessman and two companions they said they were holding, accompanied by a threat to kill them within four days unless Ankara halts all cooperation with the US military and firms in Iraq. A short videotape aired by Dubai television showed the businessman identified as Ali Abdullah seated on the ground in front of a banner bearing the name Ali bin Abi Taleb Brigades and flanked by two gunmen. Turks have frequently fallen victim to the persistent violence in their southern neighbour - around 90 have been killed in recent months, most of them truck drivers caught in ambushes. Amid heightened tensions between the Shiite majority and the ousted Sunni Arab elite, the Iraqi Islamic Party said a Sunni cleric had been lured from his home in the mainly Shiite southern city of Basra, tortured and executed. "The body of Salem Abdel Karim al-Kardissi was found under a bridge" northwest of Basra after men wearing police uniforms took him from his home on Sunday, said Alaa al-Sabah, an official with the country's main Sunni party. Police in Basra, where a Shiite cleric was killed in a drive-by shooting last Thursday, said they no information on the affair. The Committee of Muslim Scholars, the most influential grouping of Sunni clerics, Tuesday accused the government and security forces of singling out Sunni Arabs in Operation Lightning. "We hold the government responsible for the fate of those arrested, the majority of whom are not terrorists," Sheikh Abdel Salam al-Kubaisi, a senior member of the committee, told reporters. And in Kut, southeast of Baghdad, a judge handed the death sentence to two men on "terror charges". The previous US-led occupation banned capital punishment which had been in effect during Saddam Hussein's rule, but it was reinstated by the former transitional government of Iyad Allawi. Two US marines were killed on Sunday and Monday in separate roadside bombings near the rebel hotbed of Fallujah, west of the capital, the US military said. The deaths brought the number of US service personnel who have died in Iraq to 1,675, according to an AFP tally based on Pentagon figures. - Nampa/AFP
