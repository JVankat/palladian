Pennsylvania blowout portends more scrutiny for hydraulic fracturing

By

Dave Michaels/Reporter

dmichaels@Dallasnews.com | Bio

5:48 PM on Thu., Apr. 21, 2011 | Permalink

As natural gas companies defend hydraulic fracturing from federal regulation, a recent accident in Pennsylvania involving a well owned by Chesapeake Energy Corp. can only hurt their cause.

According to Reuters:

Chesapeake Energy Corp. said today it would suspend its use of hydraulic fracturing in Pennsylvania in light of the blowout of a natural gas well there that spewed drilling fluid into nearby waterways.

The escape of thousands of gallons of chemical-laced water has now been contained, Chesapeake said in a statement today. The natural gas company plans to plug the well with a mixture composed of plastic, ground-up tires and heavy mud.

"We have put all well completion operations on hold," said Chesapeake spokesman Rory Sweeney. "Hydraulic fracturing is completely within that process."

Well completion involves the practice of hydraulic fracturing, in which chemical-laced water and sand are injected underground to break apart rock formations and release gas.

The response is likely to appeal to environmentalists and residents who have complained about the dangers hydraulic fracturing poses for local water supplies and have called for long-term regulations that would guide its use in the state.

Chemical industry opposes legislation to carry out Pickens Plan

By

Dave Michaels/Reporter

dmichaels@Dallasnews.com | Bio

4:10 PM on Fri., Apr. 15, 2011 | Permalink

The chemical lobby came out this week against the legislation that would put the Pickens Plan into action. The bill was HR 1380, introduced last week by a group of four House lawmakers.

The American Chemistry Council, whose membership includes many of Texas' petrochemical companies, argues that new incentives for natural gas vehicles could lead to higher costs for manufacturers that depend on natural gas as a feedstock. The group says that subsidies (the bill lowers the cost of natural gas vehicles through tax credits) for just one type of natural gas use are fundamentally unfair. Here's the case they make in a recent press release:

"Supply diversion and its impacts must be considered as lawmakers discuss this bill," Cal Dooley, President and CEO of the American Chemistry Council said. "Congress needs to recognize how the NAT GAS Act favors one U.S. sector over another, and that it distorts markets: Congress should not subsidize some natural gas uses at the expense of others. Natural gas shouldn't need generous federal preferences to compete in energy markets, given its many economic and environmental benefits."

Pickens and other natural gas advocates say the chemical lobby's opposition is reflexive -- they want to keep natural gas prices low. They also dispute the argument that using natural gas in heavy-duty trucks and buses would do much to raise the price of the fuel. In a recent report, the U.S. Energy Information Administration said the price of natural gas is projected to be stable over the next decade thanks to the country's huge shale gas reserves -- even as industrial demand for natural gas is projected to grow 29 percent.

Pickens has largely enjoyed support for his plan. But now that it's been endorsed by President Barack Obama, expect the opposition to grow louder.

By

esouder@dallasnews.com | Bio

9:18 AM on Thu., Apr. 14, 2011 | Permalink

Energy Future Holdings Corp. made less money in the first quarter than last year, according to a filing in which the company announced it took on more debt.

The Dallas power company said in a filing on Thursday with the Securities and Exchange Commission that operating revenue for the first quarter dropped to $1.67 billion from $2 billion a year ago.

Net income swung to a loss of $315 million from a profit last year of $401 million, and adjusted earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization fell to $871 million from $953 million.

The numbers are part of a filing announcing the company made a private placement of $1.73 billion in additional debt, on top of the $34 billion in long-term debt already on the books.

EFH took on the fresh debt in order to pay off $1.6 billion in old debt. The rest will pay fees to lenders who agreed to give the company more time to pay off some loans.

The company had faced about $20 billion in debt coming due in 2014. The company asked lenders for three more years to pay the loans, and on Wednesday, EFH announced that lenders holding 80 percent of the debt had agreed. In exchange, EFH boosted the interest rate on the loans and will pay lenders a bonus.

EFH said the financial data are preliminary, and could change slightly when the company releases final results.

EFH asks lenders for more time

By

Elizabeth Souder/Reporter

esouder@dallasnews.com | Bio

3:18 PM on Fri., Apr. 1, 2011 | Permalink

Dallas power company Energy Future Holdings asked lenders for more time to repay them, and to officially affirm that the company hasn't defaulted.

In return for more time, EFH offered to pay fees and a higher interest rate, according to a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission on Friday.

The deal could give the company three more years to pay off the $20 billion in debt that comes due in 2014 - a huge weight that some analysts have warned could sink the company.

Investors took the company private four years ago through a buyout that has saddled EFH with $34 billion in long-term debt. The buyout was a bet that natural gas prices, which tend to set electricity prices, would remain high. Instead, natural gas markets sank.

EFH also asked lenders on Friday to acknowledge that it hasn't violated any debt covenants. The acknowledgement is part of an amendment to loans that also includes giving the company more financial flexibility.

One lender, Aurelius Capital, had put the company on notice that EFH had defaulted, saying the way EFH handles intercompany loans doesn't conform to the agreements with outside lenders.

EFH officials have said that's nonsense; it handles the intercompany loans properly.

In the filing, EFH said that after private negotiations and discussions with lenders, it expects lenders holding more than half of the debt will agree to the amendment. EFH may only change the loan covenants if lenders holding a majority of the debt agree.

But EFH can extend maturities on any debt that is tendered, even if fewer than half of the lenders agree to that deal.

EFH would get three more years to pay off the loans that are due in 2014 and a revolving loan due in 2013, in exchange for boosting the interest rate on those loans by one percentage point.

Also, the company would pay lenders a fee of 350 basis points for extending the loan maturities. Lenders who agree to the amendment stating the company hasn't defaulted would get a consent fee of 50 basis points. The total amount of money those fees represent will depend on how many lenders accept the amendment and the longer maturities.

EFH proposes to pay the fee partly by taking on more debt.

"Our grid is uniquely positioned to accommodate plug-ins. I will be interested to hear how the Smitherman family uses the car in the coming months.

How much gasoline will Smitherman end up using in the car? Will he have to plug in as soon as he gets home in the afternoon in order to get a full charge? And does that mean he's pulling juice during peak hours? Will the car be convenient for long trips, or will the family use it just to scoot around town? Will it be any trouble to get a fast charger installed? And will it require any upgrades to the neighborhood grid?

EFH sold less electricity to fewer customers in 4Q

By

Elizabeth Souder/Reporter

esouder@dallasnews.com | Bio

5:00 PM on Fri., Feb. 18, 2011 | Permalink

Energy Future Holdings brought in less revenue in the fourth quarter as the company sold less electricity to fewer customers.

Executives with the Dallas power company predict 2011 will be worse than last year. Already EFH lost $30 million on the plant outages earlier this month.

"We're starting this year a little bit in the hole," said chief executive John Young on a conference call Friday.

Still, extreme weather or higher electricity markets could turn things around for the company struggling to pay off about $35 billion in debt.

EFH fourth quarter revenue dropped 33 percent to $1.6 billion. Last year's figure includes revenue from the Oncor unit, which EFH now reports separately. But even without Oncor and other adjustments, revenue dropped 6 percent.

Net income dropped to $137 million from $161 million the year before, but the bottom line figure always includes the revaluation of hedging contracts. Hedges are financial instruments used to protect the company from swings in commodities markets.

Adjust for the hedging revaluations and other issues, and EFH's quarterly loss narrowed to $295 million from $351 million.

The problem is, on the power generation side, fuel costs are rising while wholesale electricity prices remain weak.

And the company's retail unit, TXU Energy, is losing customers.

"It's a constant effort" to retain and win customers, said Jim Burke, who leads TXU Energy. "Pricing has been very aggressive" and the "number of competitors is up 70 percent from a year ago."

EFH executives offered little optimism that things will improve this year. They said they expect earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization to drop by around half a billion dollars this year. In 2010, that number was $3.7 billion.

That's a problem because EFH owes nearly $35 billion in long-term debt. Executives are working to negotiate deals with bondholders to cut the debt and delay maturities. Around $20 billion comes due in three years, and a revolving loan matures in two years.

EFH has ratcheted up the warnings it must legally give shareholders about the debt. At the end of the third quarter, EFH said in a filing that if the period of low electricity prices caused by cheap natural gas persists, it could "adversely impact our ability" to refinance.

This quarter, EFH said:

"We may not be able to repay or refinance" those loans "particularly if forward natural gas prices do not significantly increase."

By

Elizabeth Souder/Reporter

esouder@dallasnews.com | Bio

5:26 PM on Thu., Feb. 17, 2011 | Permalink

Sen. John Carona, R-Dallas, doesn't think the Legislature needs to inact any new laws to prevent another day of rolling outages.

No, he said, "the answer lies in the marketplace self-correcting" and better anticipation and oversight by regulators.

That's his conclusion after a day of testimony earlier this week by power plant executives, regulators and transmission line operators. On Feb. 2, 82 power plants broke down because they weren't adequately prepared for the unusually cold and windy storm.

"I haven't seen anything that rises to the level of requiring changes in law," said Carona, who is chairman of the Business and Commerce committee.

So here's the question: Is $30 million enough of a punhishment for Luminant to do a better job next year preparing for the winter? The answer, of course, depends on how much it costs to beef up winter prep, and Luminant hasn't released that number.

Carona's counterpart on the Natural Resources committee, Sen. Troy Fraser, R-Horseshoe Bay, is making his own plans for legislation.

He told power generation executives on Tuesday: "I would prefer a free-market solution if we can do it. But guys we're government. We have a right to protect the public. We have to have a little bit of government regulatory activity going your way because we can't have this happen again."

He also said he's contempating a law required generators to have firm fuel contracts or back-up supplies for their plants. Some natural gas plants have interruptible fuel contracts, and they lost fuel service in the cold weather. Those plants couldn't fire up when they were needed.

Weatherization plans confidential, even for ERCOT

By

Elizabeth Souder/Reporter

esouder@dallasnews.com | Bio

10:58 AM on Wed., Feb. 16, 2011 | Permalink

After a deep winter freeze caused 82 power generation units in Texas to break down, electricity regulators are asking how those plants prepared for the cold weather.

It seems no one ever thought about regulations for the weatherization of power plants. The outages reveal a bureaucratic black hole. The kind of red tape mess that I imagine might wake the ghost of Molly Ivins to cackle through the Capitol.

Plant owners must file some documentation of weather preparations with the Public Utility Commission. However, the PUC doesn't have the staff to check that the preparations are sufficient or even prudent.

The PUC cannot give the data to someone else to handle, because the weatherization plans are confidential. So, it seems, these documents sit in a locked file.

The Electric Reliability Council of Texas, which answers to the PUC, cannot even see the documents. And it's ERCOT's job to schedule power plants to come online. So, ERCOT might schedule plants to operate during bad weather, without knowing whether those plants are prepared to fire up.

ERCOT had to formally ask to see the weatherization documents by filing an open records request with the PUC.

This is the way a member of the public, such as a journalist, would request information. In fact, that's how I got a copy of the ERCOT request. Jump to see it.

Texas Republicans aim to stop new clean-air rules through spending bill

By

Dave Michaels/Reporter

dmichaels@Dallasnews.com | Bio

5:00 PM on Tue., Feb. 15, 2011 | Permalink

Texas Republicans are seizing an opportunity to block EPA regulation of greenhouse gases through a continuing resolution to fund the government.

The lawmakers, who include Reps. Joe Barton (R-Arlington), John Carter (R-Round Rock), and Ted Poe (R-Humble), are seeking to cut off any funds used to regulate greenhouse gas emissions from power plants, refineries, and other stationary sources. The lawmakers' amendment is one of more than 400 proposed for a bill that would fund the federal government, including EPA, for the rest of the 2011 fiscal year, which ends in September.

Carter also has proposed an amendment that would block new limits on mercury and other toxic pollutants emitted by cement plants. Technology to reduce mercury emissions is available, yet the EPA has never required existing cement plants to adopt it. The EPA estimates that the rule would reduce mercury emissions by 92 percent.

But an SMU study performed for the Portland Cement Association says the regulation would force the closure of some plants and cause more than 15,000 jobs to be lost. The EPA's own analysis found the regulation would cause cement industry employment to fall about 8 percent, or 1,167 workers. Texas is home to 22 cement kilns, more than any other state.

The proposal has touched off a war of words between Carter and Rep. Eddie Bernice Johnson, D-Dallas, who supports the regulation. Johnson wrote that Carter was "standing in line with dirty polluters who put big business and profits ahead of American people." Carter responded that Johnson "and her radical leftist allies at Earthjustice and the Sierra Club" support a regulation that would outsource the production of cement to countries like China and India.

The GOP amendments appear likely to make it into the spending bill, which is likely to pass the full House. But the Democratic-controlled Senate is unlikely to pass the legislation, which would cut $100 billion from discretionary spending levels. And President Obama said today that he would veto the bill.

EPA airs plan for study of drilling's impact on drinking water

By

Dave Michaels/Reporter

dmichaels@Dallasnews.com | Bio

5:59 PM on Tue., Feb. 8, 2011 | Permalink

The EPA issued its blueprint today for studying whether hydraulic fracturing can contaminate drinking water.

Many oil and gas companies are worried that the study will lead to federal regulation of fracking. The practice is currently overseen by state regulators, but Democrats have raised questions about whether states are adequately supervising its environmental impact.

-How might large volume water withdrawals from ground and surface water impact drinking water resources? - What are the possible impacts of releases of hydraulic fracturing fluids on drinking water resources? - What are the possible impacts of the injection and fracturing process on drinking water resources? - What are the possible impacts of releases of flowback and produced water on drinking water resources? - What are the possible impacts of inadequate treatment of hydraulic fracturing wastewaters on drinking water resources?

This production is critical to America's economic growth, but unfortunately has been the subject of a growing number of uninformed allegations and misleading attacks. Accordingly, I will be working to ensure that this congressionally directed study is balanced, appropriately scoped, and most importantly, driven by sound scientific and risk assessment principles."

Shut down old coal, shut out the lights

By

Elizabeth Souder/Reporter

esouder@dallasnews.com | Bio

11:29 AM on Thu., Jan. 20, 2011 | Permalink

According to the Texas Energy Report, Lt. Gov. David Dewhurst told reporters last week he's considering incentives to get power companies to either upgrade or replace old coal plants with natural gas.

Thirteen coal-fired power plants in Texas were built before 1980. Shut those down without immediately replacing the full capacity, and the lights go out.

The Electric Reliability Council of Texas, which operates the state grid, expects Texas will need 65,206 megawatts of electric generating capacity this summer to meet peak demand. ERCOT predicts we will have 73,656 megawatts, giving the grid a fat, 16 percent cushion of excess capacity.

But if you remove those 13 plants, or 8,288 megawatts of capacity, you get a razor-thin reserve margin of 0.2 percent.

By 2013, without additional plants, Texas wouldn't have enough juice to keep the lights on during peak summer days.

Of course, shutting down old coal plants reduces pollution that can cause heart and lung problems, as well as global warming. Natural gas doesn't emit as much pollution when burned as coal.

Some industry insiders tell me they would expect electricity prices to rise if we nuked those old coal plants. But that would depend on the price of natural gas.

Further, if we're getting rid of all pre-1980 coal plants, Dallas power company Energy Future Holdings would have to shut down eight of its 12 plants. For a company with a notoriously-tight balance sheet, cutting off that revenue stream could present a serious problem.

Allan Koenig, a spokesman for EFH's Luminant wholesale unit, had this to say: "For well over a century, Luminant and its predecessor companies have been safely generating reliable and affordable energy for Texas. Just as we always have, we believe a diverse mix of generation types is the key to a thriving, successful and competitive energy market.

Still no details from the Dewhurst people on what, exaclty, he is proposing.

In 2010, Texas became a coal state.

Texans got the largest share of their electricity from coal last year, rather than natural gas.

The Electric Reliability Council of Texas said Monday coal-fired power plants generated 39.2 percent of the power used in the Texas grid last year, up from 36.6 percent in 2009. And the state got 38.2 percent of its juice from natural gas last year, down nearly 4 percentage points.

Switching to a coal state has political implications. No longer can the power industry say Texas retail prices are higher than other states because we get most of our juice from natural gas.

Wind power supplied 7.8 percent, up from 6.2 percent the year before.

Texans demanded 3.5 percent more electricity in 2010.

Court temporarily halts greenhouse gas permit program in Texas

By

Dave Michaels/Reporter

dmichaels@Dallasnews.com | Bio

12:48 PM on Mon., Jan. 3, 2011 | Permalink

The Environmental Protection Agency gave Texas an unwelcome present on the day before Christmas: the announcement that EPA would take over the award of greenhouse gas permits in Texas. State agencies typically award pollution permits under the Clean Air Act, but Texas has refused to participate in federal regulation of greenhouse gas emissions.

In response, Texas Attorney General Greg Abbott sought an emergency stay of the EPA's action. The AG argued that the EPA's action "seeks to deprive Texas of its right to manage its air resources." Abbott also argued that the EPA's decision, announced as an "interim final rule," didn't give Texas or anyone else a chance to comment on the proposal.

Late last week, the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit temporarily halted the EPA's award of greenhouse-gas permits in Texas to give itself more time to study the arguments. But the court noted that the decision "should not be construed in any way as a ruling on the merits" of Abbott's motion.

The court is scheduled to rule on Texas' request for an emergency stay sometime after Jan. 7.

The court's decision creates some uncertainty for major emitters in Texas that require greenhouse gas permits. But Bill Becker, executive director of the National Association of Clean Air Agencies, told Greenwire that the impact would be minimal. Becker said only a few businesses are expected to apply for greenhouse-gas permits in early 2011.

The political connections behind a West Texas wind farm

By

Dave Michaels/Reporter

dmichaels@Dallasnews.com | Bio

6:00 PM on Thu., Dec. 9, 2010 | Permalink

The Dallas Morning News has covered several steps in the odyssey of U.S. Renewable Energy Group, led by Dallas investor Cappy McGarr, to build a gigantic wind farm in West Texas with U.S. stimulus funds, Chinese capital and Chinese equipment. But sometimes, you need to step back from such a story and ask: okay, now how in the heck is this going to happen?

To their credit, that is what the Investigative Reporting Workshop did in their most recent story on stimulus-funded renewable energy projects. You can read reporter Russ Choma's piece here.

The story examines the political connections that seek to ease China's push into the U.S. wind-power market -- and make its stimulus subsidies more palatable to Democratic lawmakers. (Last year, Sen. Chuck Schumer, D-NY, threatened legislation to halt the project from getting stimulus money.) McGarr is a Democratic heavyweight who donated $50,000 to President Obama's inauguration. (That's him next to Jon Stewart.) Since then, he has visited the White House eight times in 18 months, according to White House visitor logs that Choma checked. Then there is the Nevada connection -- A-Power Energy Generation Systems Ltd., the Chinese firm that hoped to supply turbines to the U.S. market, announced last year it would build a factory in that state, which just happens to be the home of the Senate Majority Leader.

Another interesting nugget: A-Power recently signed a joint venture with former DNC Chairman Terry McAuliffe. McAuliffe has a long career at the nexus of business and politics, but the article connects him to G. John O'Hanlon, a Democratic operative and lobbyist who is called one of the project's "anchors" in Washington.

The article is timely because the stimulus program that US-REG and A-Power hoped to tap expires at the end of 2010. The wind industry is lobbying for a two-year extender to be included in the White House tax deal announced earlier this week. (It may indeed be extended in order to get Democratic votes for the legislation.) The project owners also want a DOE loan guarantee, which would reduce their financing costs. They have yet to apply for either incentive, the story says.
