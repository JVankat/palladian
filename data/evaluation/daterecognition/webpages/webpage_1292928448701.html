Australian politicians stumble to campaign halfway point

SYDNEY (AFP) -- Australia's battle-weary politicians have begun to stumble as a six-week election campaign reaches its half-way point, with a series of gaffes threatening to derail carefully-rehearsed pitches to voters.

Peter Garrett, the former rock star turned opposition environment spokesman, became the latest to blunder Friday when he reportedly said his party would scrap its election platform if it won office on November 24.

The one-time front man for protest rockers Midnight Oil later played down his off-air exchange with a radio host as a ""jocular conversation"" but it allowed the government to say his Labor Party could not be trusted.

It followed a trying week for the government's Health Minister Tony Abbott, who turned up late for a nationally televised debate with his opposition counterpart Wednesday, leaving her free to attack his policies uninterrupted.

To make matters worse, after finally arriving 30 minutes late, a testy Abbott snapped ""that's bullshit"" when his opposite number Nicola Roxon needled him after the debate, guaranteeing a run on national news bulletins.

Australia's political leaders have not been immune to fumbles during the campaign, with Prime Minister John Howard unable to recall interest rate levels and opposition Labor leader Kevin Rudd fluffing income tax scales.

Howard also promised last week to maintain annual increases in defense spending ""until 1916"", delighting critics who accuse the conservative prime minister of living in the past.

Former Western Australia premier Geoff Gallop said politicians were under intense scrutiny during election campaigns as the media magnified every gaffe and each unscripted meeting with voters loomed as a potential banana skin.

""In the pressure cooker atmosphere of an election campaign, when madness descends upon the Earth and people feel they can do things they wouldn't normally do, politicians in a sense are converted into the village idiot who you throw the cabbages at in the town square,"" he told ABC television.

""That becomes very stressful.""
