For over five decades, both children and adults around the planet have spent a lot of time playing and creating things with Lego bricks. What exactly you can build with the bricks is limited only by how many you have and your own imagination. Though developed as a children’s toy, the product has countless adult fans too and a quick look on the internet will throw up some amazing and creative designs.

Going a long way to prove that it doesn’t lose its appeal as we grow up, the most unbelievable thing I’ve seen done with Lego was the properly sized two level house, complete with a working bathroom and a bed which TV star James May confirmed was the worst bed he had ever slept in. I must be honest here that I may have toned down the colour scheme for the house a bit, or I think I’d have been walking around in sunglasses indoors and needing Laser eye surgery to save my eyes from hurting too much! Making an object so big could obviously only be pulled off with a large amount of help and a large sum of money, but the human mind can come up with some fantastic creations even with only a considerably smaller amount of pieces available to use.

The Lego tale began in 1932 in an unremarkable town in Denmark where an entrepreneur named Ole Kirk Christansen founded a factory producing wooden toys for youngsters. He asked his staff to suggest a name for the factory, but finally came up with one himself by merging the Danish words ‘Leg’ and Godt’, literally translated as ‘Play Well’, an appropriate name if ever I heard one!

He soon discovered that plastic was much more durable than wood when it came to toy production, and purchased in the pioneering first injection moulding machine ever used in Denmark. He started testing plastic building shapes and in 1949 he developed the prototype for Lego bricks. The Lego brick was launched onto the market in 1958, and although there have clearly been a lot of additions to the original product when it comes to colours and shapes, the size of the bricks has always stayed consistent enough that Lego blocks bought today would still fit together precisely with the original 1958 bricks.

In 2011, Lego products are manufactured in Denmark, Mexico and the Czech Republic and all factories are subject to the same incredibly high quality standards which make sure that the pieces all over the world will always slot together and stay together properly. The margin of error for the size of the bricks is so small as to be virtually unimaginable to the human brain. The variances allowed are in fact so insignificant that I must assume that they are checked by something like a Laser eye beam and a computer program to ensure such consistency.

The Lego business is one of the biggest toy manufacturers globally based on profit, and employs around 4,500 staff. At the end of the 20th Century it gained awards in both the USA and Great Britain as the ‘Toy Of The Century’ and I don’t think that there are many individuals who would argue with that accolade.

There are allegedly 2,400 different Lego pieces, which is an an amazing number of shapes, sizes and colours! Of course, nowadays, a lot of the Lego sold is in kit form and is designed to build specific objects ranging from a fort to a fire engine, a pirate ship to a space rocket and much more besides. Many of the kits are based on films or television shows, so, for example, you can create something like your own Star Wars world, including many of the characters, spacecraft, droids and even Mos Eisley Cantina! Although even Lego’s talented creators are yet to work out how to get the lightsabers to give out a authentic Laser eye beam!

For those who wish to make Lego scenes which are more realistic, there is the City collection of kits, which enables you to build all of the buildings, vehicles and people you need in your town – police, fire brigade, the airport, trains, farms and loads more so that you can douse the flames of fires, pilot planes and arrest people. I’m sure, given time, you’ll even have kits that allow you to remove a Lego appendix, carry out Lego Laser eye surgery or extract Lego teeth!

I used to believe that creating so many kits was a bit of a shame. I often asked to what extent they have deprived the youngsters of today of the chance to use their own imaginations to come up with unique shapes and designs. But now I’ve revised my opinion and believe that the kits are a good thing as long as nobody says that they must be put together as shown on the box. For example, look at a wheel and think about how many other things are that kind of shape. You soon start to understand that the human imagination is way more ingenious than you think!
