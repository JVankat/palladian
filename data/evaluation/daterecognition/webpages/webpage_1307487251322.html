Nobody wants to be taxed. So of course, from a certain point of view, everyone has TEA sympathies. Many people, however, and especially those who lean left, perceive a problem in continuing the drumbeat of lowering taxes. At some point, we're just not going to be able to pay for government. There are those who say we shouldn't pay for government at all, but of course, government provides valuable services that we don't see because we've never been directly held as slaves, or wage-slaves, or been penned in to a factory and told we were working 80 hours for the next 8 months, etc.

Our government, in the form it exists in now, exists to protect our population from unscrupulous persons who would abuse wealth and power in order to accrue more wealth and power. It happened in the Gilded Age, it happened again in the 1920s, and it can happen again. Unions, as onerous as I personally may find their practices and the degree to which they've overshot their original value, exist to protect workers from abuses that they (as a class) have suffered before.

So of course we need government. And of course we have to pay for it. So of course we need taxes. The question is where do we find the balance between being taxed enough to pay for things we want to exist (Federal protections, education, military, etc) while being taxed little enough for us to get along, improve our personal financial situations, and eventually retire. I'm not sure where that balancing point is, but with revenue at under 15% of GDP compared to the 'norm' closer to 19-20%... I'd say we don't have a 'taxes are too high' problem, we have a 'taxes are too low' problem.

Flat taxation is regressive. If a family already spends 90% of the money they earn in a year on food and housing and required transportation, then the flat 10% tax eats up all their potential savings. If another family only spends 20% of their income on food, housing, and required transportation, then the 10% flat tax leaves them with 70% of their income.

It hurts most those who must spend all of their money to survive and then have no money for taxes (except they have to pay taxes, so they suffer on food, housing, or transportation).

As long as a national sales tax doesn't target those products most purchased by low and middle income people, then it would be a sure success in terms of targeting. The problem is that it wouldn't generate much revenue. The wealthy don't buy that much more personal goods than the worse off, they buy more luxury goods and more luxurious personal goods (more dollars per roll of toilet paper for example), but there's only so much stuff a person can buy before the satisfaction gain drops off.

Steve S. May 5, 2011, 2:12am EDT
I can't disagree with your assessment. In fact, I think you point out a serious flaw in whatever kind of tax system we implement...exceptions to the rules. It seems that whatever tax-rate scheme we come up with, there are just too many ways to dodge paying a fair share as one's income increases, particularly past a certain point.

The mortgage-interest deduction is an example. A cheap house likely won't generate enough of an interest payment to be of much , if any, tax benefit to a person of modest income. Quadruple that income and house payment, however, and that person will likely want to file a long-form. Raise those numbers enough, and now the house can be sheltered as a business investment, in a foundation, or as a corporate asset. Add to this that we are also taxed in inconsistent ways by various authorities, and there just gets to be too many ways to hide one's money.

The other problem I see in America is waste of taxes. I wrote a long comparison of Japanese versus American taxation, debt, and currency as it relates to tax-productivity in another post. The Japanese carry essentially the same tax burden as Americans, but it is comparatively astounding what it produces. As a consequence, they enjoy an overall higher standard-of-living, and a stronger economy than America.
