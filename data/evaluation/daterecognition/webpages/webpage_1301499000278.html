Retail Notebook: Artisans from here and afar splash color in West Seattle

By DAN RICHMAN
P-I REPORTER

Divina, a West Seattle store selling handcrafted Latin American clothing, leather goods and jewelry, celebrates art in many forms. It's a place where good feelings predominate, in part because of its brightly colored artwork and clothing, but also because of the principles it embraces.

Not to mention its cute puppy.

 

Launched 15 months ago by an artistic high school grad who also has a career at Microsoft, the store strives to treat both local and distant artisans fairly, and in turn is supported by neighborhood residents.

Divina offers students at nearby schools talks on fair trade and the business of artistry. It displays the work of several artists at any one time, along with handmade greeting cards and refrigerator magnets from yet other artists. It puts on art shows, recently displaying the work of 150 local students.

It offers live Brazilian music every few months, featuring Seattle guitarist Kiko Freitas.

And it has Lola, a young, black pug that eagerly greets customers, sometimes wearing one of her three Peruvian handmade wool sweaters.

"Everybody wins," summed up customer Peggy Ganong, 51, a West Seattle resident who made her fifth visit to the store earlier this week.

Owner Julie Mireille Anderson, 41, said she founded Divina -- pronounced dih-VEE-nuh, which means "divine" in Spanish -- in September 2006 as a place to show off objects she loves from artists she admires.

 
 Meryl Schenker / P-I
 Julie Mireille Anderson opened Divina 15 months ago to show off objects she grew to love while traveling and working in Latin America. Behind her is a painting by artist Emanuel Vardi.

A Seattle native, Anderson finished high school and then studied flute and art at Cornish College of the Arts, leaving without a degree. She headed abroad and eventually found work helping Microsoft install software in its Latin American offices. During 11 years of that work, she traveled widely, making it a point to meet artists wherever she went.

"I just fell in love with Latin America," she said. "Uruguay, Argentina, Brazil and Chile in particular produce high-quality artisan products, and I wanted to demonstrate that in our manufactured world, it's still possible to have handmade things of quality that are really unique."

Anderson's 900-square-foot shop is laid out in an appealingly sparse fashion, with simple fixtures and minimal furnishings.

Along one wall are women's sweaters, throws and shawls, mostly made of soft wool colored with fruit, vegetable or root dyes, though the blacks are synthetic.

Many of the striped and solid items are bright, and some are very bright. Colors include blues, purples, reds and oranges, the latter from carrot dye.

"Even their muted tones have more vibrancy than what we typically see," Anderson noted.

The sweaters come in several styles, from short turtleneck capes to calf-length. All are on sale for $100 through the end of the year.

Divina doesn't carry men's clothing, because "Northwest men don't go for the colors like Latin men do," Anderson said.

Most of the knitted goods come through Manos del Uruguay, a cooperative that Anderson said now works with 400 artisans, setting up an infrastructure for them to get materials and produce work under livable circumstances.

Manos sells goods to Donna Karan, Marc Jacobs and Nordstrom under their own labels, but it waives its minimum-quantity rules to sell Divina clothing that's usually found only in the local market, Anderson said. In the spring, Manos sends collections of cottons and linens, also organically dyed.

Arrayed in the front window are about 50 pairs of dangly earrings, made in Uruguay of forged silver and quartz stones ($10-$50). Also dangling are squiggly pendants and earrings made of recycled aluminum, copper and brass ($22-$42).

On a table are leather wallets and purses, made by a 20-person workshop in Uruguay. Purses range from $65 to $180, wallets from $20 to $60 and one briefcase is $235.

Because all the items are handmade, usually no more than five are alike. For example, a sweater in one style may come in five sizes, extra-small through extra-large. The same style, in the same color, is unlikely to recur, because natural-dye lots vary widely.

"Having a unique thing, not mass produced, is appealing to a lot of consumers, particularly women, and there's an extra appeal because things are fair-traded," Anderson said.

In the back room are children's clothes. Khaki and corduroy pants, in calm neutrals and cut slimmer than is typical in this hemisphere, are $24. Bright wool sweaters are $45 to $70.

Woven wool pieces from Argentina or Uruguay and usable as scarves, shawls, table runners or small rugs range from $40 to $90. Scattered throughout the store are stylish hats by Vashon Island milliner Laura Clampitt, who is also an American Airlines flight attendant.

The walls are covered with framed artwork.

Weekday manager Caroline Bell, who was born in France, produces colorful children's illustrations and graphics. Emanuel Vardi, a former violist now unable to play after an accident, paints large oils, acrylics and pastels of musicians in a style reminiscent of Modigliani and Picasso. Sharon Birzer, a local artist, embellishes antique wallpapers.

All are for sale, for $120 to $15,000.

Whimsical greeting cards of sewn designs on paper are made by another local artist, Jihyun Andersen, and sell for $4 each. Refrigerator magnets of colorful abstract paintings by local artist Deana Plymale are $1 to $5.

"All the artists are my favorites to work with," Anderson said. "The nice thing about owning your own business is you only have to work with people you like."

Most of the store's customers come from West Seattle, though some are from Fremont and Ballard, Anderson said. The store isn't profitable yet, but she predicts that it will be within two to three years. Business has been steady, even during a paving project that blocked street access for months.

"The community has supported me, and I feel like I'm able to help make an impact on the community," she said.
