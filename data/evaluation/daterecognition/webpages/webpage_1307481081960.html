Threat of federal government shutdown prompts concern among area residents

Comments 0

April 07, 2011 4:44 PM
Michael Barrett

Ginger Feimster taught school for more than 30 years before recently retiring, and her Social Security benefits just kicked in.

With a potential federal government shutdown looming at midnight Friday in the wake of stalled budget negotiations, the Belmont resident fears those benefits might kick right back out.

Link to interactive feature

“It’s a frightening thing,” said Feimster, 65, who is getting married at the end of May and planning a honeymoon. “Those of us who are retired and do get Social Security benefits definitely depend on it.”

Feimster was at the Social Security Administration office in Gastonia for a different matter Thursday afternoon. But with the shutdown on her mind, she didn’t receive any words of comfort from officials there.

“I haven’t been told anything,” she said.

Danny Boyd of Gastonia receives a disability check on the first of every month. He and his wife, Sharon, had mixed emotions about the effect of the potential shutdown Thursday, and how Congress will work to avoid it.

“If it’s going to be shut down at all, it’s probably going to be less than a day,” said Sharon Boyd. “They’re going to have to get their act together if they don’t want the whole country to rise against them.”

The couple said they feel the problems will be worked out by the end of the month.

“I’m not really worried,” said Danny Boyd. “I’m more concerned about the people in our military getting paid. They work a job just like the rest of us.”

The lack of a congressional agreement on a budget or another short-term measure wouldn’t affect some federal government responsibilities. But a partial government shutdown could lead to some consequences for area residents.

U.S. Postal Service

Don’t stop checking your mailbox if a shutdown occurs. The U.S. Postal Service is self-funded, officials say. So the mail will continue to be delivered.

Social Security Administration

Federal officials have said they would continue to mail Social Security and Medicare payments to current recipients. And applications for benefits would continue to be processed, though some services would be limited depending on how long the shutdown lasted.

Social Security field offices would be closed. Some limited services would still be available at field offices, but it’s unsure how many.

Some 48 million people receive Medicare in the United States, most of whom are seniors, but several million of whom are permanently disabled Americans. Those claims would continue to be paid, though payments to doctors and other service providers could be delayed if a shutdown lasts several months.

Internal Revenue Service

Tax audits would be suspended in the wake of a shutdown, according to the Obama administration. The IRS would continue to deal with electronic tax returns, although the processing of paper returns would be halted.

Taxpayers who are expecting a refund would be asked to file their returns electronically and request that the money be deposited directly into their bank accounts.

Veterans services

The Veterans Services Office in Gaston County assists veterans and their dependents in processing claims and obtaining benefits to which they are entitled under federal, state and local law.

But Angela Karchmer, coordinator of the Gastonia office, said veterans have nothing to fear.

“We are not anticipating any impact on programs or benefits our folks are receiving,” she said. “Veterans benefits will continue to be issued, even if there is a shutdown.”

Karchmer said the Gaston County office is 100 percent locally funded, and does not rely on federal financial support.

USDA Farm Service Agency

The U.S. Department of Agriculture has a Farm Service Agency office in Dallas that serves farmers in Gaston and Lincoln counties. But executive director William Davis said they also foresee no problems there.

The FSA provides price support payments, marketing assistance, loans, crop insurance payments and conservation payments to area growers.

“I don’t think it’s really going to affect our customers too drastically,” said Davis. “If it’s just a short shutdown of a week or two, it’s not going to delay anyone’s payments from this office.”
