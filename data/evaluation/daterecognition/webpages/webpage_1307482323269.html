Chapter FourEntry #514519
A black yet stylishly elegant carriage scythed its way through the cobbled streets of London town. Two of the finest black stallions at the helm, there shadows illuminated by two brilliantly burning oil lanterns as the thunderous sound of there cloven hoofs drilled hard unto the rain drenched street below. A familiar sight to many of the locals it may be but something about the sheer sound and grandeur of its form and presence never failed to gain the attention of all those nearby. All of London knew of its occupancy, for few commanded a wealth to uphold such stature.

Inside Sir Maxton Silas basked in the comfort of plush red velvet while staring out at the tattered site of the common people. The sight of such poverty always left him with such a feeling of disgust deep in his gut, the vagrants littering the street slowly consuming London like a bloody disease, a disease he wanted rid off. Still despite this innate disgust, he respected each and every one of them. He had no desire to see them harmed, in fact the services of a great many of them he had recruited to fill the needs of his own business endeavours, paying them quite handsomely for their services to.

Through his experience he�d found many of the more so called respectable members of society simply too greedy, possessed by a spirit of self importance and a belief that their position enabled them to enjoy a more lazy lifestyle, a combination of traits that infuriated a man to whom success and a disciplined hard work ethic meant everything. The poor were simply more driven, more appreciative of a chance to change their squalid lives with a moral code and loyalty often absent in the higher rungs of society.

Sir Silas was well aware of the corruptness that was London, he formed an integral part of it himself, still through his acts of compassion and his unrivalled charm he had the respect of the people, a respect his peers envied wildly for none could touch him. To hurt this man he would first need to be disgraced. In the past a select few had tried such a feat, all had failed, all had suffered, many had died. Maxton was every politician�s friend, after all their success depended on it, not to mention the Queen�s strongest ally.


Sir Maxton Silas� carriage finally arrived at his destination with a gravelly whoa from his driver. A well dressed elderly door man hastily approached the carriage to open the door.

�Sir Silas, so grand to see you again. Here sir may I take your cane, let me help you down.�

Silas with the aid of the doorman lowered himself out the carriage to be faced with the grand double oak doors of the lavish Commonwealth Club

�Here sir, your cane.� A nervous hand returned the cane that formed an integral part of Silas� proven image.

�Thank you, that�s very kind deer fellow.�

The doorman bowing his head with respect �Always a pleasure sir.�

Silas turned his attention to the driver �Giles�, he barked �I�ll require you return for me at 11pm, do not be late. Now be off�.

�As you say sir� the driver hastily departed into the ever darkening street.

Silas made his way up the cold stone steps to the grand foyer. The Commonwealth Club represented the grandest of all the Gentlemen�s Clubs in London, only the most respected, most influential men would even be considered for membership and Silas himself represented the lynchpin of its nobility. The grand foyer itself was the work of great craftsmanship, grand gothic carvings etched into the priceless oak by grand masters in their fields forming a perfect background to some quite exquisite antiquities reaped from the servants of the Great British Commonwealth nations.

A deep red carpet laced the floor and cast a path to the grand staircase and the club itself. Silas slowly climbed the stairs, cane in hand and entered the room of his peers. Silence temporarily fell, a pattern that often transpired in the awe of this man, before being shattered by the inescapable attention that always inevitably followed.

Thomas Hinckley, a revered engineer, took his opportunity and quickly greeted Sir Silas �So wonderful to see you this fine evening, I was wondering if you�d be interested in hearing my proposal for the building of a great glass structure in honour of our Queen�s most illustrious reign��

Silas interrupted, �Mr Hinckley, I hold the Queen in the highest regard as you do so know, I am pleased to be able to call her a close friend and confident. And for those reasons alone I must so decline any offer of being involved in such a project for it is my profound feeling that such a creation will only serve to insult our beloved Queen. For glass though radiant in its beauty when the light is cast just right, is so easily shattered, a symbolism of weakness in my eyes and one I would not wish our enemies to ever bare witness. Now if you will excuse me.�

With the rebuke of Hinckley�s proposal witnessed so publicly by his peers, the patron�s in presence quickly realised that tonight was not the night to engage Silas in matters of business.

Although almost a nightly attendee at the Commonwealth Club Silas rarely seeked excessive interaction with his peers, instead he preferred to sit back in his favoured leathered chair and watch the endeavours and transgressions in practice of his fellows. Besides this provided a perfect and most reliable alibi should any acts he may be involved with transpire.

Silas sat in waiting, scotch in hand in amusement of the frivolity of the pastimes his peers had engaged in. Excessive drinking, high stakes gambling, ill informed business decisions cast with an alcohol dampened mind. It amused him how these represented his biggest challenges to him in this, the greatest city in the world, and how they paled in inferiority to him. This train of thoughts was brutally disrupted with the entry of Superintendent Jennings. The Superintendent was the one man Maxton Silas was truly weary off. His unrelenting strong minded pursuit of all things that peaked his interest made him potentially dangerous to Silas. He never feared being outsmarted by the Superintendent or making a vital mistake in his presence for Silas knew he was simply much too smart for that. Still his blind adherence to the pursuit of justice at any cost could not be understated. Naturally Silas therefore ensured he maintained an excellent friendship with the Superintendent, he represented one of the few peers he regularly engaged with, a firm believer in keeping your enemies forever close at hand.

On engaging eye contact the Superintendent approached. �Jennings so good to see you, can I pour you a Scotch.� enquired Silas.

�Of course you can my friend, best make that a strong one, the hands this city is in I need one.�

Bemused by his response Silas further pursued his enquiries, �Something troubling you, I fear little progress has been made in your investigations of late?�

�Little progress, try no progress, these monkeys I command wouldn�t know common sense if it bashed them to death with their own truncheons. We have burglaries, street theft, rapes, murders and it keeps getting worse, the state of this society truly scares me�.

Though Silas had been involved in recent events of a undesirable nature he knew his activities and that of his aide Henry remained unsuspected. Silas had taken careful measures to ensure his activities had been covered up well taking full advantage of the current murderous and distasteful activities that had been holding London fast in the grip of crime.

�Maxton my friend, are you feeling ok� snapped the superintendent

Silas gratefully received the white handkerchief and wiped the rich crimson blood from his nose. As Silas cleaned up the last of the blood he couldn�t help but detect the distinct aroma of the handkerchief, an aroma he was only too familiar with, one with feminine origins no doubt.
