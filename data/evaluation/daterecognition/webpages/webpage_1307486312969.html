Caring for the environment means becoming aware of the consequences of your actions. How you live, what you buy and how you get from point A to B all have effects on the earth. Get clued up on the major issues and support a cleaner, greener planet! 

Recycle!
Help keep our landfills (waste disposal sites where rubbish is buried) from filling up by recycling as much as possible. Drop off old papers, magazines, tins, glass and plastic in local recycling bins. You can also reduce the amount of paper that you waste by not printing emails and by reusing envelopes and gift wrapping whenever possible. When out shopping, use a fabric shopping bag (or reuse your old plastic ones) rather than paying for new plastic ones.

Reuse
Think before you throw anything away. Many household items can be reused. Peanut butter containers can be used to store soap powder, juice bottles can  be used for storing fabric softeners and cooldrink cans can be reused as cash boxes. Use old newspapers for starting fires or for stuffing into shoes and boots  to help keep their shape. If you can’t find an alternative use for something you  no longer need, ask neighbours or friends if they can use it.

Use less energy
We all need to save electricity, because if we don’t the cost will just keep rising. Use low-energy light bulbs and if possible get a geyser blanket (to keep the geyser hot for longer). Turn off electrical appliances at the wall plug when not in use, and turn off the lights when leaving a room. Also unplug your cellphone, MP3 player or camera charger when not in use. And if you have the means, explore solar power alternatives. In terms of Eskom’s solar power initiative, you can get a big rebate on any solar power system you install at your home or business. Visit www.eskomdsm.co.za/?q=Solar_water_heating for details.
 
Buy local
Buying imported food, clothing, CDs and DVDs means that extra fuel is being burnt to transport the items to you. Help reduce your carbon footprint by buying locally. Look out for clothing and shoes made in South Africa, and buy your fruit and veg from a local market or store. Always buy what’s in season – it’s cheaper, healthier and will support a local grower. Another avenue to explore is growing your own fruit and vegetables.

Go easy on water
The way we waste water is a shame –  especially when you consider that 1.1 billion people in the world don’t have safe drinking water! To save water, close the tap when brushing your teeth, and shower instead of bathing (it uses less water). Also, never leave a tap or hose running while you do something else, and try to reuse your bath or washing-up water for watering your plants.

Help reduce pollution
Pollution results in smog, acid rain, unclean water, global warming and holes in the ozone layer. All of these conditions affect us negatively and impact directly on our health. An easy way to do your bit against pollution is not to litter. Also, choose your transport options carefully: if possible, use public transport, a bicycle or start a lift club with your friends. Walk whenever you can, and if you are going to buy a car, buy one that is fuel-efficient. Governments around the world are taxing car owners based on their fuel emissions, (the more fuel used, the more tax paid) so the less fuel your car uses, the less you will be taxed.

Don’t dump!
Don’t dump anything except at the municipal dump. It’s dirty and disrespectful, and it’s against the law so you can be fined. Dumping poisons the soil and poses a health threat as it spreads disease. Find out from your local council offices where you can dump legally in your area.

Green lifestyle tips

  • Check that your fridge door seals properly, and don’t leave  it open for long periods as this wastes electricity.
  • Turn your geyser to 55 or 60 degrees so that it doesn’t use too much electricity. Switch it  off every morning and back  on again at night.
  • Replace old bulbs with energy-saving bulbs, available at your nearest supermarket.
  • Buy things like sugar, flour, rice, potatoes and maize meal in bulk with friends and relatives  – to save money and save on packaging.
  • Wherever possible, buy items with the least packaging (plastic, foil, cardboard, etc).

What does ‘carbon footprint’ mean?
Your carbon footprint is the sum of all emissions of carbon dioxide (CO2) caused by your activities (such as driving a car or burning other fuels) over a given period of time.

What is global warming?
It is the increase in the average temperature of the earth’s atmosphere, which upsets the balance of nature and causes all sorts of environmental problems – and health problems for people and animals.
Jet Club magazine, July/August2010
