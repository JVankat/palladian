Roosevelt departs from Yalta Feb. 11, 1945

  • Print
  • Comment
  • Email
  • Subscribe
By ANDREW GLASS | 2/11/08 6:24 AM EDT
Text Size
  • -
  • +
  • reset

On this day in 1945, President Franklin D. Roosevelt, accompanied by his daughter Anna, departed from the Black Sea port of Yalta in the Crimea aboard the heavy cruiser USS Quincy after conferring for a week with British Prime Minister Winston Churchill and Soviet Premier Joseph Stalin.

“The Big Three” met to fix the postwar boundaries of Europe. Roosevelt also pressed Stalin to declare war on Japan to help shorten the war in the Pacific.

At the time, an Allied victory against Nazi Germany seemed imminent.

Confined to a wheelchair, FDR remained a heavy smoker.

His health declined further as a result of the 7,000-mile trip; he would be dead from a brain hemorrhage two months later at age 63.

Roosevelt took Anna with him to Yalta instead of his wife, Eleanor.

Serving as his personal assistant and confidant, Anna also dealt with such details as adding an extra person to a dinner table of 13 to soothe her superstitious father.

His decision to bring Anna deepened the rift between the already estranged couple.

He sent Eleanor a brief report that concluded, “I am a bit exhausted but really all right. It has been grand hearing from you.”

During the journey home, Roosevelt’s closest adviser, Harry Hopkins, fell ill and disembarked at Algiers to recover, choosing to fly home at a later date.

Roosevelt took Hopkins’ departure personally; his failure to forgive him soured their friendship.

To make matters worse, Roosevelt’s military aide, Edwin “Pa” Watson, died of a stroke two days into the return voyage.

In his 1965 memoirs, Britain’s Anthony Eden wrote: “Roosevelt was, above all else, a consummate politician. Few men could see more clearly their immediate objective, or show greater artistry in obtaining it. As a price of these gifts, his long-range vision was not quite so sure.”
