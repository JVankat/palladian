BP Cleanup On Hold as Tropical Storm Nears

Jul 23rd 2010 – 8:07AM
Text Size

CBC News
Work to permanently plug BP's damaged well in the Gulf of Mexico is on hold as tropical storm Bonnie swirls toward the Gulf region.

BP said in a statement Friday that work on relief wells will be "temporarily suspended" because of weather risks associated with the tropical storm.

Federal officials said Thursday the custom-built cap that has largely contained the flow of oil since integrity tests began last Thursday would remain closed - even if most ships are ordered out of the area.

There had been some concern that the cap would have to be reopened and left gushing if a major storm moved through the area, but engineers expressed confidence that keeping the cap closed would not damage the well.

"While these actions may delay the effort to kill the well for several days, the safety of the individuals at the well site is our highest concern," said retired U.S. Coast Guard admiral Thad Allen, head of the federal effort to deal with the spill.

Officials ordered dozens of vessels to leave the area around the well site Thursday. They also ordered crews to remove boom equipment from delicate marsh areas not currently threatened by oil to protect it from being crushed in tempestuous weather.

Allen said teams would be able to "promptly re-start" cleanup efforts as soon as the storm passes.

A tropical storm watch is in effect for parts of the central and northwestern Bahamas and parts of Florida's east coast, including the Florida Keys.

"A tropical storm warning means that tropical storm conditions are expected somewhere within the warning area within 36 hours," the U.S. National Hurricane Center said.

The storm caused flooding in Puerto Rico, the Dominican Republic and Haiti before reaching tropical storm strength later Thursday. It could reach the Gulf area late Friday or early Saturday, forecasters said.

Bonnie is the second named storm of the Atlantic storm season, which is expected to be busier than usual. Researchers at Colorado State University predict there will be eight hurricanes in 2010, compared with the last-century average of 5.9.
