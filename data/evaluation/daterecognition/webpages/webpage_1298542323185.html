2010-12-26   Rolex Sydney Hobart Yacht Race 2010   Rolex Sydney Hobart Yacht Race Sailing Instructions 44.1 & 44.2 requires competitors on approaching 37⁰ 15" South ( Green Cape) to assess a number of items relating to the safety of the vessel including ?its HF radio is operational and has signal strength fit for the ensuing purpose?. Before reaching 37⁰ 34" South (Gabo Island) the skipper is required to radio the Radio Relay ship on HF radio and confirm that they have complied with 44.1. Failure to comply with this instruction entitles the Race Committee to score the boat DNF, amending RRS 63.1
Two of the yachts competing in the event made the radio call as required prior to passing Gabo Island but shortly thereafter their HF ceased functioning. Both yachts established a private schedule informing the Race Committee of their position by satellite phone in accordance with SI 40.6 .
The Race Committee protested both boats on the grounds that the radios were not fit for the purpose, that is they should have remained operational for the remainder of the race (over 300 miles)
The International Jury found that as the radios were in working order and that the boats were able to make the obligatory radio call prior to Gabo Island, both boats had complied with the SI?s at that moment in time. The SI was deficient in that it made no provision for the subsequent failure of the radio nor did it express the Race Committee?s intention.
Both protests were dismissed.

The relationship between the Race Committee and the International Jury was excellent		   Published

2010-12-15   2011 World Sailing Cup Sail Melbourne   When starting in the afternoon on Charlie Course the Laser Standard class and their coach boats caused problems by going through 2 course areas whilst earlier fleets were still racing. The jury were requested by the Charlie Race Officer to assist in clearing the boats, especially near the inner loop gate.
This was done but numerous competitors were intransigent and the jury were moving close to one Laser when a fast moving coach boat came towards the jury boat and Laser. The driver had to make an immediate fast turn to port to avoid a collision which would have ended in damage. In doing so, the other judge who was sitting on the sponson talking to the competitor ended up in the water under the hull. The driver responded very quickly in turning off the engine preventing serious injury.
Quite a number of competitors apologised for this and their behaviour improved for the remainder of the week.

Race Management
On the Thursday afternoon a localised fast moving storm with winds in excess of 35 knots and severe rain came through the race areas which had not shown up on any of the weather radar.
Very few boats remained upright and all jury, support and coach boats went into rescue mode attending to all the boats who were either on their side or had turned turtle, assisting in picking up crews and reuniting them with their boat or assisting to take down the main. Priority was given first to the junior classes.
The storm lasted for about 40 minutes before abating and all boats were recovered and escorted or towed to the marina. Minor damage to boats, mainly sails and rudders, was caused and a number of RS:X boards on shore were lifted up and blown around the dinghy area causing various extents of damage.
The Race Management's emergency plan worked well

2010-10-3   J-80 World Championship   The J-80 Class uses judges on the water for Appendix P, and to monitor for breaches of Class rule C.8.3(b) involving premature launch or tardy retraction of the bow sprit. They also use a sailing instruction to permit a member of the international jury who witnesses what he believes to be an infringement of a racing rule to signal this with one long whistle from the jury boat. Boats then decide whether to take a penalty or not. The international jury limited the latter to rules of Part 2. In winds over 20 knots most days it was a challenge to observe whether, after a breach, a boat protested, and to get close enough to signal an observed breach. Using a zone system, one jury boat was always at the windward mark and one was always at the start, leeward gate, and finish.

2010-9-20   2010 Melges North American Championship   The IMCA 24 Class requires that the jury be proactive on the water. Quoting the class instructions to the jury: The class operates both the yellow flag for rule 42 and the red and U flag for class rule C.11 and all other RRS (violations). A boat taking a penalty is required to do 1 turn but a penalty given by a judge is 2 turns.

The jury has a prolonged discussion regarding the use and timing of the red flag, relating specifically to violations of the RRS. The jury did not want to interfere with the sailors ability to protest or take a penalty yet wanted to ensure the wishes of the class were followed. Our decision was to follow a 3-step process.
1- The judge must have clearly observed the incident.
2- Wait for the competitor to display a protest flag. If no protest flag was displayed within the expected time, penalize give-way.
3- If a flag was displayed, wait for give-way to clear to begin her turn. If no reaction, penalize give-way.

Of the 6 calls made during the event, 4 were on the first day. In the following days, competitors were seen to taking penalty turns, sometimes without a protest flag being seen.

Not new to the class, comments from the competitors were that they liked this system. It encouraged boats to take their penalty. One commented that the fleet was very aware of the jury boats,one was always within view.

Over the 5 days and 14 races, wind ranged from 6 to in excess of 25 knots, and from flat water to waves of 1 to 2 metres. The race management was very good, under some difficult conditions. Course changes occurred often, quickly and efficiently.		   Published

2010-9-10   2010 World Laser Masters Championship   Appendix P was modified by the sailing instructions to provide for an 8 point penalty for a first penalty that is signalled after a boat has finished. Appendix P3 was also modified by the sailing instructions to deny a boat that has received a second or subsequent penalty under P1 in a race to compete in the restart if that race is subsequently postponed, recalled or abandoned.

Binding arbitration, using two judges working as a team, with a scoring penalty of 30%, was also included in the sailing Instructions.

In addition, the normal starting penalties in RRS 29.1 was modified such that a boat in the triangle during the final minute before the start was scored OCS with no opportunity to restart but unlike a RRS 30.3 penalty was permitted to restart in a race that was postponed, recalled or abandoned.

The sailing instructions provided for a whistle system to be used when jury members observed rule infringements on the water.
