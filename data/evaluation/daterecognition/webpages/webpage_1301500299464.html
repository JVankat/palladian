Purpose

To articulate the policy of the Department of Transport and Main Roads on the state dimension of planning scheme matters relating to cycling. The department's Interest in Planning Schemes also advises local governments about aspects they might want to consider when they coordinate and integrate this policy into planning.

The government, through the Department of Transport and Main Roads, aims to achieve a sustainable transport system, particularly by reducing use of private motor vehicles.

Integrating land use and transport planning contribute significantly to this. Done effectively, it produces an environment where public transport, walking and cycling can support a greater proportion of trips. Local government planning schemes are the primary tool for creating this environment locally.

Cycling is a healthy and sustainable transport option that we need to cater for with the same degree of attention as other transport modes. There are national, statewide and regional transport and cycling strategies that reflect the need to provide for cycling. In 1999, the Commonwealth Ministers for Transport and Health launched Australia Cycling â€” The National Strategy 1999-2004. This strategyÂ aimed to make cycling trips four per cent of all trips by 2004. The department'sÂ Queensland Cycle StrategyÂ targets a doubling of the proportion of trips made by cycle by 2008, with that figure to double again by 2018.

To support these targets it is necessary to create cycle-friendly environments where bicycle riding is a viable alternative to the private motor vehicle. Local government planning schemes play an important role in this process.

Specific outcomes

The following outcomes provide guidance on how cyclingÂ might be coordinated and integrated in planning schemes. SectionÂ three of this document suggests measures that might be used in planning schemes to implement these outcomes:

  • network of safe roads and highly interconnected and efficient bikeways that give cyclists a choice of route
  • convenient, safe and attractive cycle routes connecting major activity nodes with each other and also linking them to residential areas
  • ensure appropriate end-of-trip facilities including bicycle parking and shower and change rooms are incorporated into developments that are likely to attract significant numbers of bicycle trips, such as business centres, workplaces, community facilities, educational facilities and retail developments.

Planning scheme measures to achieve these outcomes will depend on local circumstances. Some factors to be considered include:

  • whether it is a metropolitan or non-metropolitan area
  • the location of various centres in the area, be they educational/retail/employment based
  • the demand for cycling facilities in the localityÂ 
  • whether the demographic makeup of the area is supportive of cycling as a viable form of transport.

Scheme measures

Overlay provisions (scheme maps)

To provide for the appropriate development of new cycling facilities, it is preferred that planning scheme maps show the detailed location of existing and proposed cycle networks. It is also preferred that the scheme strategic structure and movement network maps, as well as any local plan documents indicate the type of existing and proposed facilities, both:

  1. on-roadÂ â€” cycling lanes, including bike lanes, shared bike/car parking lanes, shared public transport lanes
  2. off-roadÂ â€” exclusive bikeways and shared bike and pedestrian paths.

Existing and proposed bike parking facilities should also be indicated on planning scheme maps where appropriate.

Infrastructure charges schedules

An Infrastructure Charges Schedule is one method the local government can obtain funding towards the provision of cycling infrastructure.

Cycle ways are defined as development infrastructure under the Sustainable Planning Act 2009 and can be planned under the local government's Priority Infrastructure Plan and charged for under an Infrastructure Charges Schedule. Planning for cycle ways would involve specifying a Desired Standard or ServiceÂ for the cycle way network, and planning cycle way infrastructure to meet the Desired Standard or Service. The planned cycle way infrastructure could then be charged for under an Infrastructure Charges Schedule. The cost of the planned infrastructure must be apportioned between existing and future users in accordance with the fair apportionment principles in the Sustainable Planning Act 2009.

The State Cycle Unit has prepared information on funding mechanisms for bicycle infrastructure information as part of the cycle notes series.

Development assessment

To enable cycle networks to be appropriately protected and enhanced through new developments, local government cycling access and mobility standards should be integrated into relevant development assessment codes or provisions. Furthermore, if a local government has developed an Integrated Local Transport Plan or similar transport plan, the actions and policies of this document should also be reflected in the planning scheme components.

Cycling facilities not identified in the Infrastructure Charges Schedule could be included in the development conditions forming part of a development approval. Such requirements usually apply to lower order facilities for infrastructure within the development area itself. However, it is important these facilities show connectivity to adjacent areas and are not isolated.

Code provisions

Codes providing the specific technical aspects of development, including provisions for cycling facilities, are achieved through the planning scheme.

Planning scheme provisions for the convenient and safe movement of bicycle riders to their intended destinations can be included in Codes for Transport and Road Networks as well as Codes for Centres, Residential Subdivision and Community Facilities. An example of outcomes sought relating to efficient movement and safety of cyclists is included in the attachment.

Such provisionsÂ might require the street network to provide for safe and efficient movement for cyclists to major attractors such as schools and educational facilities, town centres, employment centres, recreational facilities and tourist attractions in the area. Code provisions should also enable cycle routes to be located in areas where there is high potential for casual surveillance from neighbouring land uses.

Planning scheme measures can also require adequate provision for end-of-trip facilities andÂ might be included in a stand alone Code, or be integrated into Codes for Centres, Residential Reconfiguring of a Lot, Community Facilities and/or Parking Facilities Codes. The inclusion of such provisions will depend on the appropriateness of the situation.

Performance criteria for such code provisions should provide for adequate, secure and convenient parking spaces for bicycles, safe cycling access to, from and within the site, and showers and lockers. An example of outcomes sought and possible solutions relating to end-of-trip facilities is included in the attachment.

Probable or Acceptable solutions for appropriate design standardsÂ might be derived from AUSTROADS* Guide to Traffic Engineering Practice Part 14Â â€” Bicycles.
