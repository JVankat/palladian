Bruins' QB derby a spring tradition

By ADAM MAYA
THE ORANGE COUNTY REGISTER
amaya@ocregister.com
Next Article »
Tweet
Share
  
  • Article
  • Photos
  • Videos

LOS ANGELES – A quarterback competition has become a spring tradition at UCLA and this year will be no different when the Bruins open camp next Tuesday. Just don't expect them to announce a winner.

The Bruins figure to have a followup battle in the fall, seeing that junior Richard Brehaut and top prospect Brett Hundley are the only healthy options heading into camp. Two-year starter Kevin Prince will be limited to isolated throwing this spring while continuing to rehabilitate a knee injury he suffered last fall.

Richard Brehaut is on the baseball team, but his agreement is to be available for the entire spring practice.
ARMANDO BROWN, FOR THE REGISTER

"Prince is not going to be able to do much," Coach Rick Neuheisel said Wednesday. "I had hoped he could do seven-on-seven, but I've been told he won't be able to do much of that either."

UCLA will get a full dose of Brehaut. Though currently with the baseball team — an endeavor he pursued for the first time as a Bruin this offseason — Brehaut will return to the football field full-time once spring practice begins.

"He won't miss anything," Neuheisel said. "That was our deal when he wanted to pursue baseball."

Less Prince also means more Hundley. Neuheisel said the 6-4, 225-pound freshman, who enrolled in January, will be given a true shot to win the starting job.

"He's going to get lots of chances," Neuheisel said. "How fast he grows and matures in the offense and shows he can understand all he needs to handle to be effective, that will just enhance his ability to play at an early time in his career."

What that offense might look like remains to be seen. Neuheisel reasserted the Bruins will continue to work out of the Pistol formation but not exclusively. He quickly shot down the "spread" label, something new offensive coordinator Mike Johnson has been linked with, but said UCLA will operate primarily out of the shotgun.

"We have to marry the run game with the throw game," Neuheisel said. "We can't just be so one-dimensional that we can be stopped when it comes to throwing the football. We want to be able to throw the ball down the field, throw it with some measure of consistency. I don't want to be a part of it anymore where we're not over 60 percent."

WILLIS QUESTIONS

Neuheisel intimated former Bruins defensive tackle Brandon Willis could be headed back to UCLA. A week ago North Carolina announced Willis had signed a scholarship agreement. UCLA released the freshman from his scholarship earlier this month so he could be with his ailing grandmother.

"That's still an uncertain situation," Neuheisel said. "We signed a release and want him to have an opportunity to explore his options and make sure he's meeting his family obligations. But he has yet to enroll at North Carolina, and I don't think he's heard from the NCAA about the waiver and his eligibility. I'm still waiting to hear. He has not withdrawn from UCLA."

North Carolina requested that Willis be declared immediately eligibility after sitting out last season following his transfer to UCLA. Willis, one of the top defensive tackles in the nation coming out of high school, originally signed with the Tar Heels in 2010 but then left the program for Westwood last summer after his father landed a job in Los Angeles.
