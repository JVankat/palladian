The quangocrats' march into oblivion

As the bonfire of the quangoes blazes, Geoffrey Lean stops to consider what might have been.

You can come, too: it's farewell to the Zoos Forum. 
6:28PM BST 15 Oct 2010

Comments

There but for the grace of God, etc. As ministers culled the quangocrats this week, I paused to reflect that I could have been among them.

Back in the 1990s, I received a letter from an unknown civil servant inviting me to become chairman of the Zoos Forum, one of the victims of Thursday's announcement. I had never heard of it (in fact, it is "an independent advisory body on zoo licensing matters") and knew little about zoos.

I rang the official to confess my ignorance, but was assured it could not matter less. "Actually, it's an advantage," he said.

No gravy train beckoned. I would not be paid, I was told, and I would have to be security vetted, no doubt to ensure that I didn't smuggle in any Tamil Tigers or mountain guerrillas. But no doubt I could have had a career of eminence grisery, shuffling from quango to quango before collecting some minor gong. Naturally, I declined: apart from anything else, I could not see myself commenting to fellow hacks every time an elephant keeled over.

In fact, the fate of the Forum (whose last report was indeed on elephants) seems unclear – it is to be "reconstituted as a committee of experts" – as, I fear, is the point of the entire exercise.

Take it from a refusenik quangocrat: he doesn't.

In for a penny? Not in the Maldives

You will have heard about the sun and sea, the coral reefs and the turquoise-ringed atolls, but no tour of the Maldives should omit a visit to the gents in the foyer of the Holiday Inn in the capital, Malé.

It’s computerised. First, you set the temperature of the seat. Afterwards, you can order up a jet of water for “cleansing”, fix

its temperature, pressure and precise targeting, decide whether you want it to “oscillate”, and finally produce a blast of air for “drying” (the environmentally conscious can do it all in “energy-saving” mode).

Last but not least, you can press a button marked “power deodoriser”.

Mind you, even this is not the most spectacular WC in the city. That distinction belongs to the gold-plated contraption installed in the presidential palace by the autocratic Maumoon Gayoom during his 30-year presidency.

When the former journalist and (tortured) political prisoner Mohamed Nasheed ousted him in country’s first multi-party elections in 2008, he gave the palace to the Supreme Court – to symbolise “the supremacy of the law” – and moved to a more modest residence, with bog-standard loos.

In all, he says, he slashed the cost of running the presidency from some £20  million a year to £1.3 million. Now, that’s a cut to make even George Osborne’s plans for next week look like a flush in the pan.
