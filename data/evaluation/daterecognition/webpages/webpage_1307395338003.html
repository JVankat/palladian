Enron

Lucy Prebble’s play about the fraudulent corporate giant Enron was a huge hit at Chichester and the Royal Court earlier this year. It is no surprise therefore that the show gets a West End transfer in the New Year. It will open at the Noel Coward Theatre in London on 16 January 2010 with theatre tickets already available until 8 May 2010.

Naturally, the show had particular meaning coming at the time of the credit crunch as the story of Enron was in many ways a powerful comment on the instability and inequitable nature of modern capitalism, made before the western world’s financial system went into meltdown. However, it is arguably the epic nature of the show and its fantastic theatricality which make it truly memorable.

It is co-produced by Headlong Theatre and directed by Rupert Goold. The show manages to take what could be very dry material and turn it into a series of memorable images including raptors (that’s right, those nasty dinosaurs from ‘Jurassic Park’) living in the basement and consuming billions of dollars worth of debt. The play stars Tim Pigott-Smith as Kenneth Lay, the grand old man of Enron and Samuel West as Jeffrey Skilling, Lay’s protégé, who ultimately takes the fall.

If you ever thought accountancy was boring, this play will make you realise how wrong you were. Indeed, you may well decide that accountancy is criminal instead! Enron’s accountant’s Arthur Andersen, once the largest accountancy firm in the world, went out of business as a result of the Enron scandal.
