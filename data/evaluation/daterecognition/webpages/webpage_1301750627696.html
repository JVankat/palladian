Christmas bomb attack foiled by U.K. police raids

John Voos, Reuters

Shoppers walk along an unusually snowy Oxford Street in central London Monday. Twelve people arrested in police raids yesterday were allegedly planning to attack "iconic targets" in and around the London area.

  • Twitter

  • Email

Duncan Gardham, Gordon Rayner And Nick Britten, National Post · Dec. 21, 2010 | Last Updated: Dec. 21, 2010 2:03 AM ET

An al-Qaeda plot to target Christmas shoppers with a wave of suicide bomb attacks in London was foiled by a series of raids across the country on Monday.

Twelve men were arrested in Cardiff, Stoke-on-Trent, Birmingham and the capital after intelligence a terrorist cell was at an advanced stage of planning co-ordinated attacks on shops, banks and "iconic targets" in the capital, The Daily Telegraph has learned.

The dawn raids followed months of surveillance by MI5. Police moved in when intercepted communications suggested the bombers were ready to act.

Police Assistant Commissioner John Yates, the country's most senior counter-terrorism officer, said the raids had been "absolutely necessary" to keep the public safe.

Those arrested were young British men of largely Bangladeshi origin who were influenced by al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula and the preacher Anwar al-Awlaki, who is based in Yemen.

The investigation focused on radicals in the Midlands city of Stoke-on-Trent who were associated with the banned group al-Muhajiroun, and others in Cardiff and London.

Mr. Yates said the operation was "intelligence-led" and the men were arrested on suspicion of the commission, preparation and instigation of acts of terrorism.

Theresa May, Britain's Home Secretary, said police were still at an "early stage of what could be a complex and lengthy investigation."

"We know we face a real and serious threat from terrorism and I would like to thank the police and security service for working to keep our country safe," she said.

Those arrested included men from Stoke using the pseudonyms Abu Saif, 19, Abu Bosher, 26, and Abu Sumayyah, 25, a Muslim convert.

Another 26-year-old man from Stoke was among those held, along with five men aged 28, 26, 25, 23 and 24 in Cardiff, and three men, aged 17, 28 and 20 in London.

Some had protested in favour of Islam4UK, a successor organisation to al-Muhajiroun, now also banned, which planned a march against the British troops in Afghanistan.

Neighbours in the Stoke district of Cobridge said one of the arrested men had distributed extremist material. He was one of several young Muslims who stood on street corners, handing out leaflets promoting holy war.

Four row-houses belonging to the same family were raided. Neighbours said the head of the family was a taxi driver, who lived in one house; his children lived in the others.

"They are a very nice family, the dad always says hello, he's very polite," said Wendy Bird, who lives in the street.

Also in Stoke, neighbours said another of those detained was an Asian man in his mid-20s who had moved in to the bungalow four months ago.

"He has never spoken to anyone," said Peter Lewis, a neighbour.

"He has a lot of people coming and going in the early hours of the morning -- it's very odd -- and you can often hear religious chanting and singing coming from the house."

A British intelligence operative, who spoke in return for anonymity under departmental rules, said the investigation into the 12 men arrested Monday had drawn on co-operation between several intelligence agencies and electronic monitoring in several European countries.

Internet communications, he said, had been intercepted, but he declined to give details.

A British former counterterrorism official, who did not want to be identified by name while discussing current operations, said in an interview last month Scotland Yard is working on almost 100 terrorism cases. Thirty of those are pressing, with around 10 classified as urgent or imminent.

The Daily Telegraph, with files from The New York Times
