Fighting talk

The controversy surrounding the Oxford Union's debate on Israel and Palestine misses the point. It's the debating that is the problem.

    • Tweet this
  • Comments (…)
    • Alex Stein
    • guardian.co.uk, Tuesday 30 October 2007 09.30 GMT

The controversy over the one state/two states/no states will they/won't they debate at the Oxford Union (OU) totally misses the point. This is because it is constructed around two dubious assumptions. Firstly, that we should pay careful attention to the words spilled in anger within the "august" walls of the OU. This is expressed by sycophancy towards the institution's importance, a sycophancy expressed by all involved. Secondly, and more importantly, is the unsaid assumption that the traditional form of a debate is somehow geared towards progress and enlightenment.

Leave aside your views on the Israel/Palestine debate - they are irrelevant here. This is about form, not content. As is well known, a traditional debate features a supposedly carefully constructed motion and two teams who are for or against it. "This house believes that children need heterosexual parents," was the topic of a debate held last month at OU, a motion that was obviously really concerned with the issue of whether or not homosexuals should be allowed to raise children.

Say I wanted to attend this debate in the hope of educating myself about the issue, about the theories regarding what makes a good parent, about empirical evidence from homosexuals that have raised children, about all the other models of child-rearing there are out there (single parents, children's houses etc). I'd want a representative sample of experts in the field, and I'd want them to gather in a spirit of free enquiry in order to elucidate the issue. I certainly wouldn't be interested in who wins and who loses.

But, as long as they are masters of polemic, it doesn't really matter who shows up to debate. The participants are there to win, not to pursue truth. It is an adversarial model of discourse, a bit of sport. To concede points to an opponent is a sign of weakness, to express doubt is suicidal. We all laughed when George Bush said you are either with us or against us, but this encapsulates neatly the model offered up in the world of debating.

The fact that the form encourages polemic is reflected in the topics that are chosen. I've already referred to the Israel/Palestine and homosexual parenting debates. What else is on offer this term to the lucky Oxford students?

"This House believes that grammar schools work," whatever "work" means in this context, no doubt a firm favourite in an institution that regularly bats off allegations of elitism.

"This house looks forward to seeing a democrat (sic) in the White House." When? Which one? Vagueness is frequently deployed to elicit controversy.

"This House believes that the EU's 50th Birthday was well worth the party," which hardly seems the most constructive way to open a discussion on the future of the union.

And as if things weren't too predictable, there's "This house believes that in the war on terror the best defence is a good offence," and "This House believes that aid does more harm than good in the developing world." Make what you will of the random capitalisation.

It's as if people can't leave the sixth form behind them. Maturity means developing a sensitivity to nuance, to particularity. It is not best served by locking swords in discussions based around false premises. While great intellectual skill goes into a debate, this does not make it valuable. Nor can the spectacle be justified solely on the grounds of fun - we should take the self-importance of institutions such as the OU seriously, and not leave it unexamined. In this regard, the OU team can't be that surprised at all the fudging they have to do concerning the participants in their debates. You reap what you sow: if your main selling point is controversy, you can't complain when that controversy comes back to bite you in the face.

Maybe I'm just bitter at not quite making the school debating team. But I've referred already to the problem of sensationalism in public discourse, and it's a really important issue, one that should be raised at every opportunity. This isn't just about dumbing down. It's about the deeply felt belief that public forums are important for developing solutions, and as a result should be used in a constructive way. So I suppose the rumours that the one state/two state debate is going to be replaced with a "more open forum where a free flow of views can be held" are encouraging. In the meantime, those watching from the sidelines should remember that the great leap forward isn't going to come from an Oxford Union debate.

  • JonathanWest

    30 October 2007 10:31AM

    Alex, you have rather missed the point of debating and its various applications.

    Unless you can build an argument, you are not going to be able to persuade intelligent people of the validity of your ideas. The skill debating teaches is critical thinking, which includes a thorough understanding the opposite point of view. The immediate purpose of an OU-style debate is of course to understand the opposite point of view in order most effectively to counter it. But the skill taught also has wider applications. Somebody who fails to understand the strengths of the opposite argument will not make a good debater. I've taken part in debates (though not at the OU) where I was proposing a motion I profoundly disagreed with. The skill was just the same - to find the strengths of a position I disagreed with in order to present my argument as well as possible.

    I suggest that learning how to investigate and understand the other person's point of view is a very useful skill, without which no peacemaking efforts could even get started. Unless you acknowledge the nature of the other person's grievances you cannot address them, either by accommodating them or by persuading them that the grievances are unjustified. In turn, the person holding the grievances cannot and will not accept your explanations unless he in turn has some capacity for understanding your point of view. Debating helps teach these skills.

    As for debates not being aimed at discovering the truth, something very similar is aimed at precisely that - our adversarial court system. A jury trial bears a startling resemblance to a debate. Opening statements on both sides, questioning of witnesses and closing statements, all with a view to presenting both sides of the case as fully as possible for the jury to decide. In many cases, the lawyers on one side or the other have profound doubts about the strength of their case, but it is their duty to present it as well as possible so that the jury can make the decision. In order to do that, it is their duty to test the opposing case as far as possible, and that cannot be done without a thorough understanding of the opposing case.
