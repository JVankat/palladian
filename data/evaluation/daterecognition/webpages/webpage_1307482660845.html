Specifics on Family Leave

Published: Wednesday, January 30, 2008, 2:41 PM     Updated: Wednesday, January 30, 2008, 5:30 PM
By Thurman Hart/NJ Voices The Star-Ledger
Follow
Tweet
Share close
Google Buzz Digg Stumble Upon Fark
Share Email Print

Yesterday, I was guilty of passing along bad information. I was corrected by a commenter who passed along outdated information. I'd like to correct that.

S.786 is the bill that passed. I erroneously said it capped payments at $300 a week. The actual cap is 2/3 of average weekly salary up to the state mandated disability payment cap - which currently is set at $524 a week. That's the part I got wrong. The part the commenter got wrong is that the assessed rate is only 0.09% for 2009. For 2010 and subsequent years, it would rise to only 0.12%. But that is only assessed on taxes already subject to Temporary Disability insurance assessments - $27,700. That means the maximum taken from any one worker in 2009 will be $24.93. In 2010, it would rise to $33.24. That is actually well below the one dollar a week I had stated.

I appreciate the correction. Workers should know that they are getting more for less. That would mean there is less reason to resist the bill than what I stated yesterday.

The bill also provides that any employer who wants to exceed the state minimums and offer a private plan may do so. So they aren't taking away anyone's choice in the matter. If you don't want your money going into the state program, talk to your employer. Perhaps they'll buy that thing about us all being stronger if we stand alone.

Some have pointed out that they already have coverage that is as good or better. Wonderful. That won't be taken away. But for many people, the only option available is federal/state FMLA, which allows them to take off for twelve weeks without pay. Guess what? Few are able to do so. According to NJ Citizen Action, a nationwide survey found that seventy-eight percent of those who did not take unpaid leave could not afford to do so. Nine percent of those who did take unpaid leave relied on welfare programs to make ends meet. Consider yourself very fortunate if you are in a position to be cavalier about this program.

This includes small business owners. Pooling resources allows small businesses to be on equal footing with their larger competition. The small mom-and-pop pharmacy just down the block from me just doesn't have the resources that CVS or Rite-Aid has. Even if they want to give an employee time off with pay, they may not be able to afford to do so. This program will allow them to keep valuable employees through the harsh realities of modern life.

This isn't a perfect program, but it's a good program. But, of course, the herd of trolls trampling through here aren't going to let that stand in the way of their desire to sling mud at it. It's a shame, because it cuts short any real and meaningful debate. But then, they also can't help demonstrating how little they are capable of understanding - just today there was an updated comment on my post entitled "An Anti-Bias Crime" which continued to insist that I am am wrong to call the violation of a Jewish cemetary a hate crime. I'd like to point out that "anti" means "not" - as in "not a bias crime". If a person can't understand that, then there is no way they can possibly understand the actual meaning of that post. And that says a lot to me about why they cannot - or will not - understand the need for paid Family Medical Leave. As the old saying goes, there is no one as blind as the one who refuses to see.

Thurman,

You began your previous post on the proposed paid family leave bill with this:

"Family Leave may make it to New Jersey ...But not if the Chamber of Commerce and its allies have their way. That, by the way, should include the ideologically driven concern trolls that frequent these environs."

Others did disagree with much of you opinion on the bill and explained why. That's called debate. I'd be curious to know which commenters on your post did you consider to be "ideologically driven concern trolls"?

In this post you write: "This isn't a perfect program, but it's a good program. But, of course, the herd of trolls trampling through here aren't going to let that stand in the way of their desire to sling mud at it. It's a shame, because it cuts short any real and meaningful debate. But then, they also can't help demonstrating how little they are capable of understanding ..."

Who's the one cutting short any real and meaningful debate?

I wrote on your previous post, "I think we all understand the emotional and financial stress involved with caring for a sick family member. It's just not clear to me how many people this plan would actually help..."

I also wrote on your previous post: "This debate isn't about family leave, that's already the law. It's about providing some level of income replacement while the worker is out on a six week family leave."
And I wrote: "If paid family leave is something people really want, I think a better plan is in order. Why not make employee participation in the state plan voluntary, with sign-up at time of hire and annual open enrollment thereafter? Allow each worker to decide the benefit level (up to X percentage of their weekly pay) they'd like to purchase, with the cost based upon the number enrolled and the benefit level desired? "
You wrote in this post: "seventy-eight percent of those who did not take unpaid leave could not afford to do so."

I think the percentage who still couldn't afford to take family leave would still be quite high given the "horrendously low", as you called it, benefit this bill would provide.

I offered my suggestion to eliminate two problems. One, the people who object to the program and don't want the benefit, wouldn't be compelled to join the plan. Two, people could select an affordable benefit level that would supply sufficient replacement income should the need for a family leave arise. I was trying to come up with a way to make the bill more useful to more people and overcome objections to the bill.

As for the incident involving the punks tipping over headstones, I'll let others decide for themselves about that debate after they read your post and your readers' comments.
