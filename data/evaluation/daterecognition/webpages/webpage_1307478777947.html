The commissioning, which was witnessed by scores of people from the government, industry players, school children and ordinary citizens, started as early as 06h00 yesterday.

The cable ship, Le De Brehat, had positioned a day earlier, as its crew prepared the seabed for the landing of the US$600-million intercontinental project of which Namibia is a partner through telecommunication operator, Telecom Namibia.
Divers laid the cable on the seabed on Monday evening before a crew on shore could pull the cable into the beach manhole, which runs underground along Swakopmund�s busy street for its connectivity position at the Telecom Building in town � less than a kilometre away.

Namibia is joined in the project by Botswana Tele-communication Corporation on an equal investment deal of US$37.5 million each.

Kaapanda hailed the development as a significant milestone for Namibia, stating that the cable landing indicates that the country is at a turning point in information and communication technology (ICT) development. �We are at the dawn of an infrastructure revolution on the Namibian ICT landscape where broadband communication services will be further enhanced to benefit business, industry, Internet community, academia and the entire Namibian population.�

Echoing the same sentiments, Botswana�s Minister of Transport and Communication, Frank Ramsden, highlighted that his country�s involvement in the project was necessitated by the high cost of international bandwidth charges as well as the growth of ICT on the continent.

�However, the growth has been limited by the high cost of services, particularly the international bandwidth. Data roaming in our countries has been limited and this has resulted in business people suffering. Therefore, the delivery of the WACS comes at an opportune time.�

With the cable, it is widely expected that ICT-related costs would be drastically reduced � making it affordable and reliable to end-users.
This is in addition to additional bandwidth spaces that would be available on the cable and could be leased to interested parties.

The 1�400-kilometre cable would bring direct connectivity between Namibia, West Africa, the United Kingdom and the rest of the world with a design capacity of 5.12 terabit � equal to the downloads of 8�000 DVD in 60 seconds or 8 million MP3 files during the same time. According to Telecom Namibia, this would translate into much faster and more robust connectivity of voice, data and video. Frans Ndoroma, Telecom Namibia Managing Director said: �The capacity of WACS is designed such that it has several terabytes that can sustain Namibia for the next 20 years. With this in place we can expect an increase in data speed, improve voice quality and allow video conferencing through affordable and available bandwidth.�

The WACS is expected to be operational mid-year � a year earlier than initially anticipated.
