Cotton futures come down from record highs to contract lows
Published: Sunday, November 19, 2006
DUANE HOWELL
AVALANCHE-JOURNAL

Preparations for the December delivery period dominated some record high activity last week as cotton futures broke to near contract lows after bouncing to five-session highs.

The market for the week through Thursday lost 121 points to 47.88 cents in spot December, 173 points to 51.40 cents in March and 140 points to 57.05 cents in December 2007. March finished on a contract low close, and December fell to a new contract low of 47.60 cents on Monday.

Volume surged on Monday to an all-time high of 61,464 lots, easily eclipsing the previous record of 55,376 lots on May 3. For the year, volume has grown 27 percent.

Open interest hit an all-time high of 186,243 lots on Nov. 6, topping the prior record of 185,674 lots on Oct. 18. With first notice day for December deliveries looming on Wednesday, open interest subsequently declined steadily to 167,320 lots going into Thursday's session.

A big decertification order totaling 103,648 bales and options-related buying helped to propel prices to the highs for the period at 49.20 cents in December and 52.75 cents in March.

Selling intensified after the rally in March - that contract had acquired 62 percent of the open interest as funds and others rolled positions from December - stalled five points shy of a gap left Monday from 52.80 to 53 cents. March struggled to hold at Thursday's low of 51.30 cents above its contract low of 51.25 cents on Oct. 18.

Certificated stocks grew steadily to an all-time high of 817,652 bales, and plunged at the end of the period to 721,344 bales. Open interest in December totaled 25,908 lots, down from 75,886 a week earlier.

A lack of aggressive buying of U.S. cotton on the world market, especially from China, continued to hold export sales and shipments far below the pace required to achieve USDA's latest forecast.

Net U.S. all-cotton export sales during the week ended Nov. 9 were within the range of reported expectations at 177,400 running bales for delivery this season, up from 175,600 bales the week before but down from 242,200 bales during the corresponding week last season.

All-cotton shipments of 150,000 running bales were up from 129,500 bales the prior week but down from 212,700 bales last season.

Current-crop commitments reached 4,422,500 running bales, only 57.5 percent of sales a year ago. Sales trailed year-ago bookings by 3,269,800 running bales and amounted to about 28 percent of the USDA forecast, against nearly 44 percent of final record exports last season.

Cumulative shipments of 1,865,900 running bales, lagging exports a year ago by 1,226,300 bales, amounted to only 60.4 percent of the volume shipped abroad during the corresponding period of 2005-06.

Shipments for the season edged up to nearly 12 percent of the USDA projection, compared with almost 18 percent of final exports a year ago. To achieve the USDA forecast, shipments must average roughly 376,000 running bales a week.

Looking at market internals, the Cotton Exchange reported in its spec-hedge data that speculators boosted their net short position during the week ended Nov. 10, when December options expired, by 7.7 percentage points to 14.9 percent of the open interest.

They increased their outright shorts by 1,966 lots and decreased their outright longs by 10,239 lots, hiking their net shorts by 12,205 lots to 25,573. Trade interests reduced their outright holdings on both sides, trimming shorts by 14,938 lots and longs by 98,659 lots.

On the China watch, still no official confirmation has emerged in Beijing as to whether shipment against the so-called "one-for-one" quotas can be postponed until early next year, according to Cotton Outlook. At present, cotton imported under those quotas must clear customs by Dec. 31.

Many mills bought old-crop Chinese cotton at relatively high prices during August to gain eligibility for the "one-for-one" quotas.

The cotton and textile industries have called on the government to allocate the tariff-rated quota of 894,000 metric tons (4.106 million bales) and any additional quotas together in late December or early January, Cotlook reported.

In a related vein, the National Cotton Council of America and the China Cotton Association signed a "Memorandum of Understanding" promising cooperation between the countries' cotton industries.

Council Chairman Allen Helms and CCA Secretary General Madam Gao Fang signed the document at the Memphis Cotton Museum.

The U.S. industry has renewed its commitment to protect and enhance its reputation for producing high quality cotton, delivering it in a timely manner and honoring contracts, Helms said.

Helms, a Clarkedale, Ark. cotton producer, said Cotton Council International, the NCC's export promotions arm, will continue to play a key role in generating export demand for U.S. cotton in the coming year, including greater activity in China.

During the past year, he said, CCI established an office in Shanghai and launched, with help from U.S. exporters, cotton schools to help representatives of 145 Chinese cotton importing companies get a better grasp of the international cotton market's complexities. He said CCI also teamed with Cotton Inc. to conduct promotions targeting consumers in China's major urban areas.

Helms led a U.S. cotton industry delegation to China in October as part of the China Leadership Exchange program established by the NCA and the CCA. The objective of the cross-country tour was to familiarize the U.S. delegation with market demands and China's views by experiencing the country's development firsthand - from farm to mill.

One of the issues the NCC and CCA have agreed to work together on is the global threat from synthetic fibers, Helms said.

"Man-made fibers still represent the greatest challenge to cotton's profitability worldwide," he said, "and enhanced basic consumer preference for cotton products in developing economies, such as China, represents the largest opportunity for demand growth."
