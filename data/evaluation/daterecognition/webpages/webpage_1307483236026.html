Introduction.

We discuss some current opinions regarding the possible method of formation of the Milky Way Galaxy; whether from the centre outwards by accumulating small clusters of stars, or from the rapid collapse of a single cloud of gas to directly form the Galaxy.  We start with a short description of the early Universe before discussing the two main theories of galaxy formation - the ‘top down’ model and the ‘bottom up’ model - and include a consideration of the relevance of globular clusters to this problem.  We conclude that the ‘bottom up’ model is more likely to be correct one. 

 

The early Universe.

We have observations of the Universe 300 000 years after its formation [1] and of the first star clusters that appeared about 4 to 4.7 thousand million years (1Gyr = one thousand million years.) later [2][3].  It is generally accepted that the Universe formed in a ‘Big Bang’ approximately 13 Gyr ago, and has been expanding away from this event ever since.  (Noting that different workers favour slightly different ages, we select this one as being a typical value.)  This was the beginning of everything; space, matter, energy, time.  It came into existence with a ‘diameter’ of the order of 4 x 10-35 m or less (one ‘Planck length’) and has been expanding ever since.

 

The age is derived from computer simulations of the early Universe combined with observations of the rates of recession of distant galaxies and what we know about the ages of the oldest stars within our Galaxy; those found in globular clusters.  There are a number of competing models; the Inflationary models and the Cosmic Defects models [4], and the more recent 'membrane' model [5]. 

 

Strong evidence for the Big Bang is seen in the Cosmic Microwave Background; radiation equivalent to black-body at a temperature at 2.7K coming to us from all regions of the sky.  This radiation was emitted by gas in the Universe approximately 300 000 years after the Big Bang when the Universe had cooled to about 10 thousand K.  It was then cool enough for protons and electrons to combine and became transparent to radiation; ‘decoupling’ from matter.  The radiation has subsequently been cooled, or red shifted, by the expansion of space to its present temperature.

 

Most Big Bang models of the Universe predict quite specific abundances for the elements formed in the Big Bang itself (cosmic nucleosynthesis), before the abundances were modified by fusion within stars (stellar nucleosynthesis).   By mass, these are approximately; 1H 74%, 4He, 25%, deuterium and 3He each about  0.01%; with lithium and beryllium about 1 x 10-8  % as abundant as hydrogen [6].  No other elements were formed in the Big Bang, so these are probably the chemical building blocks from which the Galaxy was formed.  The question is; ‘How?’

 

Observations of the background radiation by the Cosmic Microwave Background Explorer (COBE) mission detected small but significant spatial fluctuations in this radiation, representing regions where the density of the gas is about 1 part in one hundred and fifty thousand times denser than average.  (Regions of higher than average temperature correspond to regions of higher than average density, and lower temperature correspond to lower density.)  These denser regions would have a slightly stronger gravitational field than other regions, and hence have a greater tendency to contract under their own gravity if conditions of temperature and pressure allowed.  The regions of lower density would ultimately become depleted of matter to form vast, bubble like voids. 

 

None of the competing models of galaxy formation makes any definitive predictions about the sizes of these fluctuations, although they can produce structures that model the large scale structure of the Universe remarkably well. However, the variations that we observe in the cosmic microwave background suggest that the instabilities that began to collapse under their own gravity were smaller than present day galaxies, but they subsequently accreted to form larger clouds which themselves accreted, on up to the scale of clusters of galaxies.  The microwave background comes from gas before it formed stars, but the deepest photograph we have (the Hubble Deep Field) shows galaxies already existing more 8 Gyr years ago. 

 

What happened in the meantime?  We might start by trying to explain the sizes and intensities of the variations in the cosmic microwave background.  These are believed to be dependent upon the density and temperature of matter at the time of decoupling, as well as the amount of certain types of so called ‘dark matter’. 

 

The existence of dark matter had previously been suspected; the mass of the visible material (i.e. matter emitting detectable electromagnetic radiation) in a galaxy is insufficient to account for the longevity of the galaxies.  Our Sun lies about 8 kpc from the galactic centre, and orbits around it with a period of 220 million years.  The orbital period of a star around the centre of a galaxy is determined by the total mass that lies inside its orbit, and by determining the orbital periods of stars throughout a galaxy we can estimate the distribution of mass within that galaxy.  This was first investigated in the 1930's by astronomers Jan Oort and Fritz Zwicky, and they concluded that the total mass required to maintain stable orbits over the probable lifetime of the Galaxy is about 9 times what can be accounted for by all the visible stars, gas and dust [7].  There must also be dark matter between galaxies for clusters of galaxies to be stable.

 

Much of the dark matter within the Galaxy resides in its 160 000 light year diameter spherical halo [8], and a number of possibilities for the nature of this material have been proposed.  Some involve forms of baryonic (i.e. ‘ordinary’ matter composed of protons and neutrons) matter that are simply difficult to detect, such as very faint stars, white and brown dwarfs and black holes.  They are called ‘MACHOS’ – massive compact halo objects.  Some forms have been detected, including low mass stars brown dwarfs [9], but the Hubble Space Telescope has significantly failed to detect red dwarfs in sufficient numbers to make up the short-fall.  

 

MACHOs alone being insufficient, alternative explanations involving totally unknown forms of particle have been proposed.  These would only interact with ordinary matter through the forces of gravity and the weak nuclear force, hence the name ‘WIMPS’ – weakly interacting massive particles [10].  This type of particle is predicted by theories of supersymmetry – theories that attempt to unify all the known forces of nature into one [11].  Neutrinos are the one form of WIMP already known to exist, and are now thought to have a none zero mass[12], but their combined mass is still not enough to explain the observations.

 

MACHOs and WIMPs come in two forms; cold and hot.  Cold dark matter consists of slow moving (‘cold’) objects, such as dead stars or heavy exotic particles.  If dark matter were entirely cold, there would be too many superclusters produced too early on in the early history of the Universe to explain observations.  Hot dark matter consists of fast moving (‘hot’) lightweight, almost undetectable subatomic particles (neutrinos are included in this category).  If all dark matter were hot, this could not produce galaxies early enough in the history of the Universe to account for even the largest superclusters now observed.  A process called ‘free-streaming’ allows hot, fast moving, particles to transfer mass from regions of higher density to regions of lower density; the minimum mass that could survive this would be about 1016 solar masses [13].  Cold dark matter, on the other hand, would not significantly redistribute mass on any scale.  Observations are best explained by assuming a mixture of the two; the Grand Challenge Cosmology Group (the GC3 group) [14] [47], for example, has found that 60% cold dark matter, 30% hot dark matter and 10% ‘ordinary’ matter would produce a universe recognisably like ours.

 

Supercomputer simulations such as those by the GC3 group are proving invaluable to unravelling the formation of galaxies.  For example, they have already enabled us to model their dynamics; it appears that a rotating disc can inherently produce spiral structures within it.  These result from the orbits of individual stars within the galaxy, which are elliptical, precessing and forming regions of greater star density that can appear remarkably like the spiral arms in a galaxy.  Whether or not a central bar will form appears to depend upon the mass of the halo [6].  Simulations also show that some stars can be ejected from the plane of the galactic disc into so called ‘swarming orbits’ within the halo, again as we observe in our Galaxy. 

 

In fact many simulations of individual galaxy formation can produce realistic spirals, but they rely on arbitrary starting conditions that may or may not bear any resemblance to the real Universe.  As yet, there do not appear to be any single simulations that satisfactorily generate both clusters of galaxies and stars within individual galaxies.  This problem is primarily due to limitations in present day computing power.

 

Dark matter needs to be included in any realistic simulation.  Hot dark matter particles move so fast that they are unlikely to be captured by proto-galactic clouds of gas, whereas cold dark matter is moving slowly enough to be gravitationally captured by these low mass clouds.  Whatever dark matter’s form, the pressure of hot gas in a cluster of galaxies is related to the distribution of dark matter, enabling dark matter’s distribution to be estimated from x-ray observations of the hot gas [15]  Chandra has made detailed observations of the cluster EMSS 13588+6245 at 4 thousand million light years distance, suggesting that about 80% of the cluster is dark matter.  This observation significantly places an upper limit to the volume of space occupied by dark matter within a galaxy of about 150 000 light years from the galaxy’s centre; rotational velocity curves only place a lower limit on its distribution.  Models based on cold dark matter scenarios can generate some of the other properties of individual galaxies as well, such as the Tully-Fisher and the Faber-Jackson relationships [16].

 

The recent possible rediscovery of Einstein’s cosmological constant [17] has provided another possible source of missing ‘mass’. The vacuum of space itself acts like a gravitating body, and the MAXIMA 1 and BOOMERANG missions suggest a ratio of normal matter: dark matter: vacuum energy  of 5:30:65 [18].  The density of this ‘mass’ is miniscule; a few joules per cubic kilometre [19], or ‘a few’ x 10^-17 kg per cubic kilometre.  However, it is sufficient for it to act like an additional gravitational force over relatively short distances; its repulsive force only becoming significant over distances of the order of the size of the observable Universe.

 

However these fluctuations came into being, the key question is the mass of self gravitating clouds of gas; will they be massive enough to form an entire spiral galaxy ‘in one go’, as it were, the so called ‘Top Down’ model, or will they form much smaller groups of stars that will only later accrete to form the larger spiral galaxies, the ‘Bottom Up’ model? 

 

The ‘top down’ model of galaxy formation.

This model, properly called the ‘monolithic collapse’ model, was proposed by Eggen, Lynden-Bell and Sandage in 1962 [20], and suggests that our Galaxy was formed from a single cloud of hydrogen and helium of the mass of our Galaxy, of essentially the size of the present halo (say 100 kpc in diameter).  Contraction would lead to heating due to the conversion of gravitational potential energy to thermal energy.  In these early stages the gas was transparent and could loose this energy by radiative cooling [21]. 

 

Within the cloud there would be turbulent eddies, which would lead to localised increases in density.  As the whole cloud contracted, these eddies would become smaller and denser until they had sufficient density (the Jeans density [6]) to contract until they fragmented to form individual stars.  These clouds, possibly a couple of hundred parsecs across, had the mass of typical globular clusters.  Since they formed when the cloud was spherical, they would themselves be distributed with spherical symmetry about the centre of mass of the cloud, and be in orbit around it.  Significantly, this is just how present day globular clusters move around the Galaxy. 

 

These stars, because they were formed from the material from the Big Bang, would be population III stars, containing virtually no ‘metals’.  The more massive would end their lives after a few tens of millions of years, becoming supernovae and ejecting newly formed metals into the space between the stars.  Due to the speed at which this material is ejected by supernovae, and the relatively low gravitational field of each globular cluster, most this material would escape from the cluster (but not quite all of it, see [22]).  As a result, although some material would form the population II stars we see in globulars, there would be no material available for the continued formation of younger, metal rich population I stars. 

 

Gas that had not already condensed to form globular clusters would continue to condense and contract.  The probability that the cloud would have zero angular momentum is vanishingly small due to turbulence. (Non zero angular momentum is actually very difficult to account for [4].)  The dynamics of contracting, rotating clouds is such that they form a flattened disc rotating around the resultant angular momentum vector, motions in other directions being damped out [21].  This leads to the densest part of the Galaxy being in the galactic plane, where most of the interstellar gas and dust finally accumulates, and the present day regions of star formation are found.  As stars form, they maintain the orbit of the part of the cloud from which they formed.  The central bulge could have formed from gas that contracted so fast that it did not have time to settle into the disc before forming stars.

 

This monolithic collapse theory was particularly popular in the 1970’s and 1980’s.  It correctly predicted that the disc to bulge ratio in galaxies along the Hubble sequence was related to the rate of star formation in the protogalactic cloud - rapid in Sa, less so in type Sb, and slower still in type Sc. It also correctly predicted the observed rates of rotation along this sequence.  It is still the most successful model at generating structures on the scale of superclusters of galaxies, and it also predicts the observed changes of rate of star formation throughout the history of the Universe [23]. 

 

There are, however, flaws with this model that some (including the present writer) consider to be insurmountable for the top down model to accommodate.  For example, it predicts that the collapse of a protogalactic gas cloud would have been relatively rapid, leading to the globular clusters associated with a galaxy all having a similar age (to within two Gyr or so [6]) rather than the observed spread of 5 Gyr in age.  Furthermore, the initial burst of star formation in the early Universe would have been far higher than that observed in the Hubble Deep Fields [13]. 

 

These problems have been discovered during the past two decades or so due to the increasing size of ground based telescopes, the Hubble Space Telescope, improvements to the sensitivity of detectors and the speed of computers.   These allow us to probe much further back into the early history of the Universe, much closer to the period when galaxies first formed.  A new model was required. 

 

The ‘bottom up’ model of galaxy formation.

Rather than forming from one, galactic mass, cloud over a relatively short period, the Galaxy could have coalesced from a number of pre-existing clusters of already formed stars, hence its formal name of the ‘accretion/interaction’ model.   Each of these clusters would have formed at any time within the first five Gyr of the start of star formation.  This would allow each to form as and when suitable conditions of temperature and pressure within a localised volume of gas occurred [6].  Due to the random nature of turbulence, this would occur in different places at different times.

 

Calculations by Sir James Jeans show that just such condensations could occur anytime after 300 000 years after the Big Bang [6].  As we have seen, suitable conditions of density and pressure can lead to regions that have a mass of about one million solar masses (the Jeans Mass) contracting and fragmenting to form clusters of stars of the mass of a large globular cluster.  These could then coalesce to form galaxies, and these could collect together to from clusters of galaxies.

 

Perhaps the strongest support for this model comes from the Hubble Deep Fields.  These appears to show just such objects shortly after they have formed; so-called Searle-Zinn fragments.  Some of these would then collide and merge to form the galaxies we see today. The size and mass of these condensing regions are consistent with the almost featureless early Universe (from COBE), and far from featureless Universe only 4 Gyr or so after the Big Bang as shown in the Hubble Deep Fields [24].  This shows numerous small clusters, or faint blue galaxies, 600 to 900 parsecs across, that are blue due to the intense star formation that is taking place within them [8].  Further support comes from recent observations of gravitationally lensed objects which are possibly galactic building blocks one two hundredth the mass of the Galaxy [25].  However, we still have difficulties explaining the central bulge due to problems with the system’s dynamics.

 

The above problem notwithstanding, considerations of conservation of mass, energy and momentum support this theory.  In a contracting, rotating cloud of gas, two regions of gas trying to pass through each other collide, due to the high number density of particles in the cloud.  They would redistribute energy and momentum in such a manner that the gas tends to collect in the equatorial plane of the soon to be formed galaxy.  Any stars which form during this process are very unlikely to collide with other stars (the distances between them are too great) and they will remain in the more or less inclined orbit in which they were formed.  This not only accounts for the motion of stars within a globular cluster, not formed from the merger of smaller objects, but also the distribution of stars within the halo and disc of a spiral galaxy such as our own, which are formed as the result of numerous collisions and mergers between Searle-Zinn fragments and the proto galaxy [26].

 

Collisionless groups of stars tend to merge due to an effect called dynamical friction.  Due to gravitational effects, a star passing through a group of other stars attracts them towards its path after it has passed along, so those behind it tend to be closer to it than those still in front of it and exert a net gravitational attraction opposing its motion.  This dynamical friction affects all the stars in both galaxies, with the result that the galaxies do not, in fact, pass through each other unscathed.  Energy is redistributed among the individual stars and the galaxies may very well alter their velocities relative to each other, swing round and orbit each other, or even eventually merge.  The actual outcome of the encounter is a function of the ratio of their kinetic energy to their gravitational potential energy with respect to each other.  If the kinetic energy is greater than the potential energy, then the galaxies will indeed pass through each other and continue on their way.  If, on the other hand, the kinetic energy is less than the potential energy, then they will eventually merge.  Because the gravitational potential energy is itself  a function of the distance between the two galaxies, the distance of their closest approach is a deciding factor.  The dynamics of this type of interaction is such that the resulting galaxy will be more spherically symmetrical than the colliding galaxies [27] [28].

 

Although generally collisionless, in the dense central regions of the Galactic core there are many hundreds of stars per cubic parsec, and collisions would occur.  The merger of a sufficient number of stars may lead to the formation of the central, super massive black hole of 2 to 3 million solar masses that is believed to lie at the very centre of our Galaxy.  Evidence of similar black holes in other galaxies exists in the form of their central velocity curves and, in a few instances, direct observation of a torus, or ‘accretion disc’, surrounding the central black hole.  It had once been thought that super massive black holes may have been the original cores around which galaxies subsequently formed, but such black holes are not produced in simulations and are now generally believed to form after their host galaxy.

 

In many computer simulations, matter in the early Universe condenses into filaments and sheets that intersect in ‘knots’ and surround large, bubble like voids.  The exact structure depends on the exact ratio of hot dark matter to cold dark matter.  Galaxies tend to ‘slide down’ the filaments and to form clusters of galaxies at the knots; strikingly similar to what we observe.  Some galaxies will inevitably collide, but collisions between whole galaxies, spiral or otherwise, cannot produce spiral galaxies, due to the intense burst of star formation induced by collisions between the interstellar gas and dust.  This rapidly diminishes the supply of material to form new supergiant stars (recall that it is massive, short lived O and A stars that define spiral arms).  This explains the observation that large, dense clusters of galaxies – where numerous collisions would be expected - are dominated by large elliptical galaxies, whereas lower density clusters such as our own tend to have spirals as their dominant galaxies. 

 

This is consistent with the modern recognition that the early Universe was inhomogeneous on many scales, such as the scale of Searle-Zinn fragments, galaxies and clusters of galaxies.  The dominant scale was that of the Searle-Zinn fragments, hence their early formation, but the result of their subsequent mergers has meant that galaxies are now the smallest surviving units on the whole – where galaxies of any type have collided we get larger, elliptical, galaxies.  Crucially, collisions between a spiral galaxy and a small fragment or a dwarf galaxy do not disrupt the spiral.  Indeed, our Galaxy is presently undergoing a collision with the Sagittarius dwarf.

 

Dark matter is ‘collisionless’, that is, not able to redistribute energy and momentum within itself as it contracts.  Hence it would not contract to a very small region but, rather, form an extended halo.  This is exactly what is predicted from the velocity curve of the visible part of the Galaxy which, being collisional baryonic matter, was able to loose energy by collisions and settle down into the galactic plane. 

 

There are some workers who argue that mergers can actually generate spiral arms [8], but the resulting spirals would not usually be symmetrical.  Collisions could not explain the formation of symmetrical spirals in the numbers we observe.  Merger therefore probably generates elliptical or spherical galaxies from the collision of elliptical, spiral or irregular galaxies.  Globular clusters are themselves collisionless objects; exactly the same process produces elliptical or spherically symmetric globular clusters from the merger of smaller star clusters of any shape.  As we shall see, globular clusters are invaluable clues regarding the formation of the Galaxy.

 

There are several serious problems with the bottom up model.  For example, it has difficulty accounting for the formation of structure on the scale of superclusters of galaxies within the time allowed since the formation of the Universe, and not enough galaxies are actually seen in the process of merging. 

 

Observations of the very early Universe, say more than 7 Gyr ago, we find that galaxies then are clustered to a similar extent to those in the more recent Universe – no more and no less.  How this came about so early on is a major problem that has not been satisfactorily solved.  One possible solution is the phenomenon of ‘biasing’, whereby dark matter has a different spatial distribution to visible matter.  This occurs in the Galaxy today, with most visible matter in the disc and nucleus, while most dark matter resides in the more extensive halo.  In the early Universe biasing could have lead to the visible clusters of galaxies actually having less mass than we would expect, while more dark matter resided in the apparent voids between.  This in turn could lead to the observed lack of ‘mega-clusters’ that are otherwise predicted.  This process may have been significant in the formation of the Galaxy. 

 

The dearth of observed merging galaxies could be explained if we assume that many of the stars in the original faint blue galaxies went supernova, leaving behind only faint, unobservable red stars.  Insufficiently massive  by themselves to merge to form galaxies, they were held together by previously unexpected large amounts of dark matter and did eventually merge.  This seems, to me, to be a tenuous solution to a problem that may not even exist, and illustrates just how much we have yet to discover regarding the formation of our Galaxy.

 

Work by the Grand Challenge Cosmology Consortium (GC3) [29] [30] is now solving some significant problems with this model.  They run supercomputer simulations that successfully model the structure of the early Universe down to the scale of clusters of galaxies.  Although these particular simulations do not resolve detail smaller than this, the results of other simulations run by the group tend to favour the bottom up model [14].

 

Globular clusters and the formation of the Galaxy.

Globular clusters contain the oldest stars in our galactic system [31], and have been described as ‘valuable fossils’ [32].  The formation of globular clusters is itself still open to some question, they may be formed at the same time as their parent galaxy, or as the result of the merger of two older galaxies; whenever conditions of mass, temperature and density in a region of the interstellar gas clouds become suitable [6].  The consensus in the astronomical community appears to be that we have a better understanding of the formation of globular clusters than we do of galaxies, and they can therefore act as constraints on theories of galaxy formation.

 

The mass function of a group of objects is essentially a plot of mass against the number of objects within each mass range.  The mass functions of globular clusters and open clusters, as well as giant molecular clouds, appear to follow similar power laws. This suggests that it is the masses of the clouds that determines the type of cluster that will be formed; the actual processes being identical for open and globular clusters.  This is useful; the Galaxy contains many easily observed molecular clouds and open clusters of all ages - what we learn about them can be extrapolated usefully to globular clusters.

 

The two main theories of galaxy formation make different predictions about halo stars; the top down model predicts they should all have similar ages and metallicities, while the bottom up model predicts a much wider range of ages and metallicities [33].  The ages of individual stars are very difficult to determine, but the age of a globular cluster can be determined from its turn off point on the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.  It appears that the globulars in our halo formed over a period of several Gyr – possibly as many as 5 Gyr [34] - from massive star forming regions within protogalaxies that subsequently formed dwarf galaxies.  The dwarfs subsequently merged to form larger galaxies, at an arbitrary time after their own formation, adding their globular clusters to those already part of the new system.  The globulars in a system therefore need not all be of the same age.  Indeed, the Sagittarius dwarf is presently adding its own 4 globular clusters (M54, Arp2, Ter7 and Ter8)  to our halo, and it may be representative of the building blocks of our Galaxy.  This will possibly prove to be the decisive evidence for the bottom up theory.

The old age of all globular clusters is not universally accepted, however.  Indeed, some globular clusters are possibly still in the process of formation in ‘super star clusters’ [35].  Merging galaxies produce increased pressures in the interstellar medium of the merging objects that could lead to the formation of clusters of stars of the mass and radius of typical globular clusters [36] [37] dating from the merger.   One group claims to have made observations of a galaxy at the very small distance of 12 million light years (dwarf galaxy NGC 5253 in Centaurus) where globular cluster formation is still ongoing [38].  It may be less than one million years old.  Another example of a 'new' globular cluster may be found at the centre of the blue, compact, nucleated dwarf galaxy NGC1705.  This is believed to be dominated by dark matter which has produced gravitational conditions leading to a large concentration of gas at its centre.  This in turn has formed a massive (>106 solar masses) young globular cluster at it centre.  The significance of this to the formation of our own Galaxy is that NGC1705 is not unique, but is similar to the numerous blue, compact, nucleated dwarf galaxies seen in the HDF which may be a close equivalent of the objects that merged to form our halo.

An added complication is the ages of globular clusters relative to their associated galaxy.  Elliptical galaxies are believed to have been formed by the merger of other ellipticals or spiral galaxies, the process leading to the formation of many clusters younger than the galaxies.  This distinguishes them from globulars associated with spirals, which are older than their parent galaxy, having been formed at the time of the individual constituent protogalactic fragments. 

 

Globular clusters; support for top down or bottom up?

The distribution of globular clusters is consistent with monolithic collapse, but their ages are not.  A galaxy sized collapsing cloud would start almost spherical, and the first stellar condensations would be throughout this volume.  As the remaining gas and dust continued to collapse and gravitate towards the new ‘equatorial plane’, younger clusters would be formed in an ever more disc shaped volume.  Observations show that those clusters with lower metallicities (i.e. the older clusters) do indeed tend to have highly eccentric orbits that take them well out into the halo, but they also have a small range of ages (about 1Gyr), while those with higher metallicities mostly follow orbits within the plane of the Galaxy, while having a wider spread of ages, 3 to 5 Gyr or so.  The top down model predicts globular clusters to be all formed over the shorter time scale. 

 

The spread of ages observed in globulars overwhelmingly supports the bottom up model rather than the top down model.  Mergers can happen at any time, and each merger is likely to produce its own generation of globular clusters.  Thus the bottom up model predicts a wide spread in cluster ages, much as is observed.  The monolithic collapse of Eggen, Lynden-Bell and Sandage [21], on the other hand, with its free-fall collapse of the protogalactic cloud, would have produces globular clusters over a much shorter period, 1 Gyr or so.  It should be noted that the spread of ages is observed over clusters of all metallicities, and is much greater than any realistic observational errors. 

 

Supercomputer simulations of dark matter dominated models strongly suggest a bottom up scenario.  A property called ‘scale length’ is related to gas density, velocity and motion within a cloud and determines its likelihood of forming gravitationally unstable ‘clumps’ within which star formation may occur.  In the case of the Galaxy it is of a size similar  to the observed spacing of the spiral arms, to which star formation is now restricted.  It can also explain other gross properties of the Hubble classification of galaxies; for example, the morphologies of the different classes of spirals, the relevance of gas content to whether a galaxy will be a dwarf elliptical or a dwarf irregular, and the distribution of metal rich globular clusters in the disc of the Galaxy.

 

Calculated density fluctuations are of the correct size (i.e. the Jeans length) to produce globular clusters from their collapse, while larger structures form from their subsequent merger.  In this scenario, the problem of angular momentum can be more easily explained.  The first protogalactic condensations in the gas would have little angular momentum about their own centres of mass, so the first clusters to condense would tend to be either spherical or irregular.  As these protogalactic fragments continued to move relative to each other, tidal forces from passing fragments would have an opportunity to impart rotation due to torque.  Similarly, off-centre collisions between clusters could also produce rotation.  The net rotational angular momentum of  a growing galaxy could therefore be increased over time; the increasing rate of rotation would lead to gas and dust settling down to lie in the plane perpendicular to the axis of rotation and star formation would be increasingly confined to this new galactic plane.  With the addition of the products of stellar nucleosynthesis as the more massive stars became supernovae, we have a plausible explanation for the observed distribution of ages and metallicities among the stars of our Galaxy, as well as the shapes of globular clusters and the flattened disc of the Galaxy itself.

 

Some globular clusters in merging systems predate the mergers, so the dates of mergers are only usefully constrained by the ages of associated globular clusters if those clusters are young.  If the clusters are old, then the merger could have taken place essentially at any time.  Globular clusters eventually ‘evaporate’, by losing stars due to redistribution of momentum within the cluster, over a timescale that depends upon their masses.  The oldest surviving globular clusters are just massive enough to have survived for the age of the Galaxy, those with less mass have already evaporated. 

 

The number of globular clusters in a galaxy is a function of the number of mergers that went to build it up.  Mergers result in starbursts which can produce new globular clusters [46], leading to the observed high numbers of globulars associated with large elliptical galaxies compared to the smaller numbers associated with spirals.

 

Once the galactic disc has formed, it is a relatively stable system that can withstand disruption from less massive merging objects.  Indeed, the globular cluster M54 is believed to lie at the centre of the colliding Sagittarius dwarf. (Their common radial velocities and common centre are thought to be more than coincidence.)  The presence of a globular like cluster at the centre of a dwarf galaxy makes the Sagittarius dwarf a strong contender for being a typical Searle-Zinn fragment merging with the Galaxy.  Rather than the massive Galaxy being disrupted, it is the less strongly bound fragment that does not survive the encounter, eventually dispersing within the galactic halo [39].

The importance of globular clusters to understanding the formation of the Galaxy is evident, and work is currently underway to improve our understanding of globular clusters by investigating globulars around nearby galaxies such as M31 using the Hubble Space Telescope and the Keck 10 metre telescopes [40]. 

 

Conclusion.

This brief analysis suggests that the current evidence – both theoretical and observational – strongly supports the bottom up model (see, for example, [4] [13] [41]).  For example, elliptical galaxies can be explained by the mergers of other galaxies, and globular clusters can be formed in the resulting starbursts [32], explaining both their ages and numbers.  My conclusion is that the ages of globular clusters and the structures seen in the Hubble Deep Fields will prove to be wholly inconsistent with any other conclusion, although some authorities believe otherwise [21].  There may, of course, be an as yet wholly unsuspected mechanism at work.

 

This historical displacement of the top down model by the bottom up model appears to have been necessitated by improvements in technology allowing us to observe ever earlier stages in the evolution of the Universe.  Glimpses such as the Hubble Deep Fields show that early galaxies contain what appear to be multiple nuclei.  In the case of nearby multiple-nuclei galaxies, such as NGC 1275 these are interpreted as being the result of mergers between galaxies [42].  The top down model could explain many observations of its day, but is unable to explain the more detailed observations made with ever advancing technology - it has probably served its purpose. 

 

Work is currently underway or planned that will test this (not unanimous) conclusion with stronger observational and theoretical evidence.  NASA’s Microwave Anisotropy Probe, MAP [43], was recently placed on station (October 1, 2001).  It is designed to improve the resolution of the observations made by COBE of the microwave background, and should help to decide between the inflation and the topological defects models of the microwave background fluctuations.  They (NASA) imply that they expect to confirm the bottom up model suggested by the Hubble Deep Field.  Hubble’s replacement, the Next Generation Space Telescope, will contribute by enabling more detailed observations of stars in galactic halos to be made.

 

Many early galaxies may be obscured by dust, or red shifted into the infrared region of the spectrum.  Work beginning with the Infrared Spectrometer And Array Camera (ISAAC) on the 8.2 metre ANTU telescope is designed to investigate these further [3].  Newer theories of galactic formation, such as formation caused by lobes emanating from radio galaxies compressing material and inducing it to form more galaxies [44] are also worthy of further study.  This is based on the observation that the number of radio galaxies was about 1000 times higher about 10 to 12 Gyr ago than the present. 

 

A better understanding of how globular clusters are themselves formed is essential.  As time on ever larger telescopes becomes available, large scale spectroscopic surveys of extragalactic globular clusters, better knowledge of  their radii and luminosity functions, as well as improved computer simulations of their formation, will undoubtedly lead to progress.

 

The early Universe contained fluctuations in density of the primeval hydrogen, helium and dark matter that had a range of scales, from the size of clusters of galaxies down to the size of fractions of a galaxy [45].  Although all the clouds of gas in the supercluster sized volumes of space were gravitationally bound to each, it was smaller regions within these clouds that first condensed to form stars, forming globular clusters and dwarf galaxies.  These fragments then continued to collide and merge to form the larger galaxies that we see today, including our own [27].
