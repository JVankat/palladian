Community Group Conferencing

Using restorative justice, the Community Group Conferencing (CGC) program provides a voluntary opportunity for youth who have committed an offense to deal with their charge in a way that is meaningful to themselves, the victims, their family, and the community, by repairing the harm done by their actions. The young offender, his/her parents, and the victim(s), meet with the facilitators to discuss the specific crime, typically vandalism, theft, burglary or graffiti,. The parties describe the circumstances and decisions leading up to the incident, and the resulting impact. With the help of the facilitators, the victim and the offender develop an agreement to repair the harm experienced by the victim and others impacted by the offense. These sessions are scheduled at the convenience of facilitators and participants.

This pilot program between VORP and The Juvenile Diversion Program of the Denver District Attorneys Office emphasizes repairing the harm caused by criminal behavior. It is a way to make amends, and it is best accomplished through a conversation that includes all those involved.

The environment for the CGC meeting is a safe place where the mediators help to facilitate the conversation, ensuring confidentiality and a respectful environment.

 The victim is satisfied with the results of the CGC session

 90% of youth referred to CGC attend and complete the program

 To increase by 20% the awareness in participating youth that their actions have harmed the community

Definition of long-term success

The goal of this program is to reduce repeat juvenile crime in Denver.

 Six month recidivism rate among participating youth is < 15%

 Twelve month recidivism rate among participating youth is <20%

Program success monitored by

Progress is measured using victim satisfaction surveys, pre-program and post-program surveys for offenders; and court records for incidents of recidivism.

Examples or evidence of program success

Of those youth referred to CGC, 100% have completed the program.

Additional progress is not yet available for this pilot program.

RESTORE

The RESTORE Program is a restorative justice diversion program for first-time shoplifters referred from Denver Municipal Juvenile Court and the Denver Safe City Program, developed to reduce delinquency and recidivism and increase a sense of community and accountability in juvenile offenders. It is designed to raise the awareness of juvenile shoplifting offenders regarding the far-reaching impact of their crime on their community, their families, and themselves.

RESTORE meets the 3rd Saturday of each month, with an orientation on the prior Wednesday evening. The youth and a parent attend two RESTORE sessions, first receiving information about the harm shoplifting has on the community from a panel of those impacted, including a police officer, a merchant representative, and prior shoplifters. This is followed by facilitated dialogue circles with other shoplifters and their parents. The youth create a contract for family and community service, and other ways to repair the harm done by their shoplifting. Returning the following month, the youth must demonstrate in another dialogue session how they have completed their contract.

Examples or evidence of program success

Approximately 70% of those youth who are referred to RESTORE satisfactorily complete the program.

As evidenced by Denver County law enforcement records, of those youth who satisfactorily completed the RESTORE program, 16.5 % percent have had law enforcement contacts, re-arrests, or court filings within six-months of program completion. The recidivism rate for youth who did not complete the program was 29.2%, or almost twice as high as that of youth who did complete the program. This analysis was completed in June 2010, and based on a total of 236 youth who were referred to RESTORE on or after July 1, 2008, completing or dropping out of the program by the end of 2009.

According to an analysis of data from the pre- and post- program surveys of RESTORE participants, the program has had an impact , on their awareness of shoplifting consequences and connectedness with adults. In the pre-test, 64 % responded that they agree or strongly agree in response to I care what adults in my community think about me. This increased to 88% in the post-survey. The changes in the awareness of shoplifting consequences, as shown by the percent responding agree or strongly agree:

 Shoplifting hurt my community
Pre: 45% Post: 95%

 Shoplifting hurt the store I stole from
Pre: 60% Post: 91%

 Shoplifting hurt my family
Pre: 85% Post: 99%

 Shoplifting hurt me
Pre: 80% Post: 96%
