Enbridge disaster and pipeline safety probed

By Joseph Picard | September 17, 2010 12:34 PM EDT

Some time in the evening of Sunday July 25, 2010, a section of a 40-year-old, 30-inch carbon steel pipeline, located in a swampy, wooded area about a mile south of Marshall, Michigan, split open and began spewing crude oil into Talmadge Creek and, via the creek, into the Kalamazoo River.

The oil, approximately 1 million gallons, traveled 30 miles downstream through the towns of Marshall, Ceresco, Battle Creek and Augusta, turning the waterway black, soaking and killing wildlife, overflowing the river's banks to defile yards and foliage, and shocking and sickening residents, many of whom were forced to evacuate. The oil was 80 miles from Lake Michigan when it was finally halted.

The pipeline, part of the longest oil pipeline in the world, is owned and operated by Enbridge Energy, a Canadian firm. The control room for the line running through Michigan, Line 6B, was in Edmonton, Alberta.

At 5:58 p.m. Sunday, an alarm sounded in the Edmonton control room and operators noted a drop in pressure in Line 6B.

Alarms sounded regularly in the control room throughout the evening, the night and into the next day, while operators kept trying to troubleshoot the undetermined problem from Edmonton.  

Meanwhile, residents living near the creek began calling 911, reporting a strong odor. They guessed it was natural gas.

It was about 11:15 a.m. Monday when an employee of a natural gas company, responding to consumers' calls, phoned Enbridge to let them know that their pipeline was ruptured and there was oil in the river.

Enbridge sent employees to the spot and, nearly two hours after their arrival there, reported the spill to federal authorities.

"Enbridge implemented a swift and expansive response to the Line 6B rupture," Patrick Daniel, the company's CEO, told the House Transportation and Infrastructure Committee Wednesday.

Not everyone agreed.

"Enbridge confirmed the pipeline rupture on Monday, July 26, at 11:45 a.m.," said John Porcari of the federal Department of Transportation. "PHMSA and other federal officials were not made aware of the incident until 1:33 p.m., the time when Enbridge notified the National Response Center."

"I am deeply troubled by Enbridge's detection of and response to this oil spill," Porcari said.

The Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration is an agency of the Department of Transportation.

"Just 10 days before, an executive of Enbridge Energy Partners testified before this very committee on its integrity management system and stated that its control room could detect the smallest of leaks. As we learned, it failed," said U.S. Rep. Mark Schauer, D-MI, who is a member of the committee and whose district includes the affected area.

Schauer noted that Enbridge had been operating the line at reduced pressure for a year while deciding how to deal with known defects in the line.

"As a result of in-line inspections since 2007, Enbridge knew of 390 defects but only saw fit to repair 61 of them - leaving 329 unrepaired," Schauer said.

"Isn't it Enbridge's responsibility to make sure that its pipeline is safe and that people won't be injured and the environment won't be impacted?" he said.

U.S. Rep. James Oberstar, D-MN, and committee chairman, pointed to federal as well as corporate officials.

"We also know that Enbridge knew about hundreds of defects in the line, and we know that PHMSA was made aware of them and failed to do anything to address Enbridge's inaction. That is not a culture of safety," Oberstar said.

The leak has been contained. The Environmental Protection Agency said it was satisfied that Enbridge has removed the oil from the river, but promised to stay on the company until it finishes the job, as environmental cleanup continues long the banks of the river. The company is offering to buy homes where the property has been damaged at pre-leak market values. The company has also agreed to compensate affected parties.

CEO Daniel said the company is making an honest, transparent effort to make everything right and whole.

Again, not everyone agreed with the CEO.

Several residents testified to the committee about not only the damage to their homes from the oil and their health from exposure to benzene and other toxic hydrocarbons, but also about pressure from Enbridge to sign release forms before receiving help from the company.

"A corporation that is running around urging residents under duress to sign liability releases for reimbursement of hotel and food expenses as a result of the evacuation, air purifiers (which do not even address the health impact of inhaling benzene or volatile organic compounds), and air conditioners does not have that focus on its responsibilities for lapses in safety," Oberstar said.

"Enbridge also had citizens sign waivers releasing all of their medical history to the company in return for medical treatment," Schauer said. "This is outrageous and is a clear HIPAA violation. People should not have to sign away their rights to receive medical treatment or be reimbursed for a legitimate claim."

At the urging of Oberstar and Schauer, the Department of Justice has begun an investigation into these allegations against the company.

Federal agencies are also continuing investigations of the company's initial response to the leak and the condition of the pipeline.

U.S Rep. Corrine Brown, D-FL, connected the safety concerns of the Enbridge pipeline with the explosion of the Pacific Gas & Electric natural gas pipeline in San Bruno, CA, earlier this month that leveled a suburban neighborhood and killed four people.

"This is a very timely hearing, as we are unfortunately experiencing a high level of pipeline accidents that have caused significant death and destruction," Brown said. "Our Railroad and Pipelines Subcommittee has held a series of hearings concerning pipeline safety and has found significant problems with reporting and inspections, as well as an unhealthy relationship between the pipeline industry and the agencies regulating them."

DOT Secretary Ray LaHood has presented Congress with the administration's legislative initiative for the reauthorization of the department's pipeline safety program, designed to fill regulatory gaps and strengthen enforcement. The proposal:

  • increases the maximum administrative civil penalties for the most serious types of violations from $100,000 per day/$1 million for a series of violations to $250,000 per day/$2.5 million for a series of violations;
  • adds 40 additional inspection and enforcement personnel over four years;
  • closes regulatory gaps on "gathering" pipelines - lines that collect products from processors or refiners and delivers to transmission pipelines - by eliminating current statutory exemptions for gas and hazardous liquid gathering lines;
  • requires a review of the effectiveness of current rules that apply risk management requirements to pipelines in high consequence areas (HCAs) to determine whether these requirements should be applied to entire pipelines; and
  • increases the data available to the pipeline program to minimize risks.

"As the recent oil pipeline failure near Marshall, Michigan, has shown, the Department needs stronger authority to ensure the continued safety and reliability of our nation's pipeline network," LaHood said.

The Transportation and Infrastructure Committee has decided the issue warrants a closr, more detailed look and has scheduled a hearing on the DOT's "Inspection and Enforcement of Gas and Hazardous Liquid Pipelines" for Oct. 6.
