The case against Fitts’ Law

4th
November 2010
2 Comments

Human Computer Interaction (HCI) is a complex topic that involves many different sciences: Biology, Neurobiology, Psychology and many more. And since research is often based on empirical studies and tests with sometimes rather small groups of people, HCI is often seen as a very “soft” science in contrast to “hard” sciences like Mathematics for example. But there are a few rules and theories in the HCI sector that have been studied extensively and that have defined the discussion for quite a while now, one of the is Fitts’ Law.

In summary, Fitts’ Law talks about how hard it is to reach certain points on a screen with a pointer (I simplify it somewhat here): For example the corners of a screen are really easy to hit because when you move the cursor further it stays in the corner; similar things are true for the sides of the screen. The idea is that if you want to hit a spot in the middle of the screen you have to move the pointer really precisely, if you want to hit the upper left corner that’s trivial, just move the pointer very strongly and it will “stick” in the corner.

Fitts’ Law is one of the base ideas behind Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines  (HIG) and similar rulesets, it’s for example the reason why OSX has only one global menubar at the top of the screen: Fitt’s law states that menubars somewhere in the middle of the screen are a lot harder to “hit” than the one at the top (there are other reasons as well, as for example the idea that you don’t have to search for the menu because it’s always at the same place [Principle of the least surprise]). This approach is often cited as being superior to the menu placement that most non-OSX GUI Toolkits (GTK, Qt, WindowsToolkit, etc) use. But does does Fitts’ Law really apply that much anymore?

I have an extra 24″ monitor here at home that I don’t use at the moment so I decided I’d take it with me to work so I have more screen real estate there, where I spend more than just a few hours in front of screens. Thinking whether and how it would change my workspace I came to a few conclusions:

  1. On a big screen with many pixels the borders are so far away from where the pointer might be that the benefit I get from being able to hit them easier is easily countered by the huge distance I have to move the pointer, this is made worse by adding more monitors (which many people have nowadays).
  2. On big screens the corners are so far away from the center of the screen where the stuff I care about is that I have to move my head to see them. While those areas might be easier to hit, making me look away from the area I am actively working in/using is distracting and stupid.

Fitt’s Law seems to only really hold water for small screens and resolutions (the theory was created in the 1950s so that’s not a huge surprise, 24″ screens were not all too common back then ) but as soon as resolutions and the amount of monitors grow it falls apart quickly.

Now this doesn’t mean that menubars within every window are suddenly great, Fitts’ observations on how hard hitting a target in the middle of the screen is, are still valid. I think we have to start thinking about different ways to interact with our windows, maybe something like “menu via GNOME-Do“? What do you think?

  • I have a 22″@1680×1050 and a 26″@1920×1200 monitor.

    If I use the keyboard I try to do everything with the keyboard and then something like gnome-do is really good.

    If I use the mouse I try to do everything with the mouse and then …. I realise that with those two big old monitors I have to move my 400dpi mouse a lot anyway (for all tasks). Logical next step for people that use the mouse a lot (and unlike me don’t have huge mousepad) is to set a higher sensitivity for the mouse. The invention of bigger monitors provoke that and that fact makes Fitts’ Law even more important as it gets harder to hit a small button with a high sensitivity (especially with skipping = low dpi but high sens).

    I recently tested gnome-shell on my gentoo box and it’s a good example — one main button “Activities” in the top left corner and if you move the mouse too far (Fitts’ Law style) the Activities menu opens automatically. Good job in usability engineering!

    I also hate the toolkit straightjacket that doesn’t allow the FOSS community to have global menu bars, Mac style. No matter how big my screens are I can’t help but admire how Apple saves desktop space AND supports fitts’ law and least surprise.
