    Tiger, Paddy in odd position of proving they are worthy

     
    By Steve Elling
    CBSSports.com Senior Writer
    Sep. 29, 2010Tell Steve your opinion!

    NEWPORT, Wales -- The course was shrouded in thick gray clouds and a persistent, misty rain as busloads of hearty fans rolled into Celtic Manor to watch the Ryder Cup teams practice early Wednesday morning. Foggy or otherwise, on a green hillside overlooking the sprawling facility, a familiar U.K. name was being erected in huge capital letters, sort of like the fabled Hollywood sign that's often visible from the smoggy L.A. Basin.

    These letters were far more obvious, despite the wet haze: Paddy Power.

    No, it was not intended as a pep talk, or even some sort of inspirational prediction, for a certain the three-time major champion from Ireland, though he could probably use one. It's the name of a British bookmaker who has never been shy about spending money to make money via cheeky publicity stunts.

    If a few fans confused the message, it was perfectly understandable. Because for Padraig Harrington, not to mention fellow marked man Tiger Woods, their addition to their teams is akin to taking a flier bet, if not going for broke.

    The two stars, each selected as a wild-card pick after failing to make their teams on merit, will be picked over and scrutinized like no other players this week -- and for darned good reason. Bluntly put, they must prove they deserve to be here at all.

    "All I know, speaking of Tiger, I assume he's coming in here in a similar frame of mind to me," Harrington said. "As in, he's played his way into the teams over the years and has struggled with the team element, him knowing the way he performs, to do his individual thing. He plays his practice rounds very early in the morning, having to play Ryder Cup and all of these things organized and having to play five-hour practice rounds at 11 o'clock in the day has been tough on him over the years.

    "I'm sure this time, having been a pick, he will be a lot more enthusiastic about the Ryder Cup."

     
    Tiger Woods drew more attention during Wednesday's practice round than your typical wild-card pick. (AP)  
    Neither has had a season to get excited about. Both are winless, each has a losing Ryder Cup record and both were picked mostly based on their reputations versus current form or future projections.

    Apart from European standout Lee Westwood, who is on the weekly watch-list radar solely because of an injury, nobody will draw more eyeballs than Paddy and Woody. That's not entirely unusual, given their place in the game's pantheon, but this time it's for completely different reasons.

    While Harrington, one of the most genial men in the game, is embracing the opportunity to prove that European captain Colin Montgomerie's largesse wasn't misplaced, Woods insists nothing's different this time around, even though he is a first-time discretionary pick of U.S. captain Corey Pavin.

    "The pressure is the same," Woods said. "I want to keep the Cup in the United States. I appreciate Corey selecting me, but it doesn't matter how I got on the team."

    With all due respect, the fans will decide that issue, won't they?

    Making the team on a handout is a vastly different enterprise, as Ian Poulter learned in 2008, when he was picked by Nick Faldo amid considerable second guessing. Poulter had skidded out of the FedEx Cup playoffs early and hadn't played particularly well in the second half of the year.

    You know, similar to Harrington's current situation. In what had become the defining week of his career in many ways, Poulter dug deep and produced a 4-1 mark.

    "Felt I had to," Poulter said. "I was a pick, and it potentially could've been painful had I not delivered. It would've been a problem. I was taking some bullets. I deflected most of them.

    "I had to deliver, and I delivered. That to me was very encouraging, to be put out in that pressure, that intensity, that was way more than a major or another tournament, and I delivered. It was probably one of the most enjoyable weeks of my life."

    Now, imagine the converse if Woods and Paddy don't roll up some points. While the pair is in similar straits, the Irishman's selection was far more controversial. Woods, in the final analysis, was hardly a reach as a wild-card addition -- he was playing better than any of the three others who were hand-picked by Pavin.

    For Harrington, that was hardly the case. Passed over were Englishmen Paul Casey and Justin Rose, who vastly outplayed Harrington this season. Deep into the back nine of the FedEx Cup finale last Sunday, Casey still had a shot at the $10 million bonus.

    "I don't think I've ever been described as wild before," Harrington laughed Wednesday. "A wild card. It obviously puts you under a little bit more focus during the week, and brings certain expectations and certain pressure. So it's certainly different. It definitely makes you more enthusiastic and keen to play your part and do everything you can amongst the team."

    Harrington, who won three majors in a span of 13 months, hasn't won since he collected the third title in that run -- and that was 26 lean months ago. It has hardly passed unnoticed that Harrington hasn't won a full point in either of the past two Ryder competitions, going a dismal 0-7-2.

    Monty isn't crazy about the criticism that has accompanied his controversial decision, which has only heightened because Casey contended in each of the past two events in the United States.

    "I feel the criticism was very unjustified to be honest," Monty said this week. "I know what Padraig Harrington can do, and that's why he was picked. He's a world player; he's won three major championships and the stature of the guy is second to none within our team."

    Stature doesn't win points -- execution does. Monty was partly bailed out when Harrington, who made the cut on the number at the European Tour event last week outside Paris, shot 64 in the final round and finished T8. Still, Paddy was the only player in the world top 50 in the field, and he needed birdies on three of his last six holes on Friday just to make the weekend. "It was important that he felt he had a card and pencil in his hand the week before the Ryder Cup and did well," Monty said. "There are reasons why Padraig Harrington was picked. Judge me about that selection on October the 4th and not on September the 26th."

    Rest assured, given the way the tabloids eviscerated Faldo two years ago for his perceived failings, he needn't have issued a reminder.

    His opposite number, on the other hand, has done everything but sing Kumbaya as it relates to protecting Woods' inclusion as a subjective addition to the team. He has repeatedly insisted that Woods is no different than any of the 12 players on the team.

    Uh, right. Read a newspaper in the past 15 years, captain?

    "I think there's a tremendous amount of pressure on Tiger," said NBC analyst Gary Koch in a conference call last week. "I think he realizes this is a great opportunity to get things headed in the right direction, so there's a lot for him to prove this time around."

    No question, waving the victorious Stars and Stripes overhead on Sunday night could certainly help reinvigorate the brand. He was unusually deflective in his pre-event press session, even for him, so Woods seems to have showed up this week in a feisty mood.

    "The guy doesn't like losing," U.S. teammate Hunter Mahan said. "I don't think he likes you guys writing about how bad of a Ryder Cup player he is. I think he's going to find motivation in this week to kind of get back some of the U.S. fans, and I think he's very, very eager to play well this week."

    Steve Stricker, who at some point is surely going to be paired with Woods, said that vibe is nothing unusual, though plenty have sensed something akin to ambivalence from Woods when playing in international cup matches.

    "I think he's a little misunderstood when it comes to these team events," Stricker said. "I've played in a couple Presidents Cup teams with him, and he wants to win just as badly as anybody on our team. It's perceived otherwise at times, but I think he really wants to win."

    Now he's got a slew of new reasons to rev up his engine. Revive his reputation, resuscitate his game, prove he deserved to be picked. Same for Harrington, who by his own admission is more fully engaged than ever.

    On that front, we can all make book.
