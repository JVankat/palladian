Naylor: Food sovereignty for farmer and consumer
Posted: Friday, September 17, 2004
By By George Naylor
MinutemanMedia.org

The week of September 10th is significant throughout the world. It marks the anniversary of September 11th and of the collapse of the World Trade Organization (WTO) meeting one year ago. Recent WTO rulings against U.S. farm programs woke up the world to the need to reform U.S. farm and trade policy.

Farmers and consumers in the United States, struggling in the snare of an industrialized food system, can take heart. A new concept called food sovereignty is taking root. There is growing hope that consumers and family farmers can enjoy the glory of nature's bounty without the current burdens of unhealthy diets, declining rural communities, and a polluted countryside. Born out of the tragic consequences of international trade agreements, food sovereignty aims to bring democracy back into the picture. It will address the WTO's effort to force consumers and farmers into one world market--with low incomes for farmers and food for consumers that is imported, processed, and marketed by a handful of giant corporations.

"Free trade" agreements like the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), enacted in 1994, and the Central American Free Trade Agreement (CAFTA), now pending in Congress, treat food as any other kind of commodity like tennis shoes or computer chips. Multinational corporations seek out industrial-type production with low labor costs and minimum environmental laws, which displaces quality food produced by farmers caring for their land, family, and community.

The political clout of these corporations ensures our laws and the laws of other nations don't interfere with their appetite for cheap food commodities. In fact, when low farm prices threaten the operation of the farm economy as it has in the United States, the farm bill is written so taxpayers have to pony up billions of dollars in subsidies, while these corporations make it look like the farmers are the big beneficiaries.

Grassroots farmers and consumers around the world are asking many questions, such as:

• Do we want to be totally dependent on a few big corporations producing, processing, and marketing our food with only their profits in mind?

• Do farmers want to be dependent on these same corporations for patented seed and production contracts that take all decisions out of their hands?

• What kind of economic, social, and environmental future are we leaving our children?

• What kind of national security do we have when much of our food is imported from thousands of miles away?

Whether you're a Mexican peasant forced to leave your family and village to look for work in Mexico City or the United States, or a family farmer in the United States worrying about a corporate hog confinement polluting your air and water, or a consumer stuck on a diet merry-go-round, it's clear the United States needs new rules that serve the interests of farmers, consumers and the environment.

Food sovereignty is all about new rules--new laws--and a new, democratic way of looking at our farm and food system. The world's diversity of environmental, cultural, and political needs and traditions dictate that human values--other than "Mmm, tastes good, need more fast food"--will only be served by democratic decision making, not the "one size fits all" formulas of free trade agreements.

Every country and its citizens deserve food sovereignty. Through its democratic political process, a country can assure that family farmers get fair prices for their products while treating the land with respect and preserving it for future generations. Food deficit countries need tariffs and rules to prohibit cheap or unhealthy imports from harming their farmers or consumers. Food exporting countries like the United States, the European Union, Brazil, Argentina, Canada, and Australia must pass new farm bills that respect other countries' food sovereignty and the needs of their own farmers for fair prices.

Consumers deserve the right to know where their food is produced (Country of Origin Labeling) and whether it is genetically modified. Rather than increasing the control of multinational corporations with the world's food supply through free trade agreements, Americans and future generations around the world need more democracy, more food security, and more food sovereignty. This new awareness requires that Americans vote more wisely--with our food dollars and at the voting booth.

George Naylor is an Iowa farmer and president of the National Family Farm Coalition. The NFFC is a member of Via Campesina, an international farmers movement. The NFFC was founded in 1986 to serve as a national link for grassroots organizations working on family farm issues. www.nffc.net
