What role do public authorities play in the development of an appropriate market environment?

A presentation at the conference Poliseo Europe: Political Institutions in an Information Society, sponsored by the European Union. Rome, March 19, 1999.

Chair, Emma Bonino, European Commissioner for consumer policy.

As you are considering the role that public policy can play in fostering a productive information society, I want to bring to your attention some leading edge developments in the computer industry that are likely to have an increasingly significant impact in the years ahead.

I am talking about a movement that has come to be called Open Source Software.

Open Source has become front page news these days because of Linux, the free operating system that seems to be the first serious challenger to Microsoft�s dominance of operating systems for the PC market. What�s remarkable about Linux is that it was not developed by an existing competitor to Microsoft, or by a new startup company funded by venture capital, but by an informal coalition of thousands of volunteer programmers, worldwide, whose efforts have been coordinated via internet email and newsgroups. Starting with the work of a single college student in Finland, Linux now has thousands of developers and tens of millions of users.

Linux is free. Anyone who wants a copy can download it from the Internet and install it on a computer. Anyone who wants to add to it or change it has the computer source code to do so. Few of the thousands of developers who work on Linux are paid to do so; most do it as a hobby. Yet Linux is successfully challenging one of the most powerful companies in the computer industry.

Linux is international. Linus Torvalds is Finnish (although he now works in the United States). One of his closest co-workers is in the UK. The chief developers of the user interface components for Linux are in Germany and in Mexico. The security components are developed in Australia. One of the key developers of the Apache Web Server, which is also part of Linux, is here in Italy. In all, Linux includes contributions from dozens of countries.

The forces behind the success of Linux give powerful clues to the future direction of the computer industry, and perhaps of the broader information society.

In one of the essays in Open Sources, a book I recently published about the philosophical underpinnings of the Open Source movement, Michael Tiemann summarizes the principles that have made Linux possible with two quotations:

The first is from Adam Smith:

Free markets are self organizing, permitting the most efficient use of resources for the greatest creation of value.

And the second is from Thomas Jefferson:

Information, no matter how expensive to create, can be replicated and shared at little or no cost.

Add to these two ideas the power of the Internet to enable global cooperation and information sharing, and you have a sense of the foundations of the Open Source movement.

Open Source demonstrates just how empowering to the individual the Internet can be. With minimal resources, a multinational project can be coordinated, and the results disseminated to millions of people.

Ultimately, Open Source is a natural outgrowth of the western scientific tradition into the modern world of software and interconnected computer networks. After all, what is a program but the expression of an idea about how a computer should do a job? And what is freely redistributable, open source software, but the free exchange of ideas?

Voluntary cooperation by individuals in the pursuit of better software has been going on quietly for many years, and what is most surprising is not that it happens but how much it has been taken for granted.

For example, many people don�t realize that many of the most important parts of the Internet infrastructure and standards were created by an independent collection of volunteer programmers. Some of them have been at universities or research institutions, and others in industry, but the work they�ve done has generally not been funded by their institution.

The single most mission critical program on the Internet is a program called Bind�the Berkeley Internet Name Daemon. Originally developed at the University of California at Berkeley, Bind is the program that translates the arcane numerical addresses of internet sites into the domain names that have become so familiar to users of the web today. Bind has been maintained for more than a dozen years by a programmer named Paul Vixie, who also maintains a number of other free internet programs via a small organization called the Internet Software Consortium.

When we think of the Internet, we perhaps think of the Netscape or Microsoft web browsers, or the web sites we point to with them: yahoo.com, or amazon.com, or aol.com. But none of us could reach these sites without the volunteer work of a single long haired California programmer.

Or take email. Virtually every email message sent across the Internet is routed by a program called sendmail, which was written by a Berkeley researcher named Eric Allman. Eric maintained that program as a volunteer for more than a dozen years, giving it away for free to anyone who wanted to use it. Without that act of altruism, the Internet as we know it today would be very different. Eric recently started a new company to commercialize sendmail, but the company remains committed to maintaining the free version while developing value-added tools for commercial customers.

The central Internet standards body, the IETF, or Internet Engineering Task Force, is also a purely grassroots organization. The organization has no official membership�or rather, it can be defined as a membership organization that consists of "whoever shows up for its meetings." It consists of over a hundred "working groups," which debate technical issues via Internet mailing lists, and meet three times a year at locations around the world.

It's very important to the IETF that participants have no special status as a result of their company affiliation. Instead, status�and the power to influence the technical direction of a standard�is based on technical ability, and ideally, on working software that demonstrates the proposed standard.

These examples are only a few of hundreds I could give you. The point that I want you to take away is that many of the technical innovations that will take us forward into the next century are not the product of large companies, or of government-sponsored research, but of individuals, self-organizing in a kind of free market of ideas enabled by the worldwide spread of the Internet.

There is a radical democracy at work here, a global technical powerhouse that is purely participatory, purely voluntary.

Projects such as Linux or the development of the original Internet infrastructure have proven to be self-organizing on a global scale, and the fruits of their work have been shared at little or no cost, with immeasurable benefits, both economic and in the quality of life, that reach far beyond the actual participants in the projects.

So then, the question remains: what is the role of governments in supporting the kind of innovation and cooperation that is represented by Linux and the Internet infrastructure?

First, it is essential to keep Internet standards open, and developed cooperatively.

In the US, we�ve seen attempts by companies such as Microsoft to undermine and take control of Internet standards. Because the Internet standards and infrastructure software belong to everyone, they appear to belong to no one. Now that the Internet has been discovered by commercial ventures, there�s a mad rush to take advantage. I�m reminded of the old Brothers Grimm fairy tale about the goose that laid the golden eggs. As you may recall, the owner of the goose, not content with one egg a day, foolishly killed the goose in hopes of getting them all. Commercial software vendors, not noticing just what kind of goose has been laying the foundations of the 21st century, are in danger of doing just that.

The US Justice department is doing the right thing in trying to curb the excesses of a monopolist like Microsoft, but even without the threat of monopoly, there is danger in competitive fragmentation of technologies that need to form a shared, cooperative platform. The Internet is such a platform.

I am always very reluctant to suggest government intervention in any market. However, I do think that the cooperative approach that brought us the Internet is under attack by commercial interests. Increasingly, Internet standards are not open, but manipulated by companies for competitive advantage. What is particularly ironic is that these companies are treating the Internet as if it were a natural resource to be exploited, when in fact it is the creation of a cooperative movement that needs to conserved and encouraged.

We like to talk about free markets. But there are two kinds of free market. There is the free market of goods and services, which is what we normally mean by that term. But perhaps even more important is the free market of ideas. At least in the world of computer science, Open Source is the purest form of that free market of ideas.

In addition to guarding against monopolistic and anti-competitive practices, governments could help to encourage the cooperative development of next generation software by recognizing the heroes in your midst. Much as there is a tradition of government support of the arts, I would love to see government awards to programmers who have created work of outstanding value that they have contributed freely for the good of others. Do you know the names of the important Open Source programmers here in Italy as you know the names of your major industrial leaders? I believe you should.

A perhaps more minor, but still extremely important, thing that governments can do is to ensure that telecommunications costs are low. In general, networking costs are higher in Europe than the US, which makes participation in Internet projects more expensive. High telecommunications costs represent a significant barrier to entry into one of the most dynamic markets of the 21st century.

Further, beware of limitations on the spread of information in the form of grant of new rights to copyright holders. Existing copyright law is quite sufficient to protect intellectual property rights on the Internet. Recent proposals, such as the European Commission plan that would have banned Internet cacheing, are ill considered.

But perhaps the most important thing that governments can do is simply to study and learn more about the movement. The Open Source movement is a sign of the kind of future we can create for ourselves with the Internet: cooperative, altruistic, and innovative. By studying what happens in the technical community, we can perhaps learn important lessons that we can apply in other areas as we seek to build a better world.

Thank you very much.
