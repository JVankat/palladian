Wi-Fi consumers cautioned to wait on new gear
Two different groups testing new wireless products based on a draft standard of the next generation Wi-Fi technology caution consumers to hold off on buying pre-standard gear.

On Monday, the Fairpoint Group and the technology trade publication eWeek released results of tests conducted on new products using draft versions of the 802.11n standard. While eWeek's assessment is not nearly as negative as the analysis of the testing from the Fairpoint Group, both groups said they felt it was still too soon for consumers to buy products using 802.11n.

What the Fairpoint Group found during the testing was that the Linksys SRX400, which uses Airgo's third-generation MIMO technology that isn't compliant with the draft version of 802.11n, offered higher throughput at longer distances than all three of the other products tested, which used draft N technology.

eWeek tested Linksys' new WRT300N Wireless-N Broadband Router and the WPC300N Wireless-N Note-book Adapter, which both use Draft 802.11n chip technology from Broadcom. The article said Linksys' draft 802.11n gear was the fastest wireless equipment at short distances the magazine had tested to date, "besting even a pair of products based on Airgo's Gen 3 True MIMO chipset."

But when it came to long distances, a key reason for developing the 802.11n standard, eWeek, like The Fairpoint Group, found gear based on the draft standard fell short. eWeek said that performance at 50 feet lagged considerably when compared with the products using Airgo chips.

The base of the disc is the plastic substrate which is followed by a reflective layer and a dielectric layer. Between the first and last 3 standard layers comes the first recording layer of the disc labeled "L0 recording layer." There are a total of 4 layers to the right and each layer. ATI is getting ready for another release, to hit back at NVIDIAs 7600GT. The RV560 will have eight ROP units and is capable of pushing out eight pixels per clock. That is twice the number of the X1600. It will also have 24 Pixel Shader ALUs (arithmetical logical units) and four Vertex Shader units, while the number of Texture Memory Units (TMU's) will be doubled to eight.

Then there will be the RV570 which should give NVIDIA a run for its money. It will have 12 ROP and 12 units (12 pixel/textures per clock) with 36 Pixel Shader ALU's and six Vertex Shaders. Expect clock speeds of 650 - 700Mhz.

Both parts will be 80 nanometer, and will be called X1700Pro (RV560) and X1700XL and X1700XT (both based on the RV570).

The X1900XL will be just like the XT and XTX version with 16 ROP units, 48 Pixel Shaders and eight Vertex Shaders. It should be clocked somewhere between 500 to 560Mhz and will most likely have 1.4ns memory. This card should be priced around $299, making it a direct competitor to the 7900GT.
