Councillors rubbish new waste overhaul

*Cardiff council is considering seven new options for rubbish collections in the city as part of its waste strategy
*Welsh Assembly Government suggests three-box system to reach target for nation to be 70% recycling by 2025

    • Tweet this
  • Comments (…)

Citizens across Cardiff could be using three boxes for recycling and fortnightly black bag collections if new Welsh Assembly Government proposals go ahead in a radical overhaul of the city's waste collection scheme.

Seven options for changes to waste collection were presented to last night's environmental scrutiny committee as part of Cardiff council's waste management strategy, including the WAG preferred three-box option, but council officials are keen to find a solution specific to the city's urban setting.

WAG's waste strategy is part of a wider vision for achieving 70% recycling rates across Wales by 2025 – but some councillors felt the idea of having one waste collection scheme for Wales was unrealistic.

Executive member for environment Margaret Jones said:

"No decision has been taken on any aspect of the strategy but we'll need to address a number of fundamental issues.

"This is a policy area posing major challenges for local authorities across Wales not least given the need to square the achievements of recycling targets in the city."

Councillor Simon Pickard said:

"It seems to me that different local authorites across Wales are quite different and Cardiff is the most urban and transient population, so the idea of a blueprint imposed on all local authorities seems to be misjudged."

Head of waste policy at WAG, Jasper Roberts, said:

"A blueprint gives you something to work towards and to change. It's intended to be very useful in showing where the environment challenges are. We have got to take on the responsibility for the future."

Why is waste collection being changed?

The kitchen caddy on trial in some part of Cardiff Photograph: Phil Jones

The Welsh Assembly Government has set targets as part of its Towards Zero Waste policy for every local authority in Wales to achieve 70% recycling rates by 2025, and 53% by 2012/2013. The aim is to be a 100% recycling nation by 2050.

Currently Cardiff has a 39.2% recycling rate (2009/2010). Last night's scrutiny committee were told maximum recycling rate which could be achieved for Cardiff is 84%. WAG said certain waste options would maximise the possibility for recycling as well as a number of other benefits.

What's more, WAG will impose a fine on local authorities for every tonne of waste which is not recycled at some point in the future. The council will be fined £800,000 for every 2,000 tonnes a year which don't get recycled.

Advisor from Atkins, Tony Brotherton, said:

"The strategy is focused on what is the best solution for Cardiff. It must be sustainable, achievable, and minimise the financial risk to the council as well as being affordable."

Three boxes and fortnightly bins

WAG's preferred option is weekly recycling collections of the three boxes and weekly food collections, and fortnightly collection of green waste and residual waste (other household waste). The recycling would be collected by a vehicle with compartments for each recycling material, and sorted ahead of collection by householders.

Head of waste strategy at the Welsh Assembly Government, Dr Andy Rees, said the box system was a move towards a 'closed loop' recycling scheme – where glass bottles were recycled into glass bottles etc.

Rees also said the box system was more environmentally friendly. He said:

"The evidence we have found is that this scheme increases recycling rates but it requires the public to play their part. People need to be reminded on how to sort their rubbish."

Co-mingling vs kerbside sorting

Cardiff has problems with black bin bags being ripped open Photograph: Hannah Waldram

There is an ongoing debate on whether the proposed benefits of kerbside sorting (such as the WAG preferred option) overrules the green bag system currently in operation in Cardiff – which co-mingles recyclable waste and is then sorted manually or by a machine at the Lamby Way Material Recovery Facility.

Bridgend has recently moved to the kerbside sorting scheme and purports to have 56% recycling rates, and Newcastle-Under-Lyme even goes as far to have nine boxes for sorting recycling. Rees said:

"The different boxes make it easier for people to provide space in their homes for the rubbish. There are a lot of myths around in the press. We advise authorities to use a kerbside sorting system. However we recognise the challenges and certain concerns particularly in busy city centres."

But a recent e-poll by Cardiff council showed 81% of respondents preferred the green bag system currently in place over the box scheme.

Rubbish collections in the city centre by DJLeekee Photograph: Lee Smith

Cardiff council also said they were worried about the health and safety risks of the pre-sorted recycling because refuse collection staff would be spending longer amounts of time in the middle of the road while the right box was put into the right part of the vehicle.

Chair of the environmental scrutiny committee, Simon Wakefield said:

"This would be a fully compelling case it we were starting from scratch.

We need to have a dialogue for whether over a period of time Cardiff can move towards this, or whether it's 'big brother is going to come down on you'."

Councillor Elizabeth Clark challenged the validity of WAG claims. She said:

"Bridgend may have 56% recycling rates, but one fifth of the Cardiff population is very transient and sometimes transient populations don't know what the recycling guidelines are."

Councillor Monica Walsh said:

"I was wondering what world I was living in. I would like to know where in Cardiff you have surveyed. In my ward there's a very high rate of single parents and low income families and it's hard to make them recycle properly now.

"Some people don't give a damn about whether they recycle or not.

"I'm not in favour of the fortnightly collections and with so many boxes on the kerb there will be nowhere to walk on the pavements.

"Also, not everybody buys black bins to put their black bin bags in and some people don't have places to store them so they are left out."

Roberts said:

"The problem around Cardiff is when people are mixing stuff up and haven't got good practises in how to prepare waste. This comes back to behaviour of individual residents in the city. I'm a little more optimistic about how things can change. It will take time but I believe it can be done. I actually find the Cardiff waste system incredibly easy to use and incredibly clean."

Councillor Rod McKerlick said:

"What we need is stability – I understand the aspirations but much of what Dr Rees said was pretty hypothetical."

A Cardiff Council bin, by Dj Leekee Photograph: Lee Smith

Other options

The scrutiny committee will now consider seven options presented to them in this report, and whittle the number down to three after which consultation will take place to find out what the public believe to be the best option alongside council investigations. The preferred option will be implemented over the next five years.

The seven options Cardiff council has to consider include:

*Business as usual (current)
*Alternate weekly collections with fortnightly co-mingled recyclables
*Alternate weekly collections with weekly co-mingled Recyclables
*Alternate weekly collections with fortnightly kerbside sort recycling
*Alternate weekly collections with weekly kerbside sort recycling
*Fortnightly residual collections with weekly kerbside sort recycling and combined food green waste (WAG preferred option)
*Alternate weekly collections with fortnightly co-mingled recyclables and separate glass

What do you think the best rubbish collection scheme is for Cardiff? Add your thoughts below.
