Commentary

Passing the buck

The question posed to the emissions trading industry and more broadly, to the American public today is: where do we go from here? I visited the Scientific American site today and was presented a simple survey (sponsored by Shell Oil)  which I decided to take a chance on. The question was What should be the focus of new climate legislation in the USA? (Check all that apply) 1) cap-and-trade, 2) emissions tax 3) financial incentives 4) consumer rewards 5) safety standards 6) renewable energy incentives and 7) other. After checking a few and hitting the "submit" button, I was given the current tally of votes.

At the bottom, with 384 votes was cap-and-trade, just behind the emissions tax with 454 votes. Financial incentives scored 640 votes and consumer rewards received 657 votes while renewable energy incentives garnered 691 votes. And the number one vote getter was - Other - with 711 votes.

Even though this poll was conducted by the Scientific American, the results are ironically not very scientific. That said, it tells me is that the industry got its messaging wrong in Washington and across the U.S. on cap-and-trade. While those polled say they want financial incentives, very few see cap-and-trade as a financial incentive. And many of us in the emissions markets can make the argument that cap-and-trade is indeed a model that rewards those who innovate, and punishes those who do not in the most cost effective manner. But that message was drowned out by skeptics and others who got traction with the "cap-and-tax" label.

The other thing that tells me market-based supporters may be on the wrong track is the "other" category. People believe that there must be another answer to the global warming issue that does not involve markets, taxes or incentives. In other words, people want something to be done, but very few are willing to pay for it. This is the dilemma for this industry - explaining carbon markets in a way that makes sense to people, industry and legislators.

The question still remains, can this industry do that?

Five Minutes with David Hunter

David Hunter is North American director for the International Emissions Trading Association (IETA) and has headed up the United States office in Washington D.C. since 2007. He spoke recently with Jim Kharouf, editor of EMN, about his background and the prospects for market-based emissions markets for the U.S.

Q: How did you get started in this space?

A: I did my Ph.D. in climate variability at Scripps Institute of Oceanography at the University of California, San Diego. Then I went to the U.S. Senate, and did a Congressional science fellowship, working on air, energy and climate issues. I moved on from there to working for Sen. Susan Collins of Maine, where I spent seven years covering energy and environmental policy.
Then I thought it was time to leave The Hill but wanted to stay working in the climate space in one way or another, and work with the industry in something that addresses environmental problems in an economically efficient manner and that’s what IETA does.

Q: IETA is a global organization with headquarters in Geneva, Switzerland. You founded the U.S. office. What has been your focus?
A: [My focus has been] federal, state and regional policy. So I follow the Western Climate Initiative and to a lesser extent, the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative (RGGI). But my main focus lately has certainly been on Washington and making sure legislation such as Kerry-Lieberman, Waxman-Markey, McCain-Lieberman before that and Lieberman-Warner, Dingle-Boucher - that they come out with effective trading provisions that reduce emissions at low cost. Basically, it's taking advantage of the lowest cost abatement opportunities and setting up a reasonable structure and time frame that gives industry time to prepare, adapt, but also gives them a chance to address the cheapest emissions now. So we advocate for strong offsets provision in the legislation and making sure you have a structure where you allow trading and allow capital to flow through to the projects where you get the biggest bang for your buck.

Q: Looking at the broader political landscape today, there isn’t much probability that we’ll see comprehensive climate legislation out of Washington. What’s the next best prospect - regional markets of other markets?
A: We’ve entered a period that is messy and unpredictable. But the issue is not going away and has not gone away. Cap-and-trade has taken quite a beating over the last couple years for issues that were beyond anyone’s control - the economy, anger toward Wall Street and how the economic problems arose, a lack of bi-partisanship. Despite that, cap-and-trade has been extraordinarily resilient.

Make no mistake. You could not pass anything else at this point, in this Congress. The fact that Congress can not extend the tax cuts, they can’t find a way to tackle the deficit, can’t agree on even a simple energy package, haven’t managed to pass the vast majority of the appropriations bill, should not be taken as a sign that the climate issue is dead. So there will be a new Congress and at some point they will have to work together to figure it out. I don’t know when that will be. But sooner or later Congress needs to get down the business of running the country and addressing our problems. When they do that, climate change is not only an environmental problem but also one for industry on the regulatory front, with the EPA regulations. You have not seen EPA’s command and control increase in popularity, nor have you seen carbon taxes increase in popularity. So while you’ve seen cap-and-trade take a beating, it still remains the most popular method there is.

I think we’re going to see something at the state level. We’ll see what happens at the elections. And if Washington does not act decisively, states usually do step up and take more action. I think we’ll see more legal action, more lawsuits and more regulatory action. But as Congress sees that is not the most effective way, they are going to be looking for alternatives.

Q: So is it likely then that we’ll see a combination of more EPA leadership on this issue - either command and control structures, or maybe even some type of market-based structure - along with states saying, “We’re going to just push on through?”

A: I think it’ll be all of the above, a combination of state and regional action. On top of that, you’ll have EPA regulatory actions. And you’ll see Congress trying to have an impact. It will have to work with the administration because Congress doesn’t have the votes. Effectively, we already have a divided government because no single party has enough votes to get something done, without support from the other party. That situation won’t change in the next Congress.

Q: As you look out there, what are you hearing from your members? Are they pulling back then because of this uncertainty?
A: A lot of members are pulling back. It’s unfortunate because now is the time when we really need to be ramping up, putting more capital into reducing emissions and preparing the infrastructure to reduce CO2 emissions. Instead, Congress has not provided the signals that industry needs to effectively deploy capital.

Q: When you look at the global marketplace. EUETS is pretty vibrant. We keep hearing interest in Australia, even China for emissions markets along with Japan and Korea. Could that pull the U.S. back into rethinking an emissions market?

A: In many ways, cap-and-trade emissions markets have shown remarkable staying power throughout a very difficult political and economic period. The fact is that you still see them as the method of choice of the current House and Senate leadership, and around the world. There really are not other good alternatives, so I think people will come back to market-based approaches. The U.S. really needs to lead on that.

Q: So is a sectoral, or a utilities industry, approach the best way forward?

A: From an economic perspective, an economy-wide approach is the best approach because the broader the market, the more efficiencies you have. But from a political practicality standpoint, you’re looking at smaller, less comprehensive solutions.

Q: So what does this industry need to do then to move the ball forward politically?

A: We need to be more effective with the back to basics message. We need to remind politicians that the idea of market-based approaches is both fundamentally an American idea and one we came up with for the Acid Rain Program. It’s also the one we pushed internationally, even though the U.S. withdrew from Kyoto. And it's also fundamentally a Republican idea. The Acid Rain Program passed under a Republican president [George H.W. Bush]. It’s a good idea and one that should be bi-partisan. I think the industry can get that message across.

Q: This is an interesting issue in that we’re really also talking about an energy efficiency message. You have other countries investing heavily into renewables but the U.S. is standing still at the moment. Does this messaging also go hand in hand with the other message?

A: Yes, we are competing on many different levels. The world in the future will be a lower carbon world and those countries which develop the technologies and systems to be more efficient will have advantages. If you know which way the world is heading, and you can get there first, then you’ll have an advantage. I don’t think we’re showing leadership on that front now.
