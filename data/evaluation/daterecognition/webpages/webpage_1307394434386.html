Osteoporosis

Definition

The composition of bone is normal but the amount of bone per unit volume is less than normal, and is defined as a bone density below the 95th percentile of the young normal range By this definition everyone from 40 on becomes increasingly osteoporotic This process is accelerated after the climacteric (~ 50 years in women and ~ 65 in men) Osteoporosis in people under 45 should be investigated to exclude malignant disease, dietary deficiency etc

Aetiology

Nutritional
Scurvy Malnutrition Malabsorption
Malignant disease
Carcinomatosis Multiple myeloma Leukaemia
Endocrine disorders
Hyperparathyroidism Gonadal insufficiency Cushing's disease Thyrotoxicosis
Non malignant disease
Rheumatoid arthritis Ankylosing spondylitis Tuberculosis Chronic renal disease
Drug induced
Corticosteroids Alcohol Heparin Idiopathic Juvenile osteoporosis Post climacteric osteoporosis
Mechanical
Disuse Immobilisation
Haematological
Thalassaemia Sickle cell disease

Incidence

Commonest of the metabolic bone disorders All women over the age of 65 have suffered vertebral crush fractures (many of them minor) White women particularly of north western European ethnic background have a much higher incidence than do those of Mediterranean descent

Classification

Type I Idiopathic Type II Females between menopause and 65 years (10 - 15% loss of bone in first 5 years) Type III Males and females > 65 years (1% / year bone loss)

Clinically

Only becomes symptomatic when it leads to structural failure of the bone Fractures occur with minimal force especially in the spine (thoracic wedge and lumbar crush fractures), distal radius (Colles'), femoral neck fractures, and unsuspected rib fractures may? chest pain Unless there is an associated metabolic disorder biochemical tests are normal

Risk factors

Early or surgically induced menopause (not in the long term) Family history Low weight and slight build Sedentary lifestyle Excessive alcohol intake? direct depression of osteoblast function Steroids? decreased formation and increased resorption

X-Rays

General loss of bone density may be obvious but does not differentiate osteoporosis from osteomalacia Wedging and biconcave indentation of the vertebrae is common but isolated fractured in the upper thoracic spine suggest malignancy In long bones the cortex is unduly thin At least 30% of the bone mass must be lost before rarefaction is evident on X-Ray Singh index? uses femoral neck trabecular patterns as index of osteoporosis

Investigations

Blood and endocrine screens to exclude metabolic bone disorders, serum calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, 24 hour urinary calcium, creatinine and hydroxy proline (fasting)? mineral and collagen turnover disorders. Pyridinoline cross links of Type 1 collagen and tartrate resistant acid phosphatase- assess bone destruction Plasma cortisol, 1,25 DHCC, PTH, T3 & T4, serum electrophoresis, blood count and ESR also useful Bone biopsy after double labelling with tetracycline 10 - 14 days apart? meaningful interpretation of bone dynamics Radiographic evaluation using photodensitometry? estimated mass by measuring optical density of the radiograph but does not take account of soft tissue thickness Also use quantitative CT scanning Single and dual photon absorptiometry (densitometry) makes use of difference in photon absorption between bone and soft tissue to determine bone mineral content (single photon? immersion in water bath to subtract soft tissue component, dual photon uses an isotope with emission of two energy levels (dual photon looks at lumbar spine or femoral neck)

Pathology

Peak bone mass achieved in mid 30's in both sexes, the value influenced by sex, nutrition, race, exercise and overall health (being 30% higher in men than women) Women lose 10 - 15% of their bone mass in the first 5 years after menopause After the age of 65 it makes no difference when the menopause occurred? approximately1% bone mass lost per year Menopause? increased bone resorption (ie increased osteoclastic activity) and further loss may be related to decreased formation Trabecular bone with its higher surface area and greater metabolic activity is first area to be resorbed? Colles and vertebral fractures early (soon after menopause) and the incidence of hip fractures increases slowly with age due to loss of cortical bone 75% of osteoporotic fractures occur in bone with density 3 standard deviations below the pre menopausal mean Fat people protected somewhat due to peripheral conversion of androgens to oestrogen and increased strain on the skeleton

Treatment

If there is a specific underlying cause it may be correctable Bone density 75 - 100% of normal no treatment required Bone density 50 - 75% of normal re scan in 12 months Bone density less than 50% of normal treat (oestrogens) Treatment directed towards one or more of the following

  1. Calcium intake
  2. Calcium absorption
  3. Bone formation
  4. Bone resorption
  5. Urinary calcium

Important to correct dietary deficiency and keep the patient active Daily requirement of calcium in Australia 800mg Pre menopausal women daily allowance 1.2gm / day Menopausal women daily allowance 1.5gm / day Extra gram of calcium per day may decrease bone loss Vitamin D may be needed if deficient or lack of sun exposure Anabolic steroids may be used (eg deca durabolin can stimulate bone formation but also? increased hair and voice changes) and is the only treatment that will increase bone density
Dose 50mg monthly with 6/12 treatment and 6/12 no treatment Bone mass once lost is extremely difficult to restore
Protocol Give calcium supplement and if still? bone loss ? measure urinary calcium and if high ? restrict salt (as salt excretion carries out calcium) May also try thiazide as this decreases urinary calcium If still? bone loss add oestrogen If still? bone loss may? calcitonin Fluoride may be used in combination with vitamin D and calcium (triple therapy) but fluoride? high incidence of side effects (40%)

Prevention

Now good evidence that hormone replacement (oestrogens) given from the onset of the menopause can prevent progressive bone loss Benefit maximised when treatment begins within 6 years of the menopause
Side Effects
Endometrial hyperplasia + cancer? use cyclical oestrogen and progesterone Worsening of pre-existing breast Ca Aggravation of DVTs (? effect of low dose post menopausal oestrogen) Potentiation of gallstones
Contraindications
Breast Ca (regardless of oestrogen receptor status) Any other oestrogen sensitive tumour Past history of PE Acute or chronic liver disease or low grade chronic hepatitis Severe uncontrolled hypertension
Dose
0.625mg conjugated oestrogen / day first 25/7 of month 10mg medroxyprogesterone / day for 10/7 (days 16 - 25 each month) Modify the dose to suit the patient, could start with 0.3mg oestrogen There is however still a fear of possible harmful side effects of long term oestrogen therapy Prospective studies have demonstrated that one hour of impact exercise 2 - 3 times per week can maintain or slightly increase bone mass in lumbar spine compared to sedentary controls

Differential diagnosis

Osteomalacia Osteoporosis
Unwell Not unwell
General bone ache Pain only after fracture
Muscles weak Muscles normal
Looser's zones No Looser's zones
ALP increased ALP normal
Serum phosphorous and calcium decreased Serum calcium and phosphorous normal
Urine calcium low Urine calcium normal or high
