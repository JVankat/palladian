Facets of Drug Abuse in Egypt: An Overview

Dr. Tarek Mahmoud Samy Abdel-Gawad

Professor, of Psychiatry, Addiction Unit, Faculty of Medicine, Cairo University

Endnotes

Epidemiological data on drug abuse in the Arab countries are still very scarce. No official reports can be gathered from many Arab countries where drug abuse is prohibited by legal and religious systems. In Egypt, with a population of 65 million inhabitants, drug addiction is considered one of the serious problems that worry both the people and government. It affects young people within their productive years. It may lead to many problems such as social maladaptation, decreased work productivity and job loss (1,2).

Alcohol and Beer were known to Egyptians since the time of the Pharaohs (3). During the last century, three major drug outbreaks occurred, the latest was in the mid-eighties, where the spread of heroin constituted a major problem. During that period, an interest for research to monitor the changing pattern of drug addiction in Egypt re-emerged.

The Epidemiological Database

A number of projects have been carried out. Abol-Magd (4) reviewed the changing pattern of drug abuse in a review of all inpatient records over the period from 1972 to 1982. His results demonstrated a significant increase in the number of addicts with a decrease of the mean age of abuse. Abuse was significantly higher among the male married group than in the corresponding female group. Hashish was commonly abused as adjuvant drug prior to the intake of heroin, the main drug of abuse in that sample.

Soueif et al. (5-8) conducted a series of national epidemiological studies about the "Ever Used Drug" among technical school students, male secondary school students, male university students, female university students and male workers in the manufacturing industry. Hashish and opiates were the most commonly reported drugs among the above-mentioned five groups. The percentages reported of "Ever Used Hashish" were 9%, 5%, 8.5%, 0.05% and 11% respectively while the percentages of "Ever Used Opiates" were 0.0%, 1%, 0.5%, 0.01% and 1% in the above-mentioned five groups respectively.

In 1990, Gawad (9) reported that the mean age of "Ever Used Drugs "(alcohol, tobacco, cannabis, hallucinogens, sedatives. & tranquilizers, opium, heroin, other opiates, amphetamines, cocaine, antidepressants, other drugs) were 17, 14, 18, 23, 24, 25, 24, 25, 21, 26, 27, 24 years of age respectively. The sample consisted of thirty-nine male inpatients recruited from three different hospitals scattered across Greater Cairo over a period of six months. The progression of their use of drugs started with tobacco, then cannabis, stimulants or alcohol, and then heroin.

A preliminary community report of the Egyptian National Research on Addiction was released in 1996 (10). This report was conducted in five governorates out of the twenty-six constituting the Republic of Egypt. The selected sites were chosen to represent the different social cultures of Egypt. The project involved a stratified sample of both sexes over the age of 16 (N = 16,645). The report addressed the characteristics of those who used substances at least once versus those who did not use psychoactive substance at all. All abused drugs were included except for tobacco. The results presented were only raw figures and percentages rather than statistically significant results. The "Did Not Use" group amounted to 15,588 individuals versus the "Used" group involving 1,043, about 15:1. As a whole, the number of positive responses in the community sample is minimal as compared to the claimed lay impression or clinical observations. A caveat is that Cairo, with a population of 16 million, was not included in the study, and it is believed that its inclusion could change the statistical results significantly.

In 1999, the United Nation Drug Control Program (UNDCP) in Egypt carried out a screening project to provide original data collected from drug users and to complement existing survey and research material. They concluded that: the most vulnerable age group lay between ages 15 to 25. Peer pressure was strongly correlated with secondary school abuse. Treatment facilities were not evenly distributed throughout Egypt and, finally, the most recently widespread drug was "Bango" (cannabis derivative).

 

Meeting the Service Needs

Despite the ever-growing awareness about addiction hazards among the Egyptians, the integration of services and communication both nationally and internationally needs to be developed and improved in order to implement consensual effective comprehensive measures as well as optimal services of whatever modality required and accepted nationally.

Egypt has about a thousand qualified psychiatrists working inside the country. Just a handful of them are trained and specialize in the field of addiction. The major brunt of care falls in the hands of the general psychiatrists. The number of beds assigned for treating addicts is about 600, scattered all over Egypt. About 49% of those beds are served by either the private sector or Non-Governmental Agencies (NGO).

In 2002, the Minister of Health and Population is leading a national reform for health services with special emphasis on developing community-based services. Mental health in general and addiction services in particular are receiving considerable attention in this respect. International consultation was sought and actively contributes to developing and promoting addiction services including the Finnish and the Canadian governments, as well as the WHO and UNDCP.ISAM was also introduced to the officials in Egypt as a professional association with expertise in developing a national training and referring system spanning from the primary health care provider to optimal culturally sensitive management options for individuals suffering from drug addiction.
