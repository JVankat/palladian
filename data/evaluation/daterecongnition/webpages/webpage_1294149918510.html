Human Rights Discourse and Global Civil Society:

Contesting Globalization?

 

Fiona Robinson

Associate Professor

Deparment of Political Science, Carleton University

 

Prepared for the 2002 Annual Meeting of the International Studies Association,

New Orleans, Louisiana, March, 2002

 

Introduction

 

Talk of human rights is, currently, nearly as ubiquitous as talk of globalization.� While globalization has been described as �the most over used and under specified term in the international policy sciences since the end of the Cold War�, the same could reasonably be said of �human rights�.[1]� Human rights are a product of the immediate aftermath of World War II, and thus they developed, in their contemporary form, in the context of the Cold War.� The philosophical and political roots of human rights, of course, date back at least to the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, and some would say even further, to the Stoics of Ancient Greece.� Globalization, too, has unfolded mainly in the late twentieth-century and has reached a position of prominence in the post-Cold War context; at this juncture, and according to popular perception, the spread of market capitalism, Western culture and modern technology fit comfortably with the death of socialism and the �end of history�.� But globalization too has roots that date back much earlier� � as early as, it has been argued, the fourteenth century.[2]

 

The purpose of this paper is to explore the relationship between globalization and human rights, in the context of so-called �global civil society� (GCS).� In particular, I seek to interrogate the use of the human rights discourse by social movements,� non-governmental organizations (NGOs), and the emerging �anti-globalization� movement.� While there has been much celebration of both the achievements and the potential of global civil society in contesting globalization, there have also been considerable doubt, scepticism and pleas for caution.� Most of this has focused on the deficits in democratic practice within the organizations of GCS; the degree of dependence of NGOs on financial support from governments and other international institutions; and the �discursive demobilization� of global social movements vis-�-vis globalization and the resultant lack of a �strategic/normative stance�.[3]�� While these are all crucial and often ignored points which deserve recognition and will feature in this paper, the central argument will focus on the extent to which the many of the organizations of GCS � especially contemporary Northern NGOs and social movements -- have relied on an ostensibly universal discourse of human rights to promote a set of values that are heavily gendered and which reflect a Western bias.�� The ethics and language of human rights, I argue, has not been wrested from the liberal framework from which it originated, and with which it has been so closely associated for the last half-century.� As such, this discourse still relies on the same basic set of values as the agents of globalization themselves � the transnational corporations, the international financial institutions and the powerful Western states.�� This suggests that, in relying on the discourse of universal human rights, not only will the organizations of GCS be unsuccessful in contesting globalization, but they may indeed be complicit in sustaining it.�

 

The paper will explore the processes of globalization, the nature and role of global civil society and the historical origins and contemporary nature of the discourse of human rights.�� I will argue that human rights as they are currently understood can provide neither a practical goal nor a discursive basis for anti-globalization movements because they represent not universal values but a particular set of values and political ends that are Western-centric and which reflect a Western hegemonic masculinity.� To support this claim I will explore two developments in human rights theory and practice: first, a renewed interest in and commitment to economic and social rights in the context of globalization, exemplified by the recent move by Western human rights NGOs � notably, Amnesty International -- to incorporate economic and social rights into their mandates; and second, the on-going struggle for the recognition of women�s human rights.�� I will argue that neither of these efforts to repackage human rights have, as yet, adequately addressed the moral, socio-economic and political challenges faced by the states and peoples of the Third World and by women the world over.� This is because the ethical and discursive construction that underwrites these modified forms of human rights remains essentially unchanged.

 

It will be a major contention of this paper that there can be no single discursive or ethico-political strategy against globalization.� I will, however, suggest that feminist ethics may offer a fundamentally new understanding of what it means to challenge globalization, based on two key factors:� first, its relational ontology, and second, its scepticism of moral universalism and ethical necessity.� On the basis of this ethics, human rights may have a role to play, but they will no longer be able to articulate �human� goods � such as democracy and environmental security � as �genuinely universal, consensual aspects of a global �common good�.� I do not suggest this approach to ethics as a normative focus strictly �for� women and peoples of the Third World; rather, it should be understood as an approach with widespread relevance which questions the necessity of globalization without replacing it with yet another moral and political imperative.

 

Globalization, Competition and the State

While globalization is an elusive and heavily contested term, it is generally recognized as referring to economic, technological and social processes which have led to a situation in whereby �social relations are less tied to territorial frameworks�.[4]� Economic globalization, however, is more specific:� it usually refers to the processes of liberalization, deregulation and integration of the global economy in the areas of production, exchange and finance.[5]� Much of the literature surrounding globalization � and especially its economic aspects � focuses on the changing role of the state in light of these transformative processes.� While the most extreme sceptics, on one hand, and the �hyperglobalists�, on the other, disagree on this question, there seems to be a broad underlying continuity among the majority of analysts:� specifically, while state and interstate relations persist at the core of governance arrangements, there is nonetheless a notable change in the character of the state: �its capacities; its constituencies; its policy-making processes; its policy contents; and so on�.[6]�

 

In one of the most compelling arguments regarding the state and globalization, Phil Cerny has argued that the main task or function of the contemporary state is the promotion of economic activities, both at home or abroad, which make firms and sectors located within the territory of the state competitive in international markets.[7]�� Cerny calls this the �competition state�, thus differentiating it from the �strategic� or �developmental� state of the 1980s.� Then, states were, as they have always been to some extent, promoters of market forces; today, by contrast, states are transforming themselves into more and more market-oriented and market-based organizations in the search for competitiveness in an increasingly economically interpenetrated world.� The effects of this are, potentially, immense:

�� we may be witnessing the transmutation of the state from a civil association into a more limited form of enterprise association, or perhaps some more complex combination of the two, operating within a wider market and institutional environment.� This is not merely a change in degree, but a change in kind.� Uneven globalization is thus leading to the emergence of a still-embryonic �residual state�, which may be vulnerable to crises of legitimacy.[8]

 

This is not to suggest, however, that the state has become redundant.�� In spite of the globalization of economic structures, the national state retains great political, social and psychological significance for its citizens, as well as some vital political and economic functions at both domestic and international levels.� However, while the state retains a crucial structural role in a globalizing world, its �holistic and overarching character has been deeply compromised�.[9]

 

 

The forces and processes of economic globalization that are pushing the state to transform � to �marketize� � are powerful ones indeed.� They have been made all the more powerful insofar as they have been presented and widely accepted not as a policy option or ideological/economic/ethical position, but as �common sense�:� in a world of deregulated exchange rates and the absence of controls on the movement of capital, financial stabilization and economic liberalization have been presented as essential to sustain a sound currency, international banking, a beneficial balance of international and international trade, and the foreign investment that is essential to growth.[10]� This �common sense� has become known as the �Washington consensus�, and was enforced during the 1980s and 1990s by the World Bank and the IMF, who compel states to observe the �commonsensical conditions� that these institutions put upon their lending.� Furthermore, the WTO has, more recently, made it difficult for states not to lose if they refuse to accept the organization�s rules on international trade.[11]� Thus, while all states are affected by globalization, weaker states, which lack the conditions currently present in the United States, have slim prospects for economic � not to mention social and political -- success.

 

Recently, it has been argued that this �Washington Consensus� has given way, in the last five years or so, to a �Post Washington Consensus�.� The principle element of this new consensus is the development of an understanding amongst the international institutional policy community of the need for a stronger �governance dimension� to the international economic order.[12]� This change has been brought about by, among other things, the protests against globalization and the general recognition that global liberalization brings with it increased inequality.� However, as Richard Higgott points out, this shift represents only the minimum response from the international policy community to manage the increasing hostility towards the liberalization and deregulation processes that have been at the heart of globalization.� Indeed, what characterizes the PWC, according to Higgott, is its �lack of an understanding of politics and a wider commitment to the creation of a global ethic of poverty alleviation via a commitment to redistribution�.[13]

 

Thus, despite the shift towards �global governance�, it can by no means be said that we are moving towards a kind of �global politicization�.[14]� Descriptions of contemporary global governance range from the ideational and ethereal � �the cloud of ideological influences that has fostered the realignment of elite thinking to the needs of the world market� � to the concrete and material � the powerful public institutions such as the WTO, the IMF and the World Bank, and the UN.[15]�� While some regard contemporary global governance as a positive step forward in the regulation of global capitalism, it has been pointed out that many of these institutions remain �creatures of the most powerful of their state members�; thus, there is concern that the institutions of contemporary global governance remain driven by the logic of capitalist growth and are themselves undermining democracy and are contributing to the growing gap between rich and poor.[16]

 

Thus, while the very existence of a system of global governance is, in part, a response to anti-globalization pressure and sentiment, there can be little doubt that the Post Washington Consensus will be as much the target of contestation as its predecessor.� To quote Higgott once more:

The PWC is no less universalizing and attempts to be no less homogenizing, that the WC itself.� Global policy debates, in this way, remain reliant on a set of generalizable, but essentially Western, liberal principles and policy prescriptions.� �these prescriptions still demonstrate a penchant for universalizing notions of a �one-size-fits-all� convergence on issues of policy reform under conditions of globalization.� Such prescriptions may well be resisted in the developing world as but a new form of Western hegemony.[17]

 

�The section which follows will explore the nature of the response to globalization and global governance from the organizations of civil society.

 

Civil Society, Human Rights and the Challenge to Globalization

The term civil society has a relatively long history and thus has had a number of different meanings.� It is, however, above all, a specifically Western term, having its roots in the European Enlightenment.� Civil society in Enlightenment thought was understood as the realm of particular interests, which in practice then meant the realm of the bourgeoisie.� This is the view of civil society as �society minus the state� as in the work of Smith and Marx.[18]� Hegel�s view, which retained this basic meaning, suggested that civil society created the basis of common welfare out the pursuit of particular interests.� With the writings of de Tocqueville in the nineteenth century, civil society is no longer identified with capitalism and the bourgeoisie but now takes on the meaning of a mobilized participant citizenry that exists between the economy and the state.� Within the contemporary literature on International Relations and Global Political Economy, it is the concept of civil society which arises from the Gramscian debate in Western Europe that is most prevalent; on this view, civil society is �the arena in which groupings in society represent themselves, ideologically and institutionally, both in relation to other social groups, and in relation to the state.[19]�� Gramsci emphasized the importance of consciousness-creation, as well as the cultural aspects of civil society as important sites in the creation of hegemony.� While civil society could challenge the established order in this context through the creation of a counter-hemonic consciousness, it could also act to support the hegmonic social order, in the sense that it consisted of institutions which helped to create in people certain modes of behaviour and expectations consistent with this order.[20]

 

The existence and nature of contemporary �global� civil society is currently the subject of much debate. There is debate, for example, about whether �civil society� � in the Gramscian sense � can actually be �globalized� in the absence of a truly global state.[21]� Martin Shaw has answered this by suggesting that global civil society corresponds to an emerging �global state� which reflects the �pooling of the monopoly of violence among major states�.[22]�� Thus, global civil society in the contemporary world is not so much a collection of already existing and often strong national civil societies; rather, it is about the growth of new forms of action which have been seen as �spearheading globalism�.� The new social movements � for peace, human rights, democracy and the environment � have seemed to represent a new potential for �global consciousness�.[23]

 

It is global civil society thus understood that is widely recognized as �spearheading� the direct political activism against globalization.� While there has been much optimism and celebration surrounding the actors of global civil society and their efforts to bring about social transformation, these same actors have been subject to substantial criticism.�� For example, even advocates of GCS, such as Shaw, admit that global civil society originated among Western social elites.� For Shaw, however, this is a problem solely of �representativeness�.� Although he worries about how far global civil society actually involves people across the globe, he seems convinced that globalist ideas represent �inclusive global interests in an ideological sense�.[24]

 

In a more radical critique, Tony Evans has argued that the attempt to secure universal civil, political and economic rights through civil society may reinforce a set of values that support current exclusionary practices found in globalization.� Relying on Marx, Evans argues that �since civil society represents the private sphere, a sphere intended to guarantee the liberty necessary to pursue private satisfactions, human rights are concerned with the egoistic, atomized, isolated individual, separated from community�.[25]� On his view, the vacuum left by the decline of the state cannot be filled by civil society.� Rather, he claims, civil society �reflects the narrow self-interests of those in a structural position to take full advantage of the conditions of globalization, to the exclusion of the many�.[26]

 

Here, it could be argued, Evans is taking what Shaw describes as the �classic� view of civil society � i.e. that taken by Smith and Marx.� On this view, it is not surprising that civil society makes use of the neo-liberal discourse of rights; as he points out, the

�human rights associated with neoliberal civil society are limited to civil and political claims�.[27]� Defenders of the transformative and progressive potential of global civil

society, however, such as Shaw or Mary Kaldor, would likely counter that civil society must be understood as the sphere of association between economy and state, and would thus claim that there is greater potential for resistance to the hegemony located in both the state and global capitalism.

 

But even when understood in this way, widespread concern is evident.� Despite the calls by global social movements and NGOs for states and corporations to be more accountable and democratic in their behaviour, many of the organizations of global civil society themselves are only questionably democratic.� As Devetak and Higgott argue,

(i)f NGOs and other non-state actors are to become legitimate agents of acceptable structures of global governance in an era of globalization they will have to develop transparent, accountable and participatory systems of decision-making of exactly the kind they expect to see in national governments, multinational corporations and international organizations.[28]

 

Moreover, the extent to which global civil society actors are truly voicing and representing an alternative to the hegemonic neo-liberal global order is also questionable.� NGOs are often dependent for financial support from governments and other international institutions; thus, the NGOs may serve more to support existing systems of governance than to replace them.[29]� Spivak has suggested that �international civil society� is, in fact, a creation of the agents of what she calls �transnational financialization�; these agents name the collectivity of collaborative non-governmental organizations an �international civil society�, bypassing individual states, and then �attempt to appropriate these NGOs by much publicized participation at UN conferences�.[30]� Furthermore, it has been argued that NGOs, promoted by the World Bank and other agencies, are driving a wedge between the NGOs and governmental departments, and are thus restraining the role of the state and for eroding its legitimacy.� On this view, NGOs are complicit in global capitalism, and are instruments of the �new thrust of the neoliberal dogma in which the state itself � is given a back seat�.[31]

 

This is not to say, however, that there are no �oppositional structures� in place within civil society; nor is to suggest that these groups are not often influential and effective at the local level. To quote Spivak once again:

Alternative development collectivities, national-local health care, ecology and literacy collectivities have been in place for a long time, and play a critical role at the grassroots level.� Why are they seldom heard? These oppositional structures are indigenous NGOs, or non-governmental organizations.[32]�

 

The NGOs that� surface at the �NGO Forums� of the UN conferences have been so thoroughly vetted by the donor countries, and the content of their presentations so organized by categories furnished by the United Nations, that neither subject nor object bears much resemblance to the �real thing�, if you will pardon the expression.[33]

 

Because the governments of most developing nations are, as she puts it �heavily mortgaged� to international financial institutions, the relationship between the government and these NGOs is ambiguous and complex.[34]��

 

It could be argued, then, that the activities of �global civil society� � the civil society that is seen and heard --� is thus neither autonomous from nor particularly challenging to the processes of globalization.� While there is certainly potential for resistance and transformation inherent within global civil society, its present incarnation it has not, and cannot, move beyond the dominant, broadly liberal framework.� As Scholte argues, the non-state actors that influence global economic governance are disproportionately Northern, Western, white, males who are also propertied, professional, computer-literate, English-speaking urban residents.[35]�� Not surprisingly, the values that tend to be put forward by these members are largely homogeneous.� As Mustapha Pasha and David Blaney point out, �envisioning a democratic global society requires resisting not only the existing oligarchical organization of global life, but also the impulse to rectify that situation with a prefigured set of global values sponsored by the transnational extensions of actors from advanced industrial societies�.� That values such as human rights and democracy (the values associated with GCS by virtually all of its proponents) are genuinely universal, consensual aspects of a global �common good� is by no means beyond question.[36]

 

The use of the human rights discourse by global civil society is widespread.� However, the perceived links between global civil society and human rights are more than discursive.� Mary Kaldor has argued that to be part of civil society implies a shared commitment to common human values and, in this sense, the concept of global civil society might be equated with the notion of a global human rights culture.� Indeed, she sees the two notions as convergent:

Some would argue that civil society is broader since it encompasses issues such as peace, gender equality and the environment.� But these can easily, and in some cases rather usefully, be reconceptualized as human rights issues.� Hence, Russian human rights groups who campaign against the war in Chechnya argue that the war is a massive violation of human rights.� Likewise, independent trade unions in Russia argue that they are human rights organizations concerned with workers� rights.� The advantage of the human rights discourse is its globalist character and its emphasis on the individual � the �last child� as Gandhi put it.[37]

 

Further evidence of the use of human rights language and norms by the organizations of global civil society which seek to challenge the ethics, ideology and processes of globalization is abundant.� However, this evidence also reveals the extent to which the same discourse is used to articulate the aims of the powerful actors who are pushing the globalization agenda.� A document on the Free Trade Area of the Americas and the Threat to Democracy, written and published by the Council of Canadians, a non-profit citizens advocacy group, provides an excellent illustration of the reliance on this language to describe both the actions and aims of the agents of globalization � the corporations � and their own � the peoples� -- aspirations and goals.

Under the proposed services chapter, foreign for-profit health, education and other social service corporations would have the right to establish a "commercial presence" anywhere in Canada - as well as the "right" to compete for public dollars with existing hospitals, schools and day care centres. It's a formula designed to ensure foreign for-profit corporations grow richer at the expense of shrinking public budgets, declining public institutions and, ultimately, the right of Canadians to universally available quality health care.[38]

 

A document by the global environmental NGO Greenpeace displays a similar use of language.

The FTAA may well include provisions on investor-state rights similar to the North American Free Trade Agreement�s infamous Chapter 11. These provisions allow corporations the right to sue governments if measures to protect the environment or human health impact on corporate profits. �The Canadian government is on the hook under NAFTA for up to $50 million because it implemented measures consistent with the Basel Convention on toxic waste,� says Jo Dufay. �It is a fundamental error that NAFTA makes it too expensive to live up to environmental measures � and that error should not be repeated across the Americas. We want safe trade � not free trade. We must protect our right as citizens to safeguard our air, water and food supply.�[39]

 

While the existence of a movement of resistance is potentially a positive and progressive development, the language and ethic guiding this movement is ultimately self-defeating.� In articulating their aspirations and objectives using the language and values of rights, these organizations are speaking the same language as their oppressors, and thus risk losing the opportunity of voicing a genuine challenge to the hegemonic values and the policies they entail.� While the question of who actually co-opted whose language is open to debate, the point is, ultimately, moot; what matters is that the voices from global civil society will become indistinguishable from the voices of states and corporations, as both sides use the rhetoric of rights to articulate their goals.

 

Advocates of the rights discourse, and of the transformative power of �new� social movement, would contest this view.� Neil Stammers has argued that �the construction and use of human rights discourses by social movements can play an important and positive role in challenging relations and structures of power�.[40]� While he admits that, in certain forms and in certain contexts, human rights actually sustain power rather than challenging it, he argues that this is likely to be the case when rights are in their �institutionalized/legal form�.� Conversely, in their �preinstitutionalized, nonlegal form, we can see claims for human rights most evidently challenging relations and structures of power�.� In this sense, social movements construct claims for human rights as part of their challenge to the status quo.[41]

 

Certainly, Stammers� argument deserves careful consideration.� He rightly points out that ideas and practices in respect of human rights are very much implicated in the processes of globalization.� He admits that many proponents of human rights, especially in the West/North, continue to defend a narrowly defined conception of human rights as civil and political rights.[42]� However, his willingness to reject the state�s actual or potential role in supplying basic material needs for its citizens, and his attendant faith in social movements and their capacity to renew and re-invigorate the challenge to economic power demonstrates a blindness to the situations of many developing states, and especially to the poorest of the poor in those states � women.� Moreover, his claim that the use of rights discourse �challenges dominant ideas of �common sense� and (is) counter-hegemonic in respect of such power� misses the point that the concept of �right� cannot escape its liberal meaning� -- of an individualistic, negative, �skeleton� concept which upholds the individual�s right to pursue his own understanding of the good.

 

The following section will explore these arguments in greater detail by focusing on the nature and development of the human rights discourse as a lingua franca for the anti-globalization movements of global civil society.� I will argue that, despite the contemporary evolution of human rights to include economic, social and cultural rights, as well as �women�s human rights�, it remains an exclusionary discourse which relies on Western-centric and masculinist notions of ethics, politics and �human� nature.

 

 

First and Second Generation Rights

 

Certainly, the idea of human rights has been extremely influential around the world since the latter half of the twentieth century � so much so, indeed, that �human rights� has become as close to a universal idea as is, perhaps, possible.�� But the contemporary global reach of human rights belies their very particular historical origin; indeed, the notion that individuals might have some rights against the state is an idea that was born out of a particular political philosophy of liberty and the nature of authority which emerged in England, France and America in the eighteenth century.� While it may legitimately be claimed that non-Western cultures have, today, their own notions of rights, it cannot be denied that the dominant conception of rights � which informs the views of the UN and other international organizations � is based on the ideas of these early liberals, and is, today, influenced by contemporary Western neo-liberal ideology.� It is this universalist language of fundamental human rights which is widely used by global social movements to articulate what they perceive to be a commonly accepted, even universal, set of values.�� The deep contradiction that lies within this discourse, however, rests on the particularly Western and gendered nature of both human rights language and the values which support them.�

 

 

Supporters of human rights would undoubtedly make at least one of two counterarguments.� First, they would point out that to claim that human rights are Western and masculinist is to take a narrow view of rights and to ignore the extent to which notions of rights have become more expansive and inclusive during the last half of the twentieth century.� In particular, they would point to the growth and increased acceptance of economic, social and cultural rights, and to the recognition of women�s human rights.� Second, defenders of human rights might also suggest that, in spite of the �flaws� of human rights language, political and social circumstances make it necessary, or compelling, as well as expedient, for civic associations to make use of the discourse of human rights in articulating their goals.[43]

 

On the first argument, it is undeniable that there is widespread and growing recognition of economic, social and cultural rights.� Certainly, the UN Charter is comprised of two human rights covenants:� one on Civil and Political Rights, and one on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights.� Thus, in international law, they have equal status.� Moreover, many non-Western countries, including the Soviet Union and its allies during the Cold War, as well as many ex-colonial states, have since 1948 demanded greater recognition of economic and social rights.�� While this was, especially during the Cold War, largely reflective of ideological differences, it also, to some extent, indicated genuine differences in values and cultural norms.� Moreover, while the states of the West, as well as most Western human rights NGOs, have tended to prioritize � again, especially during the Cold War -- civil and political rights over economic, social and cultural rights, there is no doubt that some progress in the recognition of the latter has been made.� While this change has been slow in terms of states, one of the most visible and important transformations can be found in the changing mandate of Amnesty International.�

 

From August 17 to 25, 2001, the International Council meeting (ICM), the highest policy-making body of Amnesty International, met in Dakar, Senegal. Among the 400 delegates were representatives from all sections, guests representing some national structures that are on the way to becoming sections, members of the International Executive Committee (IEC), other members of AI's volunteer leadership, and selected staff from the International Secretariat.� The Wire, Amnesty�s Magazine, reports the major decision of the meeting as follows:

After much debate it was agreed that the statute which defines AI's mandate should be changed to say that AI's work is focused on preventing and ending grave abuses of the rights to physical and mental integrity, freedom of conscience and expression, and freedom from discrimination, within the context of promoting all human rights. This unanimous decision clearly lays the basis for AI responding to a much broader range of human rights concerns than it has in the past. It enables us to tackle some abuses of economic, social and cultural rights, and to focus more effectively on the main human rights concerns in different countries, unconstrained by the limits of the previous mandate.[44]

The new Secretary General of Amnesty, Irene Khan, defended the decision as follows:

 

At its heart, Amnesty International is and remains a movement fighting for individuals whose rights are violated. The difference is that from now on, we will work, not only against torture or for prisoners of conscience, but against all forms of discrimination, whether they affect political and civil rights or economic, social and cultural rights.


We will be able to act more effectively and with a broader approach to the realities of human rights in the 21st century. Promoting the right of all persons to enjoy all human rights, Amnesty International will have the potential now to engage in new areas, such as the right to education or health.[45]


Critics have responded to this move by Amnesty by using the same arguments that have been used against economic, social and cultural rights since their inception.� The Economist has argued recently, in a Special Report on Human Rights, that �(t)he moral imperative to stop poverty and disease is � not as convincing as the moral imperative to stop torture�.� Moreover, the report suggests that while guaranteeing civil and political rights is �relatively cheap�, to guarantee economic and social rights is potentially �enormously costly�.� It is also argued that any attempt to guarantee economic and social rights would lead to the condemnation of some nations simply �because of their poverty�, while other nations would be arraigned for the policy outcomes of decisions taken democratically; thus, in the latter case, economic rights could be seen as conflicting with political rights.[46]� Above all, however, is the argument that by expanding their mandate, human rights watchdogs run the risk of tainting their defence of basic liberties with an overtly political platform on social and economic policy.[47]

 

Many of these arguments are completely without merit.� It is both philosophically vacuous and morally abhorrent to suggest that the moral imperative to stop poverty and disease is not as convincing as the imperative to stop torture.�� The type of agency involved in bringing these violations has no bearing on the gravity of the situation.� Indeed, from a utilitarian perspective, it could be argued that the imperative to stop or reduce poverty and related diseases, by far the biggest killer of human beings, is the most pressing there is.� It is also wrong to suggest that to guarantee civil and political rights is �relatively cheap�; the establishment of a truly accountable, representative and participatory democracy involves investment in more than voting booths, ballots and pencils.� Moreover, it is certainly the case that the �enormous cost� of economic welfare is not prohibitive � in the sense that there being absolutely insufficient resources -- but rather a matter of political decisions and priorities, in terms of the domestic and global allocation and distribution of goods and resources, and entitlements to those goods and resources.

�

While I would reject these traditional liberal arguments which prioritize civil and political liberties over economic and social welfare, I would still argue that the idea of economic and social rights is philosophically and politically flawed, and that the addition of these rights cannot overcome the difficulties associated with the human rights discourse.� I have argued elsewhere that so-called rights to an adequate standard of living, or to health, are not statements of right, which are statements about the rules and procedures which allow the space to pursue a plurality of goods.� Thus, the right to free speech, for example, is a statement about the basic rules which allow for individuals to pursue their own individual ends.� This is where the concept of right, limited though it may be, is of most use.� It is, however, of little use if we are seeking to articulate the positive entitlements that out to be accessible to human beings.[48]� Positive entitlements require, at the very least, conceptions of the nature and scope of obligations; better still, they demand attention to and problematization of patterns of responsibility and care within and among nation-states, communities and families.� In this sense, they are essentially political, in that positive entitlements to a minimum decent standard of living � including food, shelter, health care and education � are the result of debate and dialogue about individual and social responsibilities in the contexts of families, communities and states.

 

Supporters of economic and social rights would likely respond to this by claiming that it is unimportant; what matters, it could be argued, is that the use of �rights talk� may be what is required to shake the governments of the rich world into action to respond to the crises of poverty and ill-health in the South.��� Michael Ignatieff makes this point when he claims that, �the rights argument is a way to leverage money to fulfil this obligation�.[49]� Defenders of rights �rhetoric� argue that it provides a mechanism for �reanalyzing and renaming �problems� as �violations� and, as such, something that need not and should not be tolerated�.[50]� Even those who are critical of the human rights discourse recognize its strategic and practical potential, arguing that �rejecting the use of a human rights discourse can result in a position of political nihilism, without the potential for alternative action�.[51]

 

This argument is also evident in the claims of advocates of economic and social rights, who argue that the only way to ensure the observation of these rights is to hold not only states, but non-state actors, legally accountable.�� Chris Jochnick of the Centre for Economic and Social Rights argues that

(d)efining a human right as such allows for a process of developing reciprocal duties, monitoring conduct, and holding actors accountable.� Codes of conduct, policy directives, and legislation must be tied to the larger framework of human rights in order to ensure the positive and integrated contribution of these legal obligations to human development.� Furthermore, tying legal obligations to human rights imbues these laws with the necessary sense that rights and obligations derive from human dignity, and not generosity or whim.[52]

 

While this push to move beyond a state-centrism is certainly a step forward, I would respond by insisting that �the rights argument�, however enlarged, remains flawed both as a moral discourse and a legal strategy.�� Far from being the only moral language capable of generating a response, human rights will always remain inexorably tied to a liberal notion of negative liberty which will always be seen by some to be threatened by the push to satisfy positive entitlements to food, health and education. Certainly, the concept of right can and does articulate a moral imperative which is almost universally understood; but it cannot help us to get much beyond that assertion, and that is ultimately what is necessary to attend to the poverty that globalization leaves in its wake.

Moreover, as Tony Evans has argued, the international law framework in which human rights are located has limited potential for guaranteeing human well-being:

Human rights becomes a technical issue concerned with agreements and disagreements over the internal logic and elegance of the law, its coherence, extent and meaning.� More complex questions to do with efficacy, application and obligations under the particular social, political, and economic conditions of globalization are thus excluded.[53]

 

This critique is in no way meant to suggest that rights and justice are not important; nor is it to say that the priorities which lead to demands for economic welfare should, or will always remain, secondary to political rights.� On the contrary, the struggle for individual liberty and rights is crucial, especially in societies which are currently subjected to oppressive and exclusive regimes where basic freedoms were denied based on race, religion or ethnicity.� Moreover, as I have already suggested, the need to ensure that individuals have adequate entitlements to food, water, shelter, health care and education is of paramount importance.� I regard this as not open to debate.� What is debatable, however, is the nature of the discourse which surrounds this imperative, and the way in which this discourse translates into policy and practice.

 

The move by Amnesty International to include economic and social rights within their mandate is clearly a recognition that to focus exclusively only individual cases of torture or political persecution, important though it may be, is to address on the most minute fraction of human suffering in the world.� Moreover, it is likely also a recognition that while globalization may have brought with it the spread of �democracy� to many previously undemocratic parts of the world, it has also brought with it massively heightened disparities between rich and poor, both among and within countries.�� The move to widen their mandate is clearly motivated by causes of the utmost importance; the ethical and political justification of this move, however, is not so easily defended.

 

Women�s Human Rights

The systematic exclusion of women from the political theory, ethics and law of human rights is now well-documented.�� Most commonly, it is argued that so-called �human� rights are, in theory and in practice, �men�s� rights.� As a consequence, exclusions, constraints and abuses more typical of women�s lives are neither recognized nor protected by human rights instruments.[54]� While there are many explanations offered for the gendering of �universal� human rights, most can be traced back to the public/private dichotomy first advanced by the early liberals of the 18th c. � theorists who were, not surprisingly, the authors of the historical antecedents to our contemporary Western understanding of human rights.� Ursula O�Hare suggests that along these lines, two main arguments have emerged:

One argument suggests that the failure of the human rights system to reach women results from the deference it maintains towards the private sphere � human rights law privileges the public world while the private sphere is considered outside the scope of legal regulation.� Others argue that the myth of nonintervention in the private sphere, which has grown up in human rights discourse, simply masks the gendered application of human rights law. � In either case, both theories argue that women�s issues have not been taken seriously by the human rights community.[55]

 

As many authors have noted, the problematic nature of the public/private dichotomy is most starkly demonstrated by the issue of violence.� From the systematic rape of women in the former Yugoslavia, to the less visible, but more pervasive �intimate� violence committed against women in the home by their partners or other family members, violence against women has not been perceived as a human rights issue.[56]

 

Defenders of human rights would likely point to the enormous ground that has been gained in the struggle to ensure that women�s human rights are recognized in both theory and practice.�� When the UN�s Commission on Human Rights failed to recognize women�s aspirations adequately, women delegates and the NGOs that supported them managed to obtain a freestanding Commission on the Status of Women (CSW).� By 1979, the CSW, with the support of women delegates and NGOs and a new wave of feminism underway, had drafted and successfully lobbied the adoption of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women.� By 1980, the Convention became an international women�s human rights treaty.[57]

 

Certainly, it is without question that a vast number of conceptual and practical developments have been achieved which have made a vast contribution to enhancing awareness of women�s human rights.� But as was the case with economic and social rights, it is likely that the ethical basis of the concept of human rights has not yet, and may never be, sufficiently transformed to enable women�s roles and concerns to be taken seriously.

 

In their article on global civil society and women�s human rights in Latin America, Laura Macdonald and Cathy Blacklock argue that, in their recent engagement with the issues of domestic and sexual violence in the private sphere and the socioeconomic violence of the feminization of impoverishment perpetrated in the public sphere, women�s organizations in Mexico and Guatemala have come face to face with the gendered silence of the discourse of human rights.� Moreover, in the case of Mexico, the authors explore how indigenous organizations are encountering the limits to the �use-value� of the discourse of human rights to recognize and support specificity and difference.[58]

 

Interestingly, while recognizing these deficiencies, the authors remain persuaded by the discursive force of human rights.� They rightly point out that, limited though it may be, international and domestic events have pushed popular sector activists to adopt a discourse of universal human rights as part of their citizenship demands.� In other words, women and indigenous organizations are actually using this discourse; thus, our best course of action is to search for alternative conceptions of human rights, so that difference, subjectivity and specificity can be �incorporated rather than silenced in struggles for political inclusion and effective participation�[59].

 

Clearly, the idea of simply jettisoning the discourse of human rights seems untenable many feminists � from academics to activists.� I would argue, however, that the concept would have to be altered beyond recognition to be of any real value in the transformation of women�s lives around the world.� As Kim Hutchings has argued, the idea of human rights is premised on the recognition that in crucial respects, human beings are the same.� If instead we point to gender differences in the meanings of a human right, we are exposing the fact that global ethical life is structured by and through gendered relations of power.� Once this is appreciated, she argues, the strategic value as well as the ethical significance of drawing on a rights discourse in order to protect women�s interests changes.[60]���

At the deeper level of ethical significance the strategic necessity of grounding rights on difference can be understood � as putting into question the grounding of fundamental human rights in humanity as such � rights become conceptualized always as a strategic weapon in the construction of a form of ethical life which is no more ethically necessary, though for many (including many feminists) it may be preferable, than any other.� In such an ethics the notion of human rights cannot act as an ethical trump card.� Instead, specific human rights must be interrogated and judged in terms of the ways in which they function in the broader values, structures and institutions of world politics.[61]

 

The key point here is worth restating.�� It must be recognized that to call for �women�s human rights� is to challenge and undermine the foundation of human rights � the notion of �humanity as such�.� It is, therefore, to undermine their claim to �ethical necessity� � which is the basis of their claim to be universal, absolute, inalienable �trump cards�.� It could be argued that, once this is undermined, the moral and political force of rights is lost.� Hutchings, however, argues that their force becomes radically rethought;� rights become, as she states above, strategic weapons in the construction of a form of ethical life which is not absolutely and universally good, but which, hopefully, is preferable at least for many, if not all women, at a particular moment in time and space.� I would argue, however, that thus understood, one is no longer working with a concept of rights.� While rights may still exist, the moral ideas which are actually being used to benefit the lives of women are not rights � rather, they are a diverse set of ethical concepts and strategies which are contextual and contingent.

 

Clearly, the work that has been done by academics and activists to expand and reconstruct international human rights to include the specific vulnerabilities and concerns of women has been, and continues to be, extremely valuable.� That said, when anti-globalization activists frame their challenge using the unexamined discourse of rights, they draw on a totalizing ethical discourse which, so constructed, fails to address the needs of most women.� If rights are to be used successfully at all, the entire foundation of them must be overturned.� This may or may not be possible, given the entrenchment of universal, Western notions of human right in the international context today.�� In the context of the challenge to globalization, it may be that the totalizing discourse of rights must give way not to another universal language, but to a wide variety of norms and ethical practices that can speak to women and to non-Westerners, especially in the �developing� world.

 

Non-totalizing ethics and globalization

The feminist understanding of human rights described above provides just one example of how our understanding of �the ethical� is transformed from the position of feminist ethics.� Feminist ethics represents a relatively new intervention into the fields of moral and political philosophy, normative international relations theory, and now, public policy.� Certainly, there have been many controversies surrounding the nature and epistemological grounding of feminist ethics and its ability to translate successfully into �public� life.� That said, in its short life-span, feminist understandings of morality as contextual and concrete relations of care � understood as attentiveness and responsiveness to the needs of others � has had a significant impact on moral theory and is beginning to enter the arena of policy-making.

 

Kimberly Hutchings provides a conceptualization of feminist ethics which contrasts radically with traditional human rights.� She argues that feminist ethics must avoid any attempt to advocate values associated with the private sphere as in some way �better� than the values of rights or justice.� The reason for this is not associated with familiar arguments about stereotyping and essentializing the �feminine�; rather, it is based on an epistemological argument � in particular, a desire to avoid the kind of totalizing, prescriptive ethical arguments which feminists were engaged in criticizing.� Thus, for feminist ethics, she argues in favour of the more modest claim that the �moral ontology of relations of recognition and responsibility which is identified within the private sphere is the key to understanding �moral substance� as such�.� Thus, a feminist approach to ethics is fundamentally about ontology � what exists � and the related claim that this ontology provides both a background and a set of conceptual and analytical tools for making sense of ethics, even in the global context.[62]�

 

Furthermore, Hutchings argues that power, especially gendered relations of power, is a crucial facet of this �reality� in which these relations and practices of care are embedded. In particular, she claims that

Feminist ethicists find ethical significance in those gendered aspects of international ethical reality which, in being presented as necessary, are either not �seen� at all or are seen as unquestionable.[63]�

 

The prescriptions following from this view of feminist ethics will vary depending on context; theorists must, she argues, �take responsibility for articulating the conditions within which any prescriptions made are meaningful and therefore the kind of world they imply.�� Indeed, there is only one prescription that would be common to the practice of feminist ethics:� always be skeptical of any kind of moral essentialism or claims to ethical necessity.�[64]

 

Thus, the task of ethical inquiry and critique has two parts to it.� First, it involves recognizing the ubiquity of moral practices which do not conform to the universalist,� rights-based model of �justice ethics�.� Second, and related to this, it involves recognizing that power relations � particularly related to gender, but also to race, religion and ethnicity � are the context in which all types of international social relations are embedded.� With regard to the first part, it may be important to note that, in an era of globalization, while the welfare state is experiencing a profound crisis, care remains a hidden yet ever-present part of people�s everyday lives, in both �developed� and �developing� countries.� Whereas the modern welfare state was understood in terms of a single model � where a central administration collected taxes for the redistribution of wealth and social goods � care can be and is understood and practiced in a variety of different ways.� While much time and energy has been (usefully) expended by feminist theorists in demonstrating that care is not simply a private ethics, that is not to say that it is not, nor cannot be, private.� Care � as both a moral response and a practice � occurs daily among family members, friends and loved ones.� It is also, as it has been for years, an integral part of remaining welfare functions in many nation-states; health care workers, social workers, child caregivers, hospice workers use public funds to administer care to those who are � as we all are at one time in our lives � needy and vulnerable.� Care is also, and perhaps increasingly so as a result of globalization and marketization, a private function � where private is understood in the economic, rather than the familial and personal sense of the word.� Care occurs at all levels � the family and private spheres, and the public spheres at the local, national and global levels.� Moreover, it occurs within social relations of all types � personal relations, marketized relations, and state-level relations, either within or among states.�

 

Care is an ethic and a practice which can help to create bonds among individuals and groups within and across borders.� That said, however, care cannot simply provide an alternative to globalization or to the state.� The feminist language of �care� is not a universalist prescription for global ethics; moreover, social bonds cannot simply be transferred to the global level.� In spite of proclamations of global village, global civil society, and even the �global state�, social bonds among �citizens of the world� do not exist in any obvious way � especially not as a direct result of globalization.� However, recognizing the ubiquity and diversity of caring practices can provide us with a moral and discursive framework for imaging possible futures that challenge the logic of globalization.� Importantly, however, this is not based on the primacy of �care� as a universal value, but simply a recognition that caring practices exist everywhere.[65]�

Even this simple recognition, I would argue, can provide a challenge to the prevailing view of globalization as �common sense' and inevitable.� In demonstrating that caring practices are everywhere around us, the received worldview � of acquisitiveness, consumerism, and individualism � is contested.� But the �alternative� to globalization is not associated with any particular realm, such as a wholesale return to the state, or a wholesale embrace of global civil society.� Moreover, using this approach to ethics, the discursive strategy is not to utilize the lingua franca of rights already used by states, corporations and institutions of global governance, nor indeed any other totalizing discourse of ethical imperatives.

 

Conclusion

This paper has sought to examine the discursive and ethical construction of rights as a basis for the challenge to globalization by �global civil society�.� In particular, I have argued, along with many feminist and post-colonial theorists, that �human rights� is a Western-centric and gendered concept with pretensions to universality.� As such, it fits comfortably, at the present stage, with the general ideology and goals not only of the advocates of neo-liberal globalization, but also of �global civil society�.� Advocates of global civil society as a �political project� readily admit that it is driven by values which originated in the West (but which are now purportedly �global�) is comprised largely of Western groups and individuals.[66]� They admit that �large parts of Africa or Latin America are virtually excluded from globalised activities�, and that those who support civic internationalist values are �the cosmopolitan young people� who work in international organizations or the media, or who join Greenpeace or Amnesty International, or who offer �voluntary contributions� to �cosmopolitan causes�.� The �courageous territorially tied people� who work in local institutions are said to be �equally important�, yet there is a strong suggestion that these people remain �behind�, still locked in the �nationalist and exclusivist� ideologies of yesteryear, and as yet insufficiently tuned in to the dynamics of transnational values and activism.[67]

 

Human rights is the obvious discursive framework for this �political project�, which is modernist, Western-centric, and gendered in its approach.� It is, paradoxically, also the logical discursive framework for the neo-liberals and indeed all advocates of greater economic liberalization and globalization, who seek �open�, stable political societies as a basis for open markets.� Critics of this comparison would argue, of course, that neo-liberals remain focused on a narrow definition of rights which limits them to civil and political rights; global civil society, and in particular the anti-globalization movement, it could be argued, reject this narrow definition in favour of a broader definition of rights which encompasses economic, social and cultural rights as well as �women�s human rights�.

 

I have argued, however, that the ethical and discursive construction of rights is not sufficiently elastic to accommodate these moves.� Rights remains a concept which is tied to the individual and which articulates his freedoms from intervention, primarily from the state.� As such it is a skeleton moral concept, not in the sense that what it articulates is basic or �fundamental�, but in the sense that it is about the procedure that must be followed for individuals to pursue and enjoy freely their own conception of the good.� It lacks the ability to �flesh out�, or put features on that skeleton.� Economic and social� welfare is about that flesh and those features � human goods rather than human rights.�� Fundamentally different moral and discursive concepts to elucidate the moral imperatives surrounding physical and spiritual nourishment, health care, education and cultural expression.

 

Moreover, the notion of human rights is based on a moral foundation of universality, which cannot accompany the feminist requirement of the recognition of difference.� Human rights cannot escape the requirement of universality; to do so would be to undermine the concept entirely.� Indeed, the voices have global civil society are uninterested in doing this; on the contrary, they embrace universality in the form of �transnational� or �cosmopolitan� values.� But it is far from evident that the values of democracy, human rights and environmental stability � articulated as they are by Western NGOs � are in any sense universal.

 

In no way is this argument meant to degrade or disparage the moral and political progress that has been achieved through the discursive and legal power of human rights; nor is it to overlook the tremendous role played by social movements and non-governmental organizations � both in the West and, more quietly, at the grassroots, community-based level in the South and East � in combating exclusion and hierarchy in the effort to achieve more just and equitable societies.� Rather, my quarrel is with the unreflective acceptance of the �global� in global civil society, and the assumption that human rights can provide a discursive, ethical and political strategy for combating globalization.� In an era when there is a proliferation of new kinds or rights and new rights holders, and an increasingly widespread use of the discourse, we must explore the possibilities of alternative discursive and ethical categories to offer real challenges to globalization.
