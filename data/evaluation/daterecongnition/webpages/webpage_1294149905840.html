Monday, August 21, 2006 Source: Waxahachie Daily Light
Author: Jennifer Howell Kelley
 
Combat veteran shares Iraq experience

U.S. Marine veteran Eric Smith was the special guest speaker at the monthly meeting of the Ellis County Republican Women on Tuesday night.

A lifetime resident of Waxahachie, Smith told of his experiences in Iraq as a Marine, his belief about the situation in Iraq and why he supports Congressman Joe Barton.

Smith told of his involvement in FFA as a high school student at Waxahachie High School and of his 2001 graduation.

“I was one of five kids,” said Smith. “With five kids and all of us so close in age, there was not enough money to send us all to college.”

Smith said this prompted him to visit a recruiter while his parents were out of town. Smith joined the Marines, in part, as a way to pay for college.

“Three days after I graduated from high school, I went to boot camp in San Diego,” said Smith.

When Smith was home on leave in August 2001, he proposed to his now-wife. Engaged to be married in December 2001, Smith was off to infantry training in September 2001.

“I entered infantry training at 8 a.m. on Sept. 11, 2001,” Smith said. “We were told, ‘Pay attention to your training, you’re going to need it.’ We took training very seriously because we knew it may save our lives.”

Smith’s wedding took place at Ferris Avenue Baptist Church in December 2001 and he waited to be sent to Iraq.

“We all thought we were going to war,” said Smith, who spent from July 2002-July 2003 in Okinawa before being sent to Iraq.

Now a corporal and in charge of 12 Marines, Smith went to Kuwait on Feb. 15, 2004, and moved into Iraq in mid-March.

“Our first night in Iraq, a rocket was shot at our vehicle, a grenade was thrown at us,” said Smith, who also reported a firefight with insurgents that same night.

“Three days after we arrived in Iraq, our executive officer was hit by a roadside bomb,” said Smith, saying that officer now only has partial use of one of his arms.

Smith’s battalion of just fewer than 1,000 Marines lost 35 of its men during the course of the following eight months.

In his speech, Smith told of how tough it is for those in the military to come back home and readjust to a normal life and of how he was wounded in Iraq in May 2004.

“I lost hearing in one ear, broke my jaw and I have metal in my face,” said Smith, who urged the importance of expressing thanks and gratitude on a daily basis to those who are serving in Iraq.

He spoke of the severe poverty in Iraq, how he spent time repainting and rebuilding schools, and how his family at home sent school supplies to him in Iraq to give to the schools.

“Most military really want to help,” Smith said. “We’re not there (in Iraq) as conquerors. We’re not there to occupy.”

Smith’s battalion was in charge of Ar Ramadi, a city of about 500,000 in population. “We’re the governor, the police, the ambulance,” Smith said, listing the military’s responsibilities. “What we do is extremely challenging.”

In a ceremony that took place Feb. 19, 2006, Smith was awarded a Silver Star for his courage in an incident that took place April 6, 2004.

Smith listed the reasons why he chooses to “work for Congressman Barton,” including the fact that Barton “has served us admirably for 22 years.”

“A withdraw from Iraq would not only be a disgrace to those who have given all for their country, it would be irresponsible,” Smith said. “One thing we can not do is give up or quit.

“Barton believes the time line of war should be decided by the troops,” said Smith, who said this is not something that should be decided by Congressmen or people not in Iraq fighting.

“I appreciate that as a veteran,” said Smith of Barton’s beliefs and his stand on the issues.

Following his speech to the group, Smith answered questions from the members and recommended the book, “No True Glory,” by Bing West.
