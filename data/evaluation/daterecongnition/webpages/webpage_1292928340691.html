Honda Road Racerhead #13

March 26, 2010 by CJ  
Filed under Road Racerhead

5 Comments      

This weekend’s Suzuki AMA Pro Superbike Challenge at Auto Club Speedway is the second event in a ten-round AMA Pro Road Racing series, but it’s also the first event in a separate, unofficial three-round series: the California AMA Pro Road Racing series. Round 2 in that series is at Sonoma’s Infineon Raceway on May 14-16, and the finale is at Monterey’s Mazda Raceway Laguna Seca on July 23-25, in conjunction with the Red Bull U.S. Grand Prix weekend. The math is pretty straightforward: California—the only state to have more than one tour-stop—hosts 30 percent of the AMA Pro Road Racing rounds this season.

This weekend’s Auto Club Speedway event is one of three AMA Pro Road Racing rounds in California. • Riles/Nelson photo

For whatever reason, Auto Club Speedway has struggled to draw motorcycle fans. That’s a shame and hopefully, area readers will consider attending the race this weekend, as there are plenty of compelling reasons to do so, as anyone who was actually at Daytona three weeks ago is likely to attest. For what it’s worth, the venue has even struggled with mighty NASCAR; last month’s Round 2 at the track claimed an official 72,000 spectators, but pundits put the number closer to 56,000. Granted, that’s a decent number by motorcycle-race standards, but Kansas Speedway, for example, gets over 90,000. As a result, that facility will get a second NASCAR round next year, and many expect it to come at the expense of Auto Club Speedway’s second race. I feel bad for Gillian Zucker, who runs the track, but I also feel like a single, strong event can be preferable to multiple, weak races.

That said, I’m just fine with the trio of California AMA Pro rounds, and not just because I’m a native and resident of the Golden State. Each of these three events makes sense in a lot of ways. Fontana’s attendance issues notwithstanding, so much of the industry is based in Southern California that any legitimate national championship really has to have an annual race there, and Auto Club Speedway is the best circuit in the area. Infineon Raceway, 450 miles to the north, is one of the country’s best road race circuits, and its owners have proven their commitment to the sport be continuously investing in safety-related improvements and creatively and enthusiastically promoting their race. Laguna is just 160 miles south of Sonoma, but the Bay Area is perhaps the country’s strongest sport bike community. Besides, that AMA Pro round is really just support for that weekend’s Red Bull U.S. Grand Prix, where the MotoGP boys do battle.

Through its strong event promotion and dedication to facility improvements, Infineon Raceway has earned its slot on the AMA Pro schedule. • Riles/Nelson photo

By the way, this trio of circuits joins other California circuits in the AMA Pro history book. Past Golden State AMA road race venues include Rosamond’s Willow Springs Raceway, Riverside, and Pomona.

California is a bit of an enigma when it comes to drawing spectators to sporting events, and it’s hard to pinpoint the elusive best formula. Really, you have to treat the Bay Area and the Los Angeles basin as two separate entities (much as Golden State residents do). Yes, they’re in the same state, but as noted above, they’re separated by a pretty long drive; though Barber Motorsports Park and Road Atlanta are in separate states, for example, they’re about as close to one another as are Bay Area tracks Infineon and Laguna.

In stick and ball sports, California has long supported multiple teams. In the NBA, the Lakers and Clippers share the Los Angeles area, while the Golden State Warriors and Sacramento Kings play up north; in Major League Baseball, it’s the Dodgers and Angels in L.A. and Orange Counties, respectively, the Padres in San Diego, and the Giants and the Athletics in the Bay Area; in the NHL, you’ve got the Kings and the Ducks sharing SoCal, with the Sharks up in San Jose; the NFL has the Raiders and the 49ers in Northern California, and although Los Angeles hasn’t hosted a team since the Rams and Raiders both left town in ’95, San Diego is the longtime home to the Chargers.

Mazda Raceway Laguna Seca is the third Golden State slot on the AMA Pro calendar, although most of the spectators come for the MotoGP race. • Andrew Northcott photo

It’s also worth pointing out that AMA Pro Road Racing isn’t the only major motorcycle racing series to schedule more than one round in California. Monster Energy Supercross has long held three rounds per year at Angels Stadium (in addition to races in San Francisco and San Diego), all of which typically do quite well (with a seventeen-race schedule, that’s about the same proportion of California races as in AMA Pro Road Racing). Also, the Lucas Oil AMA Pro Motocross Championship has rounds in Sacramento and San Bernardino.

World championship racing has similar instances of hosting multiple rounds in one place, only we’re talking countries in this case (not states). One example is MotoGP, which has two races in the U.S.—Laguna and Indianapolis—although at 2,300 miles apart, those two races are further from one another than are most of the events in Europe. The big eyebrow-raiser is Spain, which this year hosts no fewer than four Grand Prix rounds! Jerez, Barcelona, and Valencia are longtime GP venues, and last week saw Alcaniz’s new Motorland Aragon added to the schedule as a replacement for Hungary’s Balatonring circuit, which might not be ready in time. That’s over 22 percent of a so-called international series’ races being held in its owner’s home country!

ANAHEIM: Anaheim’s Angels Stadium hosts no fewer than three different Monster Energy Supercross rounds every season. • Simon Cudby photo

And unlike the U.S., Spain isn’t particularly large. Of the four races mentioned, the two that are farthest apart are Catalunya and Jerez, at 690 miles, while Aragon and Valencia are separated by just 145 miles! Not only that, but the Estoril round in Portugal is just 360 miles from Jerez.

Again, though, there’s some logic to the schedule. Spain is absolutely fanatical about Grand Prix racing, to the extent that it’s home to two full-color, glossy, weekly bike magazines, and their races typically draw huge, passionate crowds. This is a business, after all, and if the country can support four Grand Prix rounds, then maybe that’s fine. In many ways, it’s preferable to putting on too many races in countries that don’t have a motorsports culture. (I’m fine with MotoGP going to Qatar and Sepang, for example, but I think Formula 1 overdoes it with its international expansion; this year’s F1 calendar has races in Bahrain, Malaysia, China, Turkey, Hungary, Singapore, Korea, and Abu Dhabi, with only seven rounds in Old World European venues.)

This season, the brand-new Motorland Aragon circuit becomes the fourth Spanish venue hosting a MotoGP round. • Courtesy Motorland Aragon

One traditional way the series promoters have sought to make these scenarios less obvious has been to get creative with the event names. For example, Jerez is the official Spanish Grand Prix (technically, it’s the “Grand Premio Bwin de Espana”; the other three races are all named after cities or regions: “Gran Premi Aperol de Catalunya,” “Gran Premio de Aragon,” and “Gran Premio Generali de la Comunitat Valenciana.” That’s also true of Italy and the U.S.: Mugello (“Gran Premio d’Italia TIM”) is the official Italian GP, whereas Misano (“G.P. Aperol di San Marino e della Riviera di Rimini”) is supposedly the official race of the Republic of San Marino (an independent, landlocked enclave, completely surrounded by Italy), despite the fact that the track is about 15 miles outside of San Marino’s border! (The same Misano-San Marino trick is employed by World Superbike, whose owners are Italian and which hosts three Italian rounds, at Monza, Misano, and Imola.)
And only Laguna is the true “U.S. Grand Prix,” whereas Indy is the “Red Bull Indianapolis GP.”

The Grand Prix series has held “European” rounds at Jarama and Catalunya; “FIM” rounds at Jarama; “Nations” rounds at Monza, Imola, Mugello, and Misano; “Pacific” rounds at Motegi; and of course “Tourist Trophy” rounds at the Isle of Man.

Although Misano World Circuit is in Italy, both MotoGP and World Superbike refer to it as their San Marino round. • Andrew Northcott photo

Multiple Grand Prix rounds in single countries have been a part of the Grand Prix series for years. The last year that there was a premier-class series with races in all different countries was 1990, when the calendar visited Japan, the U.S., Spain, Italy, West Germany, Austria, Yugoslavia, The Netherlands, Belgium, France, Great Britain, Sweden, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, and Australia—each only one time. As for Spain, it has hosted at least one premier-class Grand Prix since 1981, although it did host 125 and 250cc rounds that year, in Jarama. (Jarama is one of two Spanish tracks to have hosted Grand Prix rounds, not including the four at which races will be held this year; the other is the beautiful but dangerous public-roads course on a hill above Barcelona—Montjuich. That circuit joined the series in only its third year, in 1951, when it hosted the first-ever Spanish Grand Prix.)

While promoters have to be cognizant of the danger of their series being tagged as something smaller than they supposedly are (i.e., MotoGP being called a Spanish championship), the real bottom line when planning schedules—especially in this economy—is the financial one. AMA Pro obviously feels that California can adequately host three different races, MotoGP feels likewise about Spain, and World Superbike does with Italy. To help toward that end, in the absence of geographical distance, they’ve attempted to provide chronological space; to use MotoGP as an example, there are two-month gaps between three of the races, with one-and-a-half months between Aragon and Valencia. That last one is a bit tight, especially considering the two tracks are only about a two-hour drive from one another, but when you’ve only got less than seven months between your series opener and finale, there’s only so much you can do.

Though there are two Italian MotoGP stops, Mugello is the true national round (both officially and in terms of prestige and turnout). • Andrew Northcott photo

All of which is a very long way of saying that if you live in California, I hope you’ll consider attending this weekend’s AMA Pro race, though I have to admit that this is a classic case of doing as I say and not as I do. That’s right, despite the fact that I reside just down Interstate 91 from Fontana, I won’t be at Auto Club Speedway. I’m posting this from Hawaii, where I’m celebrating my tenth anniversary, and since this is my wife’s spring break from work, we didn’t have much choice on our travel dates. I feel a little guilty about missing my home-state race—especially since I want to see if the fresh, positive vibe I noticed in Daytona is still in place—but I’m also looking at it on the bright side: I’ve got two more chances!


When your country produces crowds like this one at Valencia, it’s easier to justify it getting four MotoGP rounds in a single season.
