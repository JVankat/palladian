Bertfried Fauser, Computer Science.

Title: "Logical aspects of C*-algebras and Gelfand duality"

Abstract: Compact Hausdorff spaces X can be studied by continuous complex-valued functions X -> C, forming commutative C*-algebras, that is by Gelfand duality. Banaschewski and Mulvey have given a constructive version of this (technically: valid in the intutionistic internal
mathematics of any topos), and the virtue of their result is that it can work fibrewise. By considering sheaves over the base space, it gives information about bundles that are fibrewise compact Hausdorff. Various groups of workers, starting with Isham's at Imperial and Landsman's in Nijmegen, have now started to examine such topos results in contexts where the fibres of the bundle are spectra from a quantum mechanical system. Our own EPSRC-funded project aims to apply logical techniques from the so-called "geometric" logic to make the fibrewise nature more manifest, and help to conceal heavy topos machinery.

Tomas Johansson, Mathematics.

Title: "Inverse Problems in Heat Transfer and Scattering"

 

Abstract: Inverse problems typically arise when solving for parameters in known models or when data is partly missing. Often, these are ill-posed in the sense that measurement errors in the data can cause arbitrarily large errors in the solution. Thus, special methods (regularizing methods) need to be used to reconstruct the physical quantities in a stable way. We shall discuss some inverse problems in heat transfer and scattering, such as finding the boundary temperature and reconstruction of acoustic obstacles, and methods for the stable solution to these.

Kreft Group, Systems Biology.

Title: "Modelling interactions of micro-organisms from simple to complex systems"

 

Abstract: My group (Chinmay Kanchi, Sónia Martins, Susanne Schmidt) is interested in interactions of organism – mainly simple brainless single cells but also their predators or multicellular hosts (us). Interactions always includes competition for resources, and often social behaviour such as cooperation and communication, as well as the transfer of genetic information in the form of partially autonomous, partially parasitic genetic elements from one cell to another (plasmids). Comparing spatially uniform systems (chemostats) with spatially structured systems (biofilms) we often find that spatial structure in the population or environmental gradients changes the outcome of the interactions qualitatively. As methods we use bottom-up models that describe the parts and their interactions to predict emergent behaviour on the next higher level of organisation – the population. A mechanistic description of interactions based on experimental knowledge is preferred as it allows proper prediction of system behaviour in new environments/conditions – not fitting – so the model can be tested against new data in a different setting. The organisms are discrete (spherical particles), autonomous agents in continuous space while the environment is modelled as a continuum (PDE for convection/diffusion/reaction). For the spatially uniform reference case we use a system of ODE’s. A variety of problems arises in our work where analytical solutions would be beneficial and we also have optimisation, machine learning, and distributed simulation challenges.

Bhima van der Molen, Systems Biology.

Title: "When is An Amino Acid Residue Buried? Why Should We Care?"

Abstract: Determining `when an amino acid is buried' is important to our understanding of protein function, protein-protein interactions and protein evolution. This may appear a trivial question, as solvent accessibility has been discussed in literature for almost 40 years. However there is no universally accepted definition or value of solvent accessibility that is used to decide if a residue is buried in the molecular structure of a protein, or exposed to solvent. To quantify this, a better statistical and physical understanding of this property is needed. Here we developed a method to analyse the amino acid distribution in individual proteins and their preferred solvent accessibilities. This is in contrast to the more common method of performing a statistical analysis of the solvent accessibilities of all amino acids in an entire data-set of proteins. We show that each amino acid residue has a statistical preference for a range of solvent-accessibilities in a sample of approximately 25,000 structures from the Protein Data Bank. Our data gives information about how exposed different residue types should ordinarily be, which is important for a statistical analysis of the role amino acids play in molecular recognition and in the design of optimal protein structures.

Chienmin Chuang, Mathematics.

Title: "Multi-asset Option Pricing Problem: Both European and American Types."

Abstract: European options restrict owners to exercise their rights on expiry date while those of American type allow owners exercise rights flexibly prior to expiry.  Countless algorithms have been developed to price options linked to one and two assets. However, pricing multi-asset option is still a challenging problem in both academy and industry. The most popular way to pricing is to start with the celebrated Black-Scholes Partial Differential Equation (PDE), which turns to a high-dimensional PDE in a multi-asset case and brings the difficult called ‘curse of dimension’. In the case of American options, the Black-Scholes theory produces a high-dimensional Partial Differential Inequality (PDI) instead of a PDE. This makes situation more complicated. Our algorithm is to employ Finite Element Technique to discretize the problem to turn the PDE and PDI to Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) and Ordinary Differential Inequality (ODI). Then we can use one-step or multi-step methods to solve the PDE and optimization method to solve a Linear Complementarity Problem (LCP). The latter problem is formed by ODI along with another complementarity condition which arises from financial properties. From computational point of view, this is also an interesting problem due to the computational cost and efficiency of algorithms.

James Whitacre, Computer Science.

Title: "Evolutionary mechanics: basic principles for the emergence of flexibility in a dynamic and uncertain world."

Abstract: In this brief talk, I will outline a basic set of concepts that are often overlooked in both our understanding of biological systems and the application of biological principles but yet are potentially fundamental to the relationships between robustness, complexity, and evolvability throughout biology.  In particular, I will outline arguments that functional plasticity (proteins/agents/devices that can perform several distinct functions but whose function changes depending on its context, i.e. one-to-many mapping), functional redundancy (structurally distinct proteins/agents/devices that at times perform similar functions and are thus compensatory in certain contexts, i.e. many-to-one mapping), and the architectural relationships between these two properties are often primary determinants of a system’s propensity to maintain/conserve core functions while also evolving new ones. 

Josh Rappoport, Biosciences.

Title: "Fluorescence Microscopy Data Analysis"

 

Abstract: Ranging from seemingly simple questions of molecular localisation and colocalisation to complex measurements of the spatial-temporal properties of biological processes, analysis of fluorescence microscopy data is becoming ever more important and complicated.  Quantitative image analysis must be applied rigorously to provide the statistical testing implicit in biological experimentation.  Although many software packages exist which facilitate certain operations often these are not sufficient for more involved investigations, particularly with the analysis of multi-spectral data from time-lapse live-cell imaging studies.  Specific applications which require bespoke solutions include registration of images acquired from different modes of microscopy, object identification, and object tracking.  The recent commercial dissemination of powerful cutting-edge imaging systems has generated a void between the ability to acquire rich data sets and the capacity for robust and appropriate processing, analysis, quantification, and statistical analysis.
