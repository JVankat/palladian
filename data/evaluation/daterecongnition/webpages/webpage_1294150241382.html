Macro Niche Software Inc.

Lead apron tracking software

10.13.08
Email Print


(Above) This inspection indicator allows managers, from approximately 10 feet away, to identify that the lead apron employees are wearing has recently been inspected. (Below) Aprons, with indicators, as seen under a fluoroscope inspection. (Macro Niche Software Inc.)

Houston-based Macro Niche Software Inc. has recently launched ApronCheck.com, a complete lead apron compliance methodology. ApronCheck not only has a powerful lead apron tracking software program that produces 18 different reports, it also has two different physical devices to choose from that attach to the apron to facilitate identification and tracking. 

In addition to the identification device, there is also an inspection indicator that allows managers, from approximately 10 feet away, to identify that the lead apron employees are wearing has recently been inspected.

“What people used to do for inspection was have a log sheet, in which they would take each apron and mark off its inspections year by year. This process would take managers hours to complete,” says ApronCheck creator, Mike Ruthemeyer, RT(R)(M)(QM).

“One of the things this software accomplishes is that managers just put in the ID number for that apron and the software gives you the complete life history of that apron – when you were able to find it, when it was lost, when it was inspected. The software also tracks aprons that have gone out to be repaired. When these repaired aprons go to inspection the next time, managers know they have to trash it – there’s no need to even inspect it.”

In addition to helping facilities meet inspection and tracking regulatory compliance, ApronCheck also offers a Web-based self-learning program on lead apron awareness that facilities can host on their own intranet and make available to their staff. 

The Web-based learning program also has an online testing component for staff to take after completing the short awareness course. This way, the staff members can print a completion document for placement in their employee file.

“We have a convenient Web-based course that we allow the hospitals to install on their own server,” says Ruthemeyer. “It’s about a 10- to 12-minute course which teaches the basics, and has a written test at the end, which the software grades. It then prints out a little certificate about lead apron awareness, which can then be filed in the employee’s jacket.”

With the Lead Apron Awareness course, directors can provide education to not only radiology staff, but also non-radiology staff, such as operating room nurses, to ensure they have appropriate training on how to use radiation protection devices.

“ApronCheck is designed mainly to make monitoring of aprons and documentation of radiation apparel efficient and cost-effective,” says Mark A. Struthers, BBA, BSRT(R)(MR), CIIP. “At this point in time, aprons are not considered to be a huge commodity to the department, even though they cost about $500 each.”

“If you have 3,000 aprons, you have about a million-and-a-half-dollar investment right there, yet people are just throwing them on the floor. Those aprons have to be checked every year, and once they are all put into the database, it becomes completely visual, and the radiology managers only have to deal with the aprons that are ‘red flags’ in terms of inspections.”

One of the greatest benefits with the ApronCheck program is its extensive online policy and procedure bank, which allows facilities to copy existing proven policies and procedures and make minor modifications for use in their own facility, saving numerous working hours. Also available on the site is an extensive list of links to articles regarding lead aprons.

“There is a serious lack of awareness of how much capital investment there is in aprons throughout the enterprise,” says Struthers.

“But also, now that aprons are enterprisewide – in cardiology, CT, endoscopy, radiology – the person in quality control who is in charge of the aprons really needs a standardized method of documenting where the aprons are, how they’re being used, and where replacements are needed.”
