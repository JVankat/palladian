Your Greatest Weakness is Your Strength

I’m excited to share another guest post with you today.  And it’s on a subject I’ve never covered.  It’s on that job interview question that so many interviewers still ask.

The one about a failing that you and I apparently have.  Or two.  Anyway, I really like the process Brent walks us through to define an answer to this common job interview question.

Hiss site (see links at the end of his post) offers great tips for the job interview.  And he’s made available a very complete interview prep padfolio there as well.

Enjoy this post and I will be back tomorrow!

From here on, you are listening to Brent . . .

BEEN THERE TOO

As I sat nervously on the other side of the table, I dreaded the question would come. It would expose that tragic flaw that would seal my candidacy into the circular file.

So what do you feel is your greatest weakness?

I stumbled for an answer… “Sometimes, I work too much.”  Perfect. That would work.

I was fresh out of college. It didn’t work. 16 years later, it still doesn’t work.

The question “So what do you feel is your greatest weakness?” is as common as “So tell me a little bit about yourself.” However, it is one of the most misunderstood questions in job interviews.

And I trust you’ve heard it before as a candidate.

WHAT IS THE REAL REASON IT IS ASKED?

The purpose for the job interview question isn’t to expose your tragic flaw.  The purpose of the question is twofold:

1.     To see if you recognize your own limitations.

2.     To see what you’ve done about it.

WHAT IS A GREAT WEAKNESS?

First, recognize what becomes a great weakness.  If you lack certain skills, it isn’t an innate failing. It is a lack of training and experience that can be rectified.

It is actually one of your strengths taken to the extreme. Think about it.

When you use your strengths or core competencies, you became blinded by your own heroics. You may be someone who is an expectional editor, but have you ever found yourself perpetually making changes – knowing (or feeling) that the document can still get better?

Do you know someone who is very comfortable speaking in front of a crowd, but fails to deliver a message in a succinct manner? Instead, he just keeps talking to make a point. Or to make (too) many points.

SO HOW DO YOU ANSWER IT?

The response to this job interview question can be handled in 3 easy to remember parts:

1.     Recognize that any strength taken to the extreme becomes a person’s weakness

2.     Identify the strength (cool note: you’ve just turned the job interview question around to highlight something positive the interviewer needs to know about you)

3.     Explain what you have done to adjust (again the real reason the question is asked)

SAMPLE RESPONSE

Here is an actual response I have used in my career:

Interviewer

So Brent, what do you view as your biggest weakness?

My response (word for word)

Thank you for asking.

I recognize that any strength taken to the extreme becomes a person’s weakness.

For example, I am very adept at researching information to solve problems.  I know from experience that there is an unlimited amount of information available and if I did nothing but research, I would never solve the problem.

Therefore, I set a time limit on how much research I will do based on the magnitude of the problem. Once I have hit that time limit whether it is two hours or two days, I know it is now time to propose a solution to the problem.

RESPONSE BREAKDOWN

Notice in the response above what just happened:

1.     I recognized that any strength taken to the extreme can become a weak area

2.     I highlighted one of my key strengths (on a question about a weak area no less

3.     I demonstrated that I can spot my own weak areas and most importantly, I can appropriately manage them.

The response was also short and to the point.  The interview moves on.

So remember.. Your Greatest Weakness is Your Strength. And you’ll do great.

  • Hung Lee

    Apologies for being a little off tangent, but I’ve been waiting for ages for someone to say this:

    “Recognize that any strength taken to the extreme becomes a person’s weakness”

    Exactly right.

    If you want to know what your weaknesses are, just look at what you perceive to be strengths and amp it up a little too much. Before you know it, your ‘ability to see both sides of the argument’ becomes ‘failure to take a position’ or your ability to ‘take charge of a situation’ becomes a ‘me first, me only’ ego trip. It’s an interesting exercise in self awareness to think about strengths & weaknesses this way.

    Cool thing is, it works the other way round just as well. You can convert a weakness into a strength by simply toning down what it is you do; it’s not easy because it’s probably a habit, but the process is simple.

    This is a great post for negotiating the interview process, but it’s even more significant because it introduces important techniques on self improvement.

    Carry on!

  • Anonymous

    Your premise is correct .. the point of the weakness question is to find our about self-awareness and willingness to adjust.

    However, your answer to the question does the ONE THING that is most taboo in interviewing. It fails to answer a direct, specific question! The interviewer asked for a weakness. You gave him a strength, along with a “wink, wink, I’m too clever to actually state a weakness”. Oh no .. I can’t let the interviewer know that I realize I am not perfect.

    I think the most effective answer is prepared, but is also direct and actually answers the question while addressing the underlying issues.

    My answer:
    “I am easily distracted, and if I am not careful can get off task quickly. I mitigate that by reducing the distractions around me, especially when I am on deadline, and scheduling my critical work for times when distractions are minimized. I regularly consult my task and schedule mangement system, using it to bring myself back on-track when I wander. The result is that distractions do not have a big impact, I am rarely late on deadlines, and am usually home for dinner.”

  • Anonymous

    Every interview question is a “trap of sorts.” That is a cynical view, but a reasonable one. Every interview answer is evaluated not just on the actual answer but on what the answer reveals about the candidate.

    I like the weakness question, and I don’t particularly care about the specific answer.

    The weakness question allows the interviewer to see if the candidate will candidly and openly discuss something personally uncomfortable. Those who are able to do so are generally able to accept and process correcting feedback easily and without drama.

    The weakness question also allows the interviewer to see if the candidate will candidly and directly answer a question when the answer does not reflect well on them.

    Brent’s answer indicates that he will avoid an uncomfortable conversation, and will evade a question when the answer to the question does not reflect well on him. I highly value candor, so neither of those traits are particularly desirable.
