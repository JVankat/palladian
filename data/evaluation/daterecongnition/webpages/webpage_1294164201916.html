Clerk of the works
Hi ya'll:

Just studying and came across this term- Clerk of the works.

I understand it to be an Owner representative/on-site supervisor that oversees the quality of work on a construction site. Their main duty is to ensure work is carried out according to Owner’s specs, standards, and schedule.

• Verifies conformance to contract documents
• Witnesses testing of materials and systems
• Maintains orderly records of construction activities
• Coordinates training and distribution of O&M manuals
• Review invoices, change orders and schedule
• Provide regular reports to the Owner, focuses on resolving issues
• Keeps Owner abreast of project issues so that informed decisions can be made

It seems as though this individuals tasks overlap some of the same things that an Architect's duties may cover, as well as some of the same things a CM or a site super might do. Just trying to understand a circumstance where an Owner would employ such an individual. Also do I have the definition all correct? Are there any other tasks this person does? I would think that this person would be a pain in the side of the contractor and maybe even the Architect as well.

Clerk of the Works Is another way of saying Owner's Representative.
They can be a representative from the owner's base organization or a third party hired entity. Understanding that sometimes the Owner is not 1 person and may be an organization (with a board of directors), the Clerk's role is all of the things that you defined, but from a general project over-site point of view and particularly the last two (provide regular reports..., keeps owner abreast of issues...)
They answer directly to the owner and have limited authority to manage the overall project but their role is much different than that of a Architect or a CM.
Architects are professionals and have specific responsibilities under the O/A contract.
Those we are aware of. (example Arch. prepares the CO and presents it to the owner) Clerk could review it "for the owner" and sign off on it.
A CM has construction related responsibilities under the O/CM contract that are clear with regards to management of the daily operations, means, methods, etc. Clerk would be included in the discussion and aware of the operations but would have no direct say with regards to the actual implementation. Certainly not a the level of a site supervisor.
Can they make Suggestions?, yes to a certain degree.
Can they convey Owner's directives?, yes, within the context of the contract limitations.
Can they Direct workers?, no.
Can they design buildings?, some may think they can but no.

Maybe one way to define it would be.
Architect has a Project Architect to manage their responsibilities to the project.
CM has a Project Manager to manage their responsibilities to the project.
Owner could have a Clerk to manage their responsibilities to the project.
All of them have to work together to execute the project as a whole.

There is a rare situation where the Owner's Clerk could actually be a third party CM providing over-site and management of a GC and providing the buffer to the owner. Then they have some more involvement with the day to day operations. This would require modification of the normal AIA documents, but that is certainly a situation more involved than the test would require an understanding of.

Well, upon some further investigation I understand that the clerk is just that, little more than a desk clerk. They are eyes and ears on the job site for the Owner and simply keep records on & report back to the Owner what is happening day to day and what issues may happen to arise. They often have construction experience but don't get paid as well as CM's and as you said they do not have much actual legal decision making power.
