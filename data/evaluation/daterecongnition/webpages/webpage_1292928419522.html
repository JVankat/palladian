What makes Apple so sticky

Posted by Philip Elmer-DeWitt
May 29, 2010 3:55 PM

There's more to customer loyalty than the love of a brand

Photo: Joeys Fun Foods

"Stickyness" is a term of art usually applied to websites like Facebook that keep visitors coming back for more.

But it can also be applied to a software platform, and in a note to clients issued Friday Deutsche Bank's Chris Whitmore takes a crack at measuring the stickiness of the iPhone-iPod-iPad platform.

What he's done is analyze the size of the iPhone, iPod and iPad installed base and the investment Apple's (AAPL) customers have made in content for those devices in terms of apps, videos and music purchased at the iTunes Store.

The result is a chart that shows two things: First, an Apple installed base today of about 150 million iTunes-dependent devices (iPhones, iPods, iPads) that could grow to more than 200 million by the end of 2011. And second -- using Apple's "Other music related products and services" revenue line to estimate sales of music, apps and videos -- a cumulative investment in those devices that stands at about $15 billion today and which Whitmore sees growing to $25 billion by the end of next year.

"This averages to ~$100 of content for each installed device," he writes, "suggesting switching costs are relatively high (not to mention the time required to port). When Apple's best in class user experience is combined with these growing switching costs, the resulting customer loyalty is unparalleled."

In light of what he calls "the stunning success of the iPad" and the demand for upgrades that the new iPhone is likely to spur, it's going to get harder, not easier, for users to switch, no matter what Google (GOOG) and the rest of Apple's competitors have up their sleeve.

Posted By Asher Pat, London: May 31, 2010 1:00 AM

Over the past six fiscal quarters Apple has averaged gross margins of 40.5%. Contributing to these extraordinary margins relative to the industry and to the consistency of these high margins are both "beyond the box" services such as MobileMe and AppleCare and post-purchase monetization activities such as music, movie and app sales.

In the March quarter the operating segment inclusive of iTunes grew revenue by 27% and represented just under 10% of the $13.5 billion in reported revenue. Clearly this growing revenue segment is as important to Apple as the content being sold is important to customers.

There's a direct relationship between content availability and hardware device sales. I remember being at a conference for technology educators back in May 2002 at which Apple representatives were pitching the original iPod not as a digital music player but as a portable FireWire drive for moving multimedia and other curriculum content from classroom to classroom. I don't recall music being specifically mentioned in the presentation. The explosive growth in iPod sales correlates with the opening of the iTunes music store in spring 2003. One may find evidence of a similar catalyst for hardware device sales following the release of iPhone OS 2.0 and the opening of the iTunes app store. It's no wonder sales of the Apple iPad, which can leverage all of content in the various iTunes stores, are off to a record-breaking start.

I've posted in other comments on Apple 2.0 columns and on my blog that Apple doesn't sell products. Apple crafts customer relationships and those relationships sell Apple products. One should look to the value of the customer relationship to understand Apple's extraordinary success. Simply tallying the number of products sold will lead to erroneous estimates of Apple's growth potential.

The crafting of new customer relationships and the expansion of existing customer relationships will vault Apple's performance this fiscal year to a 50% increase in revenue and a 70% gain in earnings per share. Apple's network of retail stores is an effective means to craft new customer relationships and introduce Apple products to millions of new customers. It's the iTunes stores that continue the "conversation" between Apple and its customers and creates the post-purchase monetization opportunities that have now become a material component of Apple's revenue and earnings performance.
